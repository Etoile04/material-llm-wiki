## a b s t r a c t

At high burnup, U–Mo fuel exhibits some form of recrystallization, by which fuel grains are subdivided. The effect of grain subdivision is to effectively enhance fission gas bubble (FGB) swelling due to increased grain boundaries. Inter-granular FGB swelling, i.e., FGB formation and growth at the grain boundaries, is much larger than the intra-granular FGB swelling. Recrystallized fuel volume fractions of U–Mo fuels irradiated to fission densities reaching 5.7 - 1021 f/cm3 were measured. Analytical expressions of recrystallization kinetics of U–Mo fuel during irradiation have been developed through the usage of the Avrami equation, a phenomenological equation which is also used to describe similar typical transformation reactions, such as new phase formation. In this work, we present a novel FGB swelling model of U–Mo fuel that is expressed in terms of Mo content, extent of cold work (fuel powder fabrication method), and fission density.

## 1. Introduction

U–Mo alloy is being developed to convert research and test reactors from HEU to LEU primarily because of its high uranium density. Extensive irradiation tests have been conducted, and this fuel has been shown to be capable of achieving very high burnup due to its characteristic stable swelling behavior and relatively low swelling rate at typical research reactor operating temperatures (<250 C) [1].

U–Mo fuel swelling can be conveniently divided into two parts: one that is induced by solid fission products, and the other by fission-gas-bubbles. The solid fission product induced swelling is caused by volume difference between a split uranium atom by fission and the produced solid fission products (including liquid fission products) that reside in the U–Mo lattice. Hence, it is proportional only to burnup (or fission density). On the other hand, the fission-gas-bubble swelling is dependent upon factors such as fuel composition, fuel type, fabrication process, fission rate. The effect of temperature on fission-gas-bubble (FGB) swelling is considered negligible at the typical temperature ranges where research and test reactors are operated (<250 C) [1].

In the early stage of the irradiation, below 3 - 1021 f/cm3 , the only FGBs visible by SEM are inter-granular bubbles on the original grain boundaries in the fuel. The fraction of generated fission gas that resides in these bubbles amounts to a mere 5–10%. Much of this fission gas has been shown by TEM [2,3] to form an ordered three-dimensional structure of precipitates on the order of nanometers: a so-called bubble lattice. The minimum size of the SEM visible bubbles is 0.1 lm [1]. However, the size of the TEM visible intra-granular bubbles is 2 nm in a super lattice with a spacing of 6–7 nm at 2.4 - 1027 f/m3 [2], which increases the fuel volume by only 1.3%. At higher burnup 4.5 - 1027 f/m3 , the intra-granular bubble size increases to 3.5 nm with a super lattice constant of 11.5 nm, which increases the fuel volume by 5.9%. In the present work, this volume fraction is considered a part of the solid fission product swelling.

At approximately 3 - 1021 f/cm3 a drastic change in the fuel microstructure begins to occur. This fission induced phenomenon has been found in other crystalline fuels and has been called grain refinement, grain subdivision or recrystallization [4–6], or in the case of LWR power reactor fuel, rim effect. Fuel recrystallization during irradiation was reported for UO2 in early fuel development era [4], and more recently from the rim region of LWR UO2 pellets [7–11]. The analogous microstructures between recrystallized UO2 and U–Mo were shown in a previous publication [12]. During irradiation, annihilation or dynamic recovery of point and line defects occurs to reduce free energy simultaneously with the production of these defects. Grain subdivision occurs in recovery through the mechanisms of polygonization, cell formation, and subgrain formation [13]. In case of U–Mo fuel, it has been observed that new small grains are nucleated at prior grain boundaries [14].

The result of the subdivision of the original several micron-size grains into many submicron-size grains is a vast increase in grain boundary area on which SEM-visible FGBs can form. During this process, it is also highly likely that the intra-granular bubbles will collapse and coalesce into the inter-granular bubbles. As a result, the fuel swelling rate increases. In addition, because inter-granular FGB swelling is much larger than the intra-granular FGB swelling, recrystallization enhances FGB swelling.

If recrystallization kinetics is known, the FGB swelling rate can be correlated with the fuel volume fraction that has undergone recrystallization. In order to derive an expression for the FGB swelling rate, quantifying and formulating the recrystallization kinetics of U–Mo fuel is necessary. Analytical modeling of recrystallization of U–Mo fuel during irradiation was the subject of several studies [14–17]. These studies are based on mechanistic modeling, whereas the present study pursues a phenomenological modeling course that enables easier use.

U–Mo fuel recrystallization kinetics was quantified by measuring the recrystallized volume fractions using SEM images taken during post-irradiation examinations (PIEs) of the irradiation campaigns of tests RERTR-1 through -5.

The recrystallization process is similar to typical phase transformation reactions and, therefore, may be described by a phenomenological equation such as the Avrami equation [18]. The FGB swelling can be predicted by integration of the FGB swelling rate, correlated with the Avrami equation, with respect to fission density. The validation of the model was made by comparing the predicted fission gas bubble swelling with the measured data.

## 2. Experimental

## 2.1. Pre-irradiated U–Mo alloy

A scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of as-fabricated atomized U–Mo alloy particle is shown in Fig. 1 [19]. The atomized U–Mo particle powder, produced from the liquid by a spinning disc, called centrifugal atomization, was provided by KAERI, South Korea [20]. The average U–Mo particle size typically used for the tests was 70 lm. Hence, the one shown in Fig. 1 is one of the largest particles. In the back-scattered electron (BSE) image, the grain boundaries appear as dark lines that are of a somewhat lower Mocontent, 17 at.% Mo instead of nominal 22 at.% Mo for U– 10 wt.%Mo alloy, designated U–10Mo hereafter. The grain boundaries were formed as a result of segregation during cooling of the alloy melt. The particle surface region contained columnar grains due to the radial solidification. The average grain size was in the range 3–15 lm [21,22].

## 2.2. Irradiation tests and post-irradiation examinations

The samples were plates containing a fuel meat, consisting of U–Mo alloy powder dispersed in an aluminum matrix, clad with Al 6061 alloy. As mentioned, two types of powders were used: atomized and ground. The plate dimensions were 100 - 25 - 1.4 mm or slightly smaller. The sample plates were fabricated by the standard hot rolling method established at Argonne National Laboratory [24]. The enrichment for all samples was 19.5% U-235. The total time of heat processes involved during plate fabrication was 1.5 h at 500 C, followed by 1 h of blister testing at 485 C.

The samples were irradiated in the advanced test reactor (ATR) for the test campaigns of RERTR-1, -2, -3, -4 and -5. Some of the samples were analyzed by SEM. To prepare a SEM sample, a small section of the irradiated plate, at the plate center where the external constraint was minimum, was punched out to form a disc with dimensions 1.4 mm H - 1.6 mm D. Then, the disc was fractured at the axial center to expose the fuel meat. Fig. 2 shows a fractured surface obtained by this method. SEM analysis was performed on a relatively flat surface. In some cases, a part of the fracture surface was further polished and etched to compare with the original fracture surface.

## 2.3. Recrystallized volume fraction measurement

The evolution of recrystallization in U–Mo is known to begin along the pre-existing grain boundaries [14,16]. Therefore, a region with multiple grain boundaries indicates that the region has been recrystallized. Because the volume fraction is equal to the area fraction [25], the recrystallized area fractions were measured using the SEM images of the samples (Fig. 3).

The SEM images included in Fig. 3 are those of U–10Mo with different fission densities, showing the evolution of recrystallization as a function of fission density. The observation of these images suggests that recrystallization in U–Mo alloy progresses as follows:

– Pre-recrystallization stage shown in Fig. 3a: FGBs are formed only on pre-existing grain boundaries. Drawing a line between the nearest FGBs reveals the pre-existing grain boundaries. At this point, no additional grain boundaries are found close to the pre-existing one, so no sign of grain-subdivision (or recrystallization) of pre-existing grains has yet occurred.

– Recrystallization stage shown in Fig. 3b and c: in this stage, extra FGBs have propagated into the intra-granular regions starting from the pre-existing grain boundaries, indicating recrystallization has progressed. Some grain sizes are smaller than 1 lm in the recrystallized regions. FGBs are also populated in the recrystallized regions.

– Post-recrystallization stage shown in Fig. 3d: all grains have been subdivided. At a fission density of 5.6 - 1021 f/cm3 -(U– Mo), atomized U–10Mo shows full recrystallization.

Recrystallization appeared to occur more rapidly in ground powders as shown in Fig. 4, in which fully recrystallized fuel is shown. The ground powders were cold worked and therefore contained higher dislocation densities. The higher extent of cold work in ground powders was deduced by a comparison of microhardness data between atomized powders and ground powders [1]. The hardness of the ground powder was much higher than the atomized powder.

## 2.4. FGB swelling measurement

Fuel swelling by the FGBs was quantified by measuring the size and number density of the FGBs using SEM images. Because the resolution limit of our SEM was about 0.1 lm, fuel swelling caused by the FGBs smaller than this limit was excluded from this category and instead considered to be included in fuel swelling by solid fission products.

Most of the FGB swelling data were measured on the SEM images taken from the fracture surfaces of the samples. Because of the uneven surface and pull out grains from the sample surface during fracturing (see Fig. 3c and d), the data tend to be biased towards over-quantification, particularly for higher burnup samples. Sample A005 was SEM analyzed on a polished and etched surface, shown in Fig. 5, to assess the effect of using fracture surfaces for fission-gas-bubble measurements. The FGB swelling measured on the fracture surface was 14% while that of the polished and etched surface was 10.6%. Based on this information, a reduction factor of 5% of the measured swelling per 1 - 1021 f/cm3 was obtained and used to correct the data obtained from fracture surfaces. For A005, 14% – (14%)(0.05)(4.8 - 1021 f/cm3 )/(1 - 1021 f/cm3 ) = 10.6%.

A more detailed description of the quantification method is available in a previous publication [22]. The obtained data are also given in Table 2.

## 3. Modeling of recrystallization kinetics

The recrystallization process is similar to typical phase transformation reactions and, therefore, may be described by a phenomenological equation such as the Avrami equation [18]. Hofman et al. [26] applied the Avrami equation to model irradiation enhancement in creep of fast reactor steel cladding. The effect of irradiation on steel cladding is to enhance precipitation of carbide, by which creep is enhanced. Therefore, the underlying mechanism, a new phase formation, fits well with the application of the Avrami equation.

Customarily – as in Refs. [18,26] – the Avrami equation expresses the evolution of a new phase as a function of time, so a slight modification has to be made to deal with irradiation induced recrystallization. In the modified Avrami equation, the recrystallization kinetics is given as a function of burnup (or fission density). The fission rate, Mo content, extent of cold work, and fission density are also included as model parameters.

In the Avrami model to fit the measured data, two assumptions are made: 100% recrystallization can be achieved at high burnup and the Avrami exponent is constant for the given sample. The slightly smaller exponent in the second term obtained in the present study suggests that the recrystallization rate was decreased at high burnup, whereas the volumetric recrystallization model adopted in Ref. [14] intrinsically uses a fixed recrystallization rate. For better predictions, a variable Avrami exponent is preferable at different burnup, which is not tried in the present study.

As Berman reported [4], UO2 has faster recrystallization kinetics, virtually undergoing full recrystallization at 3.1 - 1021 f/cm3 . As shown in Fig. 6, however, full recrystallization in U–Mo is achieved at 5 - 1021 f/cm3 . The temperature for the Berman’s sample is 480 C and the samples used in the present work have temperatures in the range 60–160 C (Table 1), which means that both cases may have similar homologous temperatures. A possible reason might be due to the difference in fuel density. U–10Mo is 58% denser than UO2. A more plausible reason may be that UO2 is a ceramic in which defects produced by irradiation may be less active for recombination, yielding relatively higher defect fluxes and more interstitial loops to cause faster recrystallization. A similar trend was observed for U3O8 [6].

A notable aspect for the recrystallization of UO2 is that recrystallization progresses one grain at a time [4]. U–Mo also appears to have the same mechanism as hinted in Fig. 3b and c, although the pre-existing grain boundaries are not clearly shown in SEM images of fracture surfaces. A further characterization including polishing and etching would be necessary as a part of PIE to show the as-fabricated (pre-existing) grains. In this recrystallization mechanism, once the whole grain is recrystallized, no further recrystallization occurs in that grain, which means that the FGB swelling rate in the recrystallized grain remains constant, as given by Eq. (5).

In the present modeling, FGB swelling is dependent on recrystallized fuel volume fraction and burnup. The latter factor determines the amount of fission gas generated. The effect of fuel type and Mo content is considered in recrystallization kinetics. Hence, these factors are indirectly correlated in our model.

## 6. Summary and conclusions

Fission gas bubble (FGB) swelling rate of U–Mo is closely related to fuel recrystallization (or grain refinement). The fission-induced recrystallization of fuel enhances FGB swelling after an initial incubation period. In order to characterize swelling kinetics, recrystallization kinetics must first be characterized. Recrystallized fuel volume fractions were measured using samples from RERTR-1, - 2, -3, -4, and -5 irradiation tests that have various Mo content, fission rate, and fission density and fabrication method. The effects of fission density, fission rate, Mo content, and extent of cold-work were parametrically examined and fitted to the Avrami equation, which was then used as the basis of FGB swelling rate expression. The post-irradiation FGB swelling was predicted by integration of the swelling rate with respect to fission density. Comparison between the model predictions and measured data showed consistent results.

The present semi-empirical modeling is useful as a significant improvement on the simple polynomial swelling model published in Ref. [1] because it can predict the effects of the Mo content and U–Mo fabrication method on FGB swelling in U–Mo fuel. In addition to its practical usefulness, the present work advances the understanding of the kinetics of recrystallization in U–Mo and its correlation with FGB swelling.

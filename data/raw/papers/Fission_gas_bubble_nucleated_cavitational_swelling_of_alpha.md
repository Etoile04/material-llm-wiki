Cavitational swelling has been identified as a potential swelling mechanism for the alpha-uranium phase of irradiated U-Pu-Zr metal fuels for the Integral Fast Reactor being developed at Argonne National Laboratory [.2] Cavitational sweling of U-Pu-1OZr at intermediate temperatures displays many features analogous to the sweling caused by void formation in non-fissionable metals during fast neutron or charged particle irradiation. Below $6 6 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , U-Pu-10Zr swells and forms large cavities in a highly distorted microstructure. Cavitational swelling begins after an exposure threshold which decreases with increasing temperature, and is greatly reduced by external restraints.

W. R. McDonnell [3] has proposed a model for cavitational swelling of uranium in which voids similar to those formed in nonfissionable metals by agglomeration of vacancies under irradiation serve as nuclei for large cavity formation. Such voids, formed by precipitation of vacancies from a defect-saturated lattice produced during irradiation, can reach sizes and produce volume changes much greater than normal fission gas bubbles in the intermediate temperature swelling range. At intermediate temperatures in the swelling range, the voids originate heterogeneously, possibly as small gas bubbles, and grow by vacancy addition to the critical size required for rapid volume increases under internal stresses caused by anisotropic distortions within the material. Concurrently formed interstitials condense into dislocation loops and associated structures on the same special planes as at lower temperatures, and produce an anisotropic swelling in which the metal elongates preferentially in the same direction as during anisotropic growth.

As fuel swelling and gas release are interrelated, it is essential that the kinetics of fission gas release from the alpha-uranium zone be consistent with the fuel swelling kinetics,e.g. those of cavitational swelling. Experimentally. [l2l the quantity of gas released shows a nearly linear dependence on burnup once the initial incubation period has passed. The fractional release increases rapidly with burnup and exhibits asymptotic behavior which approaches fractional releases between 70 and 8O%. Given that the alpha-uranium zone occupies about 7O% of the fuel volume, this release behavior could be understood if the fractional gas release from the alpha-uranium zone approached 100%.

The observed microstructure of the predominantly alphauranium zone consists of extensive “tear-like" porosity which can be interpreted as a relatively coarse-like structure of cavities existing along original grain boundaries as well as a finer laminar-type structure of “tears" existing along the original alpha-delta phase boundaries within the grains. The porosity structure is so dense that the probability is very high that a gas atom produced via fission will escape from the fuel (i.e., the fuel regions are thinner than the average distance a gas atom produced via fission travels before it comes to rest).

Thus,during the incubation period gas bubbles are formed in the U-Pu-1OZr fuel and act as potential nuclei for cavities. The bubbles on the grain and phase boundaries tend to grow to larger sizes than those in the bulk material. The reason for this is that the growth of gas bubbles on grain (or phase) boundaries at relatively low temperature $( < 0 . 5 ~ \mathrm { T } _ { \mathfrak { m } } )$ is due to the diffusive flow of gas atoms from the bulk material to the boundary (or to within a short distance of the boundary) where the gas atoms nucleate into bubbles which grow at an accelerated rate as compared to those within the bulk due to the reduced effectiveness of irradiation induced re-solution. Re-solution is thought to have a reduced effect for boundary bubbles (as compared to intragranular bubbles) due to a relatively long recapture distance; the average distance an ejected atom travels is within the steep portion of the vacancy and gas atom concentration gradients and the atom is quickly“sucked" back into the bubble. It is clear from the above discussion that the values of specific materials properties related to the strength of gas atom re-solution from boundary bubbles (e.g., the average distance an ejected atom travels) can have a significant impact on the calculated quantities.

During the incubation period, gas bubbles are being transformed into cavities, and the cavities are undergoing biased driven growth. Gas release does not occur to any great extent during this period due to incomplete interconnection of the porosity. As the incubation period nears completion, the cavities begin to rapidly interconnect providing pathways for the gas to reach open surfaces and vent into the plenum. Subsequently, whatever gas is produced is released directly into the interconnected cavity region leading to a linear dependence of quantity released on burnup. The combination of the above two processes will lead to asymptotic behavior for the fractional gas release.

It is theorized here that the swelling mechanisms at work in the alpha uranium zone can be modeled by single vacancy and single interstitial kinetics with in.tergranular gas bubbles providing the void nuclei, avoiding the use of complicated defect interaction terms required for the calculation of void nucleation. This model for cavitational swelling will allow a calculation of the transition between bias-driven growth (voids) and gas-driven growth (bubbles). In what follows, the above model for cavitational swelling will be put on a more quantitative basis.

Mechanistic models for fission gas bubble behavior based on the FASTGRASS [4.5l and GRASS-SST [6l computer codes form the basis for describing gas bubble kinetics and fission gas release from the alpha-uranium zone. Models for fission gas release from the alpha zone are based on SEM photographs of postirradiation U-Pu-Zr fuel specimens which portray the alpha zone as a laminar structure (microstructure forms at temperature) with a high degree of interconnected porosity. Based on examination of the alpha zone imicrostructure, the thin fuel lamina is approximated by a spherical grain with a 1 μm radius. Gas generated within the fuel lamina quickly diffuses to the grain boundaries and is released if a network of longrange interconnected porosity has been established. The focus of the kinetics of fission gas evolution, as it relates to cavitational swelling. is prior to the formation of a significant amount of interconnected porosity, and is on the development of small intergranular gas bubbles which can act as void nuclei.

Figure 1 shows GRASS-SST calculated intergranular cavity (gas bubble) density as a function of cavity (gas bubble) radius for five values of the fuel burnup at an irradiation temperature of 673 K for 0.290- inch U-19Pu-10Zr fuel elements. Figure 1 shows that as the irradiation proceeds, the distribution of intergranular bubbles becomes broader,and the peak of the distribution shifts to larger bubble sizes. A FASTGRASS calculation corresponds to the movement of the peak of the GRASS-SST calculated distribution (i.e., FASTGRASS calculates the density and radius of an average size bubble). Figure 2 shows the calculated behavior of the peak density and the value of the radius corresponding to the peak density (i.e., from Fig. 1) as a function of fuel burnup. As is shown in Fig. 2, the radius increases in an approximately linear fashion between about O.Ol and O.04 % burnup. Between about 0.O4 and 0.05% burnup, the increase in radius with respect to burnup diminishes. The peak density increases rapidly and reaches an asymptotic value after about O.O3% burnup. From Figs. 1 and 2 it is clear that bubbles arriving on the grain boundaries evolve to larger sizes as the irradiation proceeds. Fuel swelling due to this behavior is limited due to the eventual long range porosity interconnection, shown in Fig. 3,and subsequent gas release. Thus,it appears that if cavitational void swelling is a viable description of the swelling of irradiated alpha-uranium, then gas release from the alpha zone is only weakly dependent on the sweling mechanism. Figure 4 siiows the behavior of the cavity radius at the peak density as a function of burnup for three values of the fuel temperature. As expected, increasing the fuel temperature results in an increase of the average bubble size. The analysis just described shows that gas bubbles form and evolve on the grain boundaries. As such they are potential nuclei for void (bias-driven) growth. What remains is to provide a criterion for bias versus gas-driven growth.

In a material containing several types of point defect sinks, such as dislocations and cavities (in what follows,a cavity refers to either a gas bubble or a void), the sinks have, in general, different capture efficiencies for vacancies and interstitials [Zl. During irradiation there will be an unequal partitioning of point defects to sinks. As interstitial mobilities are generally much larger than vacancy mobilities, this bias results in a larger flux of vacancies to voids and of interstitials to dislocations. As the thermal emission rate of vacancies from cavities goes as the exponential of the inverse of the cavity radius at a given temperature. and for a fixed number of gas atoms in the cavity. there is a critical cavity radius above which the bias-induced net vacancy influx is larger than the net thermal vacancy outflux, and the cavity will grow as a void. Below the critical radius, the cavity will either shrink, or if it contains gas will become stabilized. Similarly,as the thermal emission rate of vacancies also goes as the exponential oi the inverse of the temperature, at a given cavity radius and for a fixed number of gas atoms in the cavity. there is a critical temperature below whicl the bias-induced net vacancy influx is larger than the net thermally emited vacancy outflux. As the temperature is raised, the critical radius increases.

The construction of a mechanistic model for gas-bubble nucleated cavitational swelling of the alpha-uranium zone based on the ideas discussed above has been completed and the full set of constitutive equations which comprise the cavitational swelling model will be presented elsewhere. The physics of the model is based on bias driven growth (by adsorption of excess vacancies produced by irradiation damage) of grain-boundary cavities which initially evolve as fission gas bubbles. Growth of cavities within the bulk material is not predicted to occur due to the strong effect of irradiation-induced gas atom re-solution which precludes intragranular gas bubbles growing to {he critical size required for bias-driven growth. Due to the reduced effect of re-solution on grain boundary bubbles (i.e., the boundaries act as an eficient sink for gas atcms). gas bubbles which nucleate on the boundaries can grow to critical size. depending on the fuel

temperature. The plot shown in Fig. 6 shows preliminary results of scoping calculations made using a stand alone version of this new model. Figure 6 is for calculated fuel swelling of the alpha uranium zone as a function of fuel temperature at about 1 at.% burnup. The calculated swelling is due almost entirely to the bias-driven growth of cavities (gas bubble growth results in swelling values on the order of 1%). The swelling peaks at a fuel temperature of abcut 773 K. Above and below this temperature the cavitational swelling decreases due to vacancy emission from the cavities, and reduced vacancy mobility, respectively. The results shown in Fig. 6 are intended to be qualitative as the actual value of the fuel swelling as well as the position of the peak depend on the choice of various materials properties used in the calculation (e.g.， vacancy migration energy, dislocation bias, effective diffusion length of the alpha-uranium zone lamina, etc.).

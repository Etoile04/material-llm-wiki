## a b s t r a c t

Self-organization of defect superlattices in far-from-equilibrium systems presents a promising way to mitigate swelling concerns in nuclear materials. The gas bubble superlattice (GBS) is a highly ordered, three-dimensional complex defect structure that can retain fission gasses in Uranium-Molybdenum (U-Mo) fuels. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) investigation of monolithic U-10Mo fuel irradiated to 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3 revealed that early-stage ordering preferentially occurs at the grain boundaries (GB) and that the critical bubble size for complete ordering is ∼3 nm. Once formed at the GB, the GBS extends towards the grain interior; however, the spread in distance from the GB varies likely depending on the type and strength of the GB sink. TEM results also showed a possible correlation between the growth and evolution of the intragranular disordered bubbles and large dislocation networks. The fission product distribution in and outside of the GBS was also investigated confirming the presence of xenon in the GBS, as well as other fission products including cesium, barium, lanthanum, and cerium.

## 1. Introduction

Recent discoveries of a highly ordered, self-organized complex defect structure known as the fission GBS mostly consisting of Xe and krypton (Kr) in neutron-irradiated U-Mo fuels have stimulated interest in GBS for fission gas retention [1–5]. To date, U-Mo is the only nuclear fuel with the capacity to readily self-organize and form the GBS during the early stages of neutron irradiation. The GBS can be described as a highly pressurized defect structure that can retain fission gasses in irradiated U-Mo fuels. As such, the GBS is a favorable structure with the potential to store fission products long enough to delay the release of fission gasses that contribute to the development of micron-sized fission gas pores (FGPs). Since the first report of defect superlattices during nitrogen ion irradiation of Mo 1971 [6], gas bubble superlattices have been extensively studied and observed in many other body-centered-cubic (bcc), face-centered-cubic (fcc), and hexagonal-close-packed (hcp) metals and alloys under irradiation [7].

The self-organization behavior of materials to produce complex defect superlattices are of interest to the nuclear energy community. One of the challenges of designing a nuclear reactor is the need to develop microstructures that can accommodate significant gas inventory to prevent the uncontrollable swelling of reactor materials during irradiation. The GBS defect structure presents a potential solution for storing gas inventory not only in nuclear fuel but also in structural materials. Therefore, to develop advanced radiation-resistant nuclear materials, it is paramount to thoroughly understand the formation and evolutionary mechanisms contributing to the ordering and expansion of the GBS. Although the fission GBS has only been observed in U-Mo fuel which is a primary candidate for high-performance research and test reactors that operate <250 °C [8,9], the development of the GBS in U-Mo and other fuel concepts could provide enhanced fuel performance for higher temperature reactor applications [4]. In irradiated U-Mo fuels, the GBS has been reported to develop at low temperatures (<250 °C) and relatively low fission density of 1.41 × 1021 fissions/cm3, with an average bubble size of 2 nm [1,4,8,9]. However, between 3  1021 fissions/cm3 and 4.5 1021 fissions/cm3, the GBS becomes saturated and the microstructure undergoes grain refinement and can no longer retain stable GBS [2]. The bulk of literature studies on GBS characterization has been performed on U-7Mo dispersion fuels with fission densities >3 1021 fissions/cm3 where the GBS superlattice is already fully formed. A recent study investigating the GBS at much lower fission densities in irradiated U-7Mo fuel revealed that the GBS formation can initiate at the grain boundaries (GB) [10].

The GBS formed in metals, typically induced by ion irradiation, is known to mirror the crystal structure of the host material [11]. In the case of neutron-irradiated U-Mo, the GBS forms an fcc structure in the bcc-structured U-Mo matrix. Further, the degree of ordering of the GBS in irradiated U-Mo fuel is superior to the GBS formed from helium (He) ion irradiation [12]. However, current models cannot explain the development of an fcc GBS in a bcc host material where the degree of GBS order is considered the best compared with all the GBS and void superlattice in literature [4]. The mystery of the fission GBS in U-Mo is both perplexing and fascinating because the GBS develops under very aggressive irradiation and dynamic conditions accompanied by Xe precipitation and re-solution in the fuel matrix, as well as significant atomic displacements created largely by fission fragments [4]. The gas retention ability of the GBS is a key attribute that could offer a large inventory capacity for fission gasses, mitigate radiation damage, and delay the breakaway swelling.

## 2. Materials and methods

## 2.1. Irradiation history

Microstructural characterization was performed on two monolithic U-10Mo fuel mini-plates that were irradiated in the Advanced Test Reactor (ATR) as a part of the MP-1 irradiation campaign. The two fuel plates share the same fabrication processes and were irradiated under similar irradiation conditions in the ATR large B positions at a low-power density ( 5–10 kW/cm3). The conditions during the irradiation cycles were modeled in a 10 × 40 mesh of nodes over the fuel zone of each MP-1 mini-plate. MCNP5 was coupled with ORIGEN2 to calculate the neutron flux and subsequent depletion for each node at each transport step over the irradiation cycle. This ultimately resulted in a 10 × 40 grid of endof-life (EOL) fission densities for each mini-plate. ## 2.2. Microscopy

TEM lamellae samples each measuring 20 μm ×10 μm × 100 nm from the two monolithic U-10Mo fuel plates were prepared using the FEI quanta focused ion beam scanning electron microscope (FIB/SEM). Lamellae from each fuel plate were prepared by depositing platinum (Pt) protective layers of 20 μm × 2 μm × 2 μm at 10 kV and 0.2nA near the axial center of the fuel midplane cross-section. The samples were then coarse-trenched using the focused ion beam in-situ lift-out system at 10 kV and 6nA and later mounted on a copper (Cu) grid. Following mounting, the samples were iteratively thinned on both sides at 10 kV and low beam currents with the final polish performed at 0.1nA to minimize Ga beam ion damage. The Titan TEM equipped with electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) was utilized to acquire bright field (BF), high angle annular dark field (HAADF), selected area diffraction (SAD) patterns, STEM energy dispersive spectroscopy (STEM/EDS) maps, as well as EELS maps of the fuel microstructure.

## 3. Results

The TEM samples irradiated at two different fission densities were characterized to investigate the bubble size distribution in the grain interiors, at the GB, and the interface between the ordered GBS and the disordered bubbles in the grain interior. The chemical composition of the GBS structure was also quantitatively assessed using STEM/EDS and EELS to investigate fission product distribution in the GBS.

## 3.1. Intragranular bubbles

STEM-acquired BF and HAADF micrographs and TEM SAD patterns of the U-10Mo microstructures at 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3 are summarized in Figs. 1 and 2, respectively. The grains neighboring the GB, decorated by the FGPs were oriented at the [ 131] and [0–11] zone axes for 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3, respectively. In both cases, the microstructures provided in the 3rd quadrants of Figs. 1 and 2 show disordered bubbles in the grain interiors for the respective samples with a mean size of ∼2.6 nm. Additionally, dislocation line defect interactions with the bubbles were observed within the grain interiors of both samples. The ordered GBS structure has been observed at many different zone axes in high burnup fuel samples, with the best view of the GBS observed near the U-Mo bcc [110] zone [2]. However, even at (110) family of zones at 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3, the intragranular bubbles are not yet ordered. The absence of ordered bubbles within the grain interior is a primary indicator that the GBS is not yet formed.

## 3.2. Bubbles at grain boundaries

Previous post-irradiation characterization of U-Mo fuel showed that there is a driving force for the segregation of fission gas atoms both within the grains and along the GB [2,16]. Although intragranular bubbles were disordered, early-stage bubble ordering was captured near the GB at 1.15  1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30  1021 fissions/cm3. Fig. 3 depicts multiple representations of different GB in 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3 where each row of micrographs depicts the same region with increased magnifications. Some curtaining artifacts were introduced in the sample and can be identified by the light streaks in the BF micrographs; however, the presence of curtaining artifacts did not impede TEM characterization. Because the GBS is not yet completely formed; the microstructure offers insight into the initial self-organization behavior at the GB. During the initial stages of self-organization, it can be seen in the first row of Fig. 3, that the size distribution of the semi-ordered bubbles is not uniform suggesting that there may be a critical size for bubble order to occur. At 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3, the mean bubble size at the GB was measured around 3.5 nm.

Bubble lattice dislocations and possible bubble interstitials (outlined in yellow circles) are also observed in the early stages of GBS self-organization as shown in Fig. 3. Considering the bubble interstitial is near the cut off region of the figure it is also possible that it could represent the beginning or termination of a dislocation in the GBS. Dislocations present in the GBS are observed to contribute to local misorientations and stress within the GBS. On the other hand, larger dislocation line defects in the bulk U-Mo regions are observed to accumulate or appear more concentrated on the side of the directional spread of the GBS from the GB to the grain interior. The GBS spread in distance from the GB to the grain interior does not appear to be uniform for all the GB. In fact, the largest GBS spreads are observed at GB which already possessed the highest degree of GBS ordering. For example, at 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3, the GBS spread reached a maximum of 100 nm while other spreads as low as 30 nm were also observed. This suggests that the rate of GBS formation may be dependent on GB type. Similar behavior of GBS ordering initiating at GB was observed at 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3 and is summarized using BF micrographs provided in Fig. 4.

## 3.3. Bubbles around pores

A closer look at the FGPs reveals bubble ordering around the FGPs, these bubbles around the pores (BAPs) appear larger compared to the ordered bubbles at the GB indicating locally high gas inventory. In this regard, the FGPs can act as strong sinks for the fission gas bubbles enabling gas migration to the FGP boundaries. Fig. 5 provides representative STEM-acquired BF micrographs of the FGPs and BAPs at 1.15 1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30 1021 fissions/cm3. The bubble size distribution of the intragranular disordered bubbles ordered grain boundary bubbles, and the BAPs is summarized in Fig. 11. The size of the BAPs was measured at 5.2 nm and 5.7 nm at 1.15 1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30 1021 fissions/cm3, respectively, indicating a 9% difference in BAP size.

## 3.5. GBS chemical composition

During irradiation, fission creates many products that can lead to the formation of gas bubbles, metallic precipitates, and solutes in the fuel matrix. Xe and krypton (Kr) are among the many fission products that present challenges in nuclear fuel performance. Of these two major inert fission gasses, Xe is of great concern since the ratio of Xe to Kr is nine to one. The production and evolution of fission gasses directly influence fuel performance because Xe has a high neutron absorption cross-section (2.7 × 106 ± 0.1 barns) that can decrease the reactivity of the fuel [17]. Produced during transmutation, insoluble Xe and Kr can precipitate into bubbles in the host lattice. One of the key questions posed by Ref [2] is determining the detailed composition of the ordered superlattice. Previous EDS measurements to confirm Xe-filled bubbles at 4.5 1021 fissions/cm3 were unsuccessful due to a lack of significant signal from the bubbles [2]. Furthermore, the presence of solid fission products in the GBS could not be confirmed or denied [2]. This work conducted STEM/EDS on the GBS at fission densities lower than previously reported in Ref [2]. STEM/EDS map of the GBS in Fig. 7 shows the strongest signals from Xe, Ba, and Cs fission products. Other fission products including Kr, Ce, strontium (Sr), Ru, and zirconium (Zr) are not clearly shown in the STEM/EDS map in Fig. 7.

Further investigation was performed using EELS to investigate the chemical content within the GBS bubbles and the open matrix. Fig. 8 shows the energy loss spectra of the chemical distribution inside the superlattice bubble and outside of the bubble at 1.15  1021 fissions/cm3. The spectra in Fig. 8 confirm the presence of Xe inside the bubble superlattice.

Interestingly other fission products were also embedded in the bubble superlattice, including Cs, Ba, La, and Ce. Since the GBS does not exclusively consist of Xe, EELS maps showing the spatial distribution of the two major signals (Xe and Cs) are given in Fig. 9. Here, the Xe signal appears spatially smaller than the Cs signal in the same location, which may suggest that the Xe may be coated by Cs, possibly in a core-shell arrangement. We recommend further investigation of the spatial distribution and partitioning behavior of the fission products in the GBS and the fuel matrix using a higher-resolution characterization technique. Atom probe tomography (APT) is a state-of-the-art material characterization technique that can produce three-dimensional (3D), high spatial resolution, compositional analysis of elemental and isotopic compositions at the sub-nanometer scale. A correlative TEM and APT study would answer several questions including the dislocation-bubble interaction as well as fission production arrangement and interaction within the GBS and the open matrix.

## 4. Discussion

## 4.1. Early-stage GBS self-organization

It is commonly believed that self-organization can occur because of a collective interaction between system components that drive the system far from equilibrium [18]. Although there are many commonalities in the formation of voids and bubbles, the primary difference lies in their nature. A void is effectively an empty cavity whereas a bubble may contain insoluble gasses [18]. Void superlattices and GBS have primarily been observed in bcc, fcc, and hcp metals and alloys. Similar drivers for self-organization are expected to apply void superlattices and GBS; however, bubble mechanics for GBS formation are more complicated. The complication arises when additional interactions such as dislocation loop punching could occur due to the close spacing of over-pressurized bubbles [18]. Further, the presence of gas interstitials could lower the migration of vacancies and change the recombination rate of interstitials and vacancies.

The GBS in irradiated U-Mo can be observed at several different zone axes, especially at high fission densities ≥3 × 1021 fissions/cm3 but is most clearly resolved near the [110] zone axis [2]. In this study, the irradiated microstructure at 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3 shows that the intragranular bubbles are not yet ordered near the [011] zone; however, evidence of early-stage preferential ordering was confirmed at the GB (see Fig. 10). The initiation of bubble ordering at the GB was first observed by Ref [10] in irradiated U-7Mo dispersion fuels and is consistent with monolithic U-10Mo early-stage bubble ordering behavior in this work. The ordered bubble spread in distance from different GB is not uniform which may suggest that the rate of ordered bubble spread towards the grain interior may have some dependence on GB sink strength.

Fig. 12. (a) BF and (b) HAADF micrographs showing intragranular dislocation networks at 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3. Similarly, (c) BF and (d) HAADF micrographs show intragranular dislocation networks1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3.

In both void superlattices and GBS, the presence of a region of some fixed distance away from GB absent of voids and bubbles has been reported and documented as void-denuded zones [18] and bubble-denuded zones [10], respectively.

These denuded zones are believed to arise from the diffusion of vacancies to the GB that can reduce the vacancy supersaturation near the boundary below the necessary threshold for nucleation.

The denuded zones can thus be dependent on GB sink strength. Studies have shown that GB characteristics can influence bubble nucleation at GB [19]. Particularly, coincident site lattice (CSL) boundaries and their resistance to GB deterioration have been investigated for preferred bubble nucleation in He-implanted copper and stainless steel [19,20]. Other studies have shown that random high-angle GB can exhibit a clear void-denuded zone [18]; however, CSL boundaries have shown no such zone probably due to the low energy and ultimately low sink strength. Ref [10] reported bubble-denuded zones near the GB in neutron-irradiated U-7Mo dispersion fuel; however, no clear bubble-denuded zone was observed in U-10Mo monolithic microstructures investigated in this study. Further studies are necessary to investigate the influence of GB characteristics on the nucleation and growth of fission gas bubbles on the grain boundaries, as well as GBS extension towards the grain interior. The spread of the ordered GBS from the GB appeared to have a directional preference towards large dislocation lines and nodes in the grain interior in Figs. 3 and 4. The presence of large dislocation networks in the microstructure may be suitable nucleation sites since their stress fields are expected to attract inert gas atoms in bcc and fcc metals [21].

## 4.2. GBS characterization

Previous studies have noted that bubble size can be misinterpreted based on the defocus value when imaging in TEM mode [10,22,23]; however, imaging in STEM mode eliminates the concern of bubble size uncertainty as a function of the defocus value. He implantation studies have shown that the rate of bubble nucleation is higher at the GB than within the grains resulting in a larger bubble size at the GB [19,20]. In this work, three different bubble size categories were identified in the U-10Mo fuel microstructures investigated in this study: (1) disordered intragranular bubbles, (2) ordered bubbles formed at GB, and (3) ordered BAPs. Fig. 11 shows a general agreement in the bubble size distribution between 1.15 × 1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3. The overlap in size distributions between disordered intragranular bubbles and the ordered GB bubbles indicates that there is a critical size for ordering to occur at 3 nm. Further bubble size transition from ordered GB to BAPs occurs when bubble size reaches approximately 4.5 nm which may be associated with the critical bubble size for bubble saturation. It is noteworthy that the BAPs at both fission densities have similar size distributions, which suggests that the bubble saturation around the pores may not be dependent on fission density.

On a small scale, vacancy diffusion is the main mechanism responsible for bubble growth; however, in the event of high gas pressure, additional mechanisms such as dislocation loop punching may activate [18]. Dislocation loop punching describes the process by which bubbles grow by pushing out interstitial dislocation loops [24]. Since appears to be some dependence of ordering on bubble size authors hypothesize that dislocation loop punching may be one of the contributors driving intragranular bubble growth. The presence of large dislocation networks in the HAADF and BF micrographs shown in Fig. 12 indicates a potential contribution of dislocation interactions in the bubble growth evolution of GBS from disordered to ordered in U-Mo. Studies have reported that there exists an inert gas density or pressure threshold for dislocation loop punching to activate; however, the required bubble pressure for dislocation loop punch to occur is independent of bubble size for bubbles >2 nm in diameter [25]. It is also of interest to investigate and identify any chemical segregation occurring at the dislocation lines and loops in the microstructure. APT analysis can not only provide better insight into chemical segregation at the dislocation sites but also the bubble-dislocation interactions at low fission densities. The mean bubble sizes measured for the three categories are all >2 nm which could leave room for bubble growth via dislocation loop punching. The interactions between bubbles and dislocations and their contribution to self-organization warrants further investigation.

The bubble spacing, d011, measured from the STEM-acquired BF micrographs were used to calculate the GBS lattice parameters in both U-10Mo samples. The GBS lattice parameter of 1.13  1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3 were very similar, measured at ∼11 nm. For the first time, this work investigated the chemical composition of the bubbles in the GBS and the matrix using STEM/EDS and EELS. Signals from Xe, Cs, Ba, La, and Ce were detected in the GBS although Cs were shown to be present in both the GBS and the matrix. EELS maps of Cs and Xe in Fig. 9 indicate some spatial relationship among the fission products. APT studies are recommended to further understand the nature of the spatial distribution and evolution of the fission products in the GBS.

## 5. Conclusion

This study performed TEM characterization on irradiated U-10Mo monolithic fuel at 1.15 1021 fissions/cm3 and 1.30 1021 fissions/cm3. Three bubble size categories were reported: (1) intragranular disordered bubbles, (2) ordered bubbles at the GB, and (3) ordered but saturated BAPs. The GBS self-organization begins at the GB where the critical bubble size for ordering to occur is 3 nm. It is expected that once the intragranular disordered bubbles achieve a bubble size of 3 nm, they will become ordered. The growth distance of the GBS is not uniform at all GB suggests that GB strength may play a role in the rate of GBS extension towards the grain interior from the GB. Large dislocation networks present in the grains could be contributing to bubble growth via dislocation loop punching, further studies are needed to investigate GBS bubble growth. The presence of Xe and other fission products including Cs, Ba, La, and Ce was confirmed in the GBS.

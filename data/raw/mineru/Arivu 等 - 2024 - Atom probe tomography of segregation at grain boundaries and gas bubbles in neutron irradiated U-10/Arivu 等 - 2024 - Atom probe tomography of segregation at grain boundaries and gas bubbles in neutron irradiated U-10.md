# Atom probe tomography of segregation at grain boundaries and gas bubbles in neutron irradiated U-10 wt% Mo fuel

## A B S T R A C T

During neutron irradiation to fission densities > 5.2 × 1021 fiss/cm3 , Xe agglomerates forming gas bubbles of varying size within the U-Mo fuel matrix. Herein, segregation of fission products to Xe bubbles and grain boundaries (GB) were studied using atom probe tomography (APT). Segregation behavior was found to vary among GBs, small bubbles (<10 nm), and larger bubbles (>10 nm). Solid fission products were enriched at GBs and larger bubbles, but not at small bubbles. A denuded zone was identified adjacent to a > 10 nm Xe gas bubble and a GB.

## 1. Introduction

Low enriched metallic γ [body-centered cubic (BCC)] U- 7–10 wt% Mo nuclear fuel has the desired fuel density and phase stability to replace high enriched uranium (hereafter referred to as HEU with > 20 wt% U-235) fuels in research and test reactors [1–4]. They suffer from volumetric swelling during irradiation due to generation of inert gaseous fission products like Xe and Kr [5,6]. Gan et al. showed, at fission densities > 5.2 × 1021 fiss/cm3 , Xe gas bubbles of size ̴ 2.0–3.6 nm form a face-centered cubic (FCC) superlattice structure within the BCC matrix, along with Xe gas bubbles ranging from hundreds of nanometers to several microns [7–9]. Although segregation of fission products and solutes to large bubbles has been studied, efforts to study segregation to < 10 nm bubbles have been unsuccessful [9]. Studies on GB segregation in annealed U-10 wt% Mo fuels have been conducted using aberration-corrected high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM); however, there are limited data on segregation in neutron irradiated U-Mo fuels [10]. Herein, APT was used to compare the segregation at GBs and Xe gas bubbles (<10 nm and > 10 nm). Each exhibited distinct and unique segregation behavior.

## 2. Experimental

U-10 wt% Mo fuel (hereafter U-Mo) was neutron irradiated at Idaho National Laboratory’s (INL) Advanced Test Reactor to a fission density of 2.68 × 1021 fissions/cm3 at fission rate of 1.55 × 1014 fissions/cm3 - sec and an end of life centerline temperature of 94.2 ◦C. APT tips were prepared using a FEI Helios focused ion beam at Irradiation Materials Characterization Laboratory, INL, and APT was conducted using CAMECA Instruments 4000X HR local electrode atom probe (LEAP) at Microscopy and Characterization Suite, Center for Advanced Energy Studies. APT data collection was executed in laser or voltage mode. APT in laser mode was performed at a temperature of 20 K, pulse energy of 20–50 pJ, pulse rate of 200 KHz, and target detection rate of 0.5–1 %. Voltage mode involved a temperature of 45 K, voltage pulse fraction of 20 %, pulse rate of 200 KHz, and target detection rate of 0.5 %. Reconstruction and analysis of the APT datasets were performed using the integrated visualization and analysis software (IVAS) version 3.8.4 developed by CAMECA Instruments Inc..

Proximity histograms (proxigrams) based on Xe 6.7 at.% isosurface in Fig. 1c-d, represent the chemical composition across the bubble-matrix interface. Uranium average concentration drops from \~ 75 at.% in the matrix to < 10 at.% in the bubble. Mo is enriched at the interface and depleted towards the center of the bubble. Sub-10 nm bubbles don’t show significant enrichment of fission products other than Xe, while GB exhibits segregation of all the fission products except for I and Rb. U is depleted at the GB, while Mo concentration remains approximately the same. This is different from U enrichment and Mo depletion at GBs reported by Devaraj et al. in U-Mo fuel after thermal annealing [10]. Such differences suggest segregation behavior at GBs in this study is dominated by kinetics due to irradiation. At high fission densities, matrix becomes amorphous enabling more rapid diffusion of solid fission products (SFPs) [7]. Notably, a high density of point defects from irradiation also enhances diffusion. Gan et al. showed that intragranular bubbles formed by the collapse of sub-10 nm bubbles, encapsulating SFPs [7,8]. It was suggested that bubbles formed first followed by accumulation of SFP around them, which eventually led to influx. Similarly, in this study, the presence of the denuded zone near the GB is attributed to: sub-10 nm bubbles migrating towards the GB; fission products diffusing towards the GB and accumulating around the bubbles at the GB; finally, fission products infusing into the bubbles at the GB.

A denuded zone around the larger bubble in Tip 1 (Fig. 1) is similar to that around the GB (Fig. 2). Irradiation damaged GB have high concentration of interstitials which annihilate vacancies several atomic layers away from the GB by an interstitial emission mechanism forming a denuded zone [11]. It is propounded that, after coalescence of sub 10- nm bubbles to a larger bubble, point defects accumulate at the bubblematrix interface during irradiation, which annihilate other point defects in the vicinity by recombination forming a denuded zone. The segregation/partitioning behavior observed in large bubbles is consistent with the existing literature [7,8], excluding Sr found dominantly segregated into large bubbles using scanning TEM (STEM) – energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS), was not observed using APT. The relative uncertainty of EDS is 10 % providing only a qualitative estimate of the composition used only for relative comparison [12]. In contrast, APT has a high analytical sensitivity of almost 1 appm [13,14]. APT in laser mode could have caused heating of the sample affecting the accuracy of the compositional measurements. To verify the accuracy of APT results in laser mode, APT was also performed in voltage mode and the segregation/precipitation behavior was found to resemble that. Nevertheless, APT has its limitations. As APT is executed at sub-100 K, precise physical state of the bubbles is unknown, and a difference in the evaporation fields of Xe bubbles and the matrix may cause trajectory aberrations. Therefore, some caution may be needed when embracing the results from this study.

## 4. Conclusions

Segregation of fission products to GBs and Xe bubbles of varying sizes were studied using APT. Sub-10 nm bubbles showed no enrichment of major fission products except for Xe, whereas larger bubbles exhibited segregation of fission products inside and at the periphery. Both bubbles accumulated at GBs and > 10 nm bubbles exhibited a denuded zone around them, as both GBs and bubble-matrix interfaces are effective sinks for irradiation-induced defects. This study of fission product segregation/partitioning in fission gas bubbles can aid in understanding the evolution of these bubbles that affect thermal conductivity and mechanical integrity of the fuel.

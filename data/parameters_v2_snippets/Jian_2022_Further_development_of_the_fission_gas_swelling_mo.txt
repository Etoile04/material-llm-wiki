# Further development of the fission gas swelling model for U-10Mo fuels

![](images/189dd1f15fda7abb140f9638eacecec92687f5d9199eb9c8371c3c2e55cc9b44.jpg)



## a b s t r a c t

The irradiation swelling of U-10Mo fuels is one of the critical factors to affect the in-pile structural integrity and dimensional stability of fuel plates. In this study, a mechanistic model of fission gas swelling for U-10Mo fuels is newly developed with a new evolution model of bubble density, considering the fission gas diffusion behavior and the grain recrystallization. The developed theoretical frame is validated by comparing the predictions in this study with diverse experimental data quantitatively and qualitatively, including the fission gas swelling, total irradiation swelling, the evolution rules of the bubble density and the bubble size, together with the thickness increments of fuel foil in a monolithic U-10Mo/Al fuel plates. The correlation of the irradiation creep with the fission-gas-swelling induced porosity for U-10Mo fuels is firstly considered in the simulation of irradiation-induced thermal-mechanical behaviors in Al-cladded monolithic fuel plates, and a new creep coefficient of dense U-10Mo fuels is identified as 180 10−22 mm3/MPa. The research results indicate that the developed fission gas swelling model has a satisfactory prediction ability, especially for higher burnup cases, and can describe the dominant dependences of external pressure and temperature found in the related experiments. In addition, parametric studies have been carried out to investigate the influences of temperature, external pressure and grain-boundary diffusion enhancement factor on the related fission gas behaviors.

## 1. Introduction

U-10Mo alloy is a promising fuel to replace highly-enriched uranium fuels in research and test reactors, due to its high uranium density, stable γ phase and low neutron absorption cross section [1,2]. The Al-cladded monolithic fuel plates have been proposed and investigated for application in high performance research reactors (HPRRs) [3] of United States, with the zirconium diffusion barrier layer designed between the U-10Mo fuel foil and the aluminum alloy cladding. Their structural integrity and dimensional stability should be ensured for the in-reactor safe operation, which depend on the stable and predictable irradiation swelling of U-10Mo fuels [4].

In addition to the fission solid swelling, the involved fission gas swelling of U-10Mo fuels is one of the critical factors to affect the in-pile performances of fuel plates [4], which has complex mechanisms. During irradiation, the fission gas atoms diffuse to the grain boundaries and lead to the nucleation and growth of large gas bubbles, which results in the macroscale fission gas swelling, accelerated by grain recrystallization process [5–7]. As a result, a porous structure will be formed [8–10] to weaken the thermal-mechanical properties of fuels [11,12], including the thermal conductivity, elastic parameters and fracture strength.





It has been demonstrated that the combination of irradiation swelling and irradiation-assisted creep of U-10Mo fuel foils intensifies the mechanical interactions with the cladding, presented as an increase of the plate thickness [13]. At higher fission densities, cracking of U-10Mo fuel foil near the Zr diffusion barrier layer has been observed in the post irradiation examinations [14], and the intrinsic mechanisms have aroused extensive attention. The failure of fuel foil is deemed to have intense correlations with the fission gas behavior and the irradiation-induced mechanical behavior. Above all, a mechanistic fission gas swelling model for U-10Mo fuels is necessary to be developed, for precise predictions of the deformations of U-10M fuel plates at high burnup and analysis of the fuel failure mechanism. Currently, various fission gas swelling models have been established for U-Mo fuels [1,3,5,15–17], including the physics-based models and phenomenological empirical models. The phenomenological empirical models are obtained with the irradiation experimental results, which are generally described as simple functions of the fission density [1,3]. Kim’s model [1] has been widely used to simulate the irradiation-induced mechanical behaviors in dispersion fuels and monolithic fuels, established with the examined gas bubble information in U-10Mo/Al dispersion fuels. Recently, a new series of empirical models for the total irradiation swelling have been reported [3], given as the up-bound and low-bound ones to envelop plenty of experimental data from U-10Mo monolithic fuel plates over a range of irradiation conditions.





It should be mentioned that the fission gas swelling of nuclear fuels depends on the temperature, external hydrostatic pressure and fission rate [18–20]. The fission gas behavior of U-10Mo fuels in plate-type elements will be influenced by the actual in-service conditions, where non-homogenous distributions of temperature, fission rate and mechanical equilibrium hydrostatic pressure gradients appear [21,22]. Several mechanistic models have been proposed and improved [5,15–17], with important physical mechanisms involved, such as the fission gas atom diffusion within the equivalent spherical grains [23], the grain recrystallization effect [5–7], resolution of fission gas atoms in the intra-granular and inter-granular bubbles [17,24,25]. As far as we know, although the predicted fission gas swelling results could match some experimental data, the predictions of bubble density and bubble radius have been several orders of magnitude lower than the related experimental data [8,25]. Especially, the dominant effect of external hydrostatic pressure cannot be well captured, found in some experiments [19].





In this study, a mechanistic model of fission gas swelling for U-10Mo fuels is further developed, with the inter-granular bubble swelling correlated with the in-grain fission gas diffusion behavior and grain recrystallization, with a new evolution model of bubble density involved. The obtained prediction results match well with diverse experimental data, quantitatively and qualitatively, including the fission gas swelling, total irradiation swelling, the evolution rules of the bubble density and the bubble size, together with the thickness increments of a monolithic fuel foil. The effects of irradiation conditions on the fission gas swelling, the bubble density and size are investigated, and the sensitivities to some critical model parameters are analyzed. It is noted that the correlation of the irradiation creep with the fission-gas-swelling induced porosity is firstly considered in the simulation of irradiation-induced thermalmechanical behaviors in U-10Mo/Al monolithic fuel plates, and a new creep coefficient of dense U-10Mo fuels is identified.





## 2. Model descriptions




As illustrated in Fig. 1, the grains of nuclear fuels are generally regarded as spherical ones. The fission gas atoms will diffuse toward the grain boundary, and they could be trapped by the intragranular and inter-granular bubbles. In the irradiation environments, the fission gas atoms in the bubbles are apt to re-dissolve into the fuel matrix, due to the attack of high-energy fast neutrons and the fission fragments. For the U-10Mo fuels in the dispersion fuel plates or monolithic fuel plates, grain recrystallization is demonstrated to occur, which will trigger the quick diffusion of fission gas atoms to the grain boundary of fine grains. It is noted that the intra-granular bubbles are extremely small, which are difficult to be identified by Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) [7], and their contributions could be considered in the fission solid swelling [1].

In fact, the experimental data of fission gas swelling are obtained with the identified number and size of inter-granular bubbles by image processing of the microstructures of fuel particles in the U-10Mo dispersion fuel plates [7]. Therefore, a hypothesis that the fission gas swelling only originates from the contribution of inter-granular bubbles is adopted in this study. Assuming that the same bubble radius holds for all of the inter-granular bubbles at a certain fission density, one can obtain their magnitudes at different burnup levels through the modified Van Waals gas law by considering the effect of external hydrostatic pressure. Above all, the average number of gas atoms in one bubble should be determined, which depends on the diffusion behavior of the fission gas atoms within the grains. In this study, the critical physical mechanisms are taken into account in our theoretical frame. It should be mentioned that a new bubble density model is proposed to capture the non-monotonic variations as a function of the fission density, discovered in several fuels through post-irradiation experiments. The predicted magnitudes of bubble density are consistent with those of similar U-Mo fuels. The related models will be introduced below.

## 3.1.1. The irradiation swelling results





Fig. 2 (a) displays the predictions of fission gas swelling for the fuel particles in U-10Mo /Al dispersion fuel plates, together with some experimental data in Ref. [7]. The applied irradiation conditions are chosen from Refs. [7,8], which are also marked in Fig. 2 (a). One can find that the predictions of fission gas swelling increase with the fission density, which are in general agreement with the available experimental data in Ref. [7]. Especially, the predicted fission gas swelling curve as a function of the fission density appears in a downwards convex manner, which matches the characteristics of the experimental curves of irradiation swelling in some Refs. [4,36,37].





Fig. 2 (b) shows the predictions of total irradiation swelling for U-10Mo fuel foils in U-10Mo monolithic fuel plates, which are compared with the experimental results of average swelling in RERTR-12 campaign [14]. It should be mentioned that the fuel foil temperatures in the thickness direction are different, with higher magnitudes at the mid-plane of the fuel plate. For non-uniformly irradiated fuel plates, the fuel temperatures across the plate surface also have a great divergence. So, the computational results for the two temperature values of 423 K and 473 K are given for comparison. One can see that the irradiation swelling results at higher burnup will vary with the temperature, and become larger at a higher temperature value. This stems from the enhanced fission gas swelling, as governed by Eq. (13).





It can be seen that the predicted total irradiation swelling results in this study are in good agreement with the experimental data at different burnup levels [14]. The predictions in this study locate in the range of the upper and lower bounds of total irradiation swelling proposed by Robison et.al [3]. At the fission densities lower than 4  1027 fission/m3, the predictions in this study are very close to those of Kim and Hofman [1]. At higher fission densities, the phenomenological empirical model of Kim and Hofman [1] will under-evaluate the fission gas swelling. The developed mechanistic fission gas swelling model is demonstrated to have a satisfactory prediction ability for the high fission density cases. After grain recrystallization, the fission gas atoms in the bubbles will grow in quantity, which will lead to the sensitivities of the fission gas swelling to the temperature and external pressure [15]. If the post-irradiation annealing blister temperature needs to be predicted, or the failure mechanism of fuel foil is to be revealed, it is necessary to adopt a precise mechanistic fission gas swelling model.

## 3.1.2. The results of bubble density, bubble radius, current porosity and bubble pressure




In addition to the ability to predict the fission gas swelling, the developed mechanistic model system can also simultaneously obtain the results of bubble density, bubble radius, current porosity and bubble pressure. The corresponding prediction results of bubble density and bubble radius are shown in Fig. 3. One can find that the predicted curves as a function of fission density are consistent with the evolution rules represented by the experimental data of U-7Mo fuels [8]. The magnitude orders of bubble density and bubble radius are the same to those of U-7Mo fuels [8]. In Ref. [8], it has been mentioned that the evolution law of bubble size for U-10Mo fuels is similar to U-7Mo fuels, and the bubble densities for different fuels all present an evolution trait of decrease after an increase to the maximum value. The comparisons in Fig. 3 further demonstrate the effectiveness of the developed mechanistic model system in this study.





The predicted bubble density before grain recrystallization is hardly changed, with the bubble radius growing continuously to a size of 0.1 um at the beginning of recrystallization, as shown in Fig. 3 (a) and (b). During this stage, the bubbles locate nearby the initial grain boundary. From the microstructure image of atomized U-Mo particles in Ref. [37], the bubble number can be seen to almost un-vary, and the enlargement of the bubble size dominates. After grain recrystallization, the bubble density will increase quickly and the bubble sizes vary slightly. With the spread of the recrystallization zone (in Fig. 4), the bubble density rises to the maximum and then decreases. When the fission density exceeds 5 × 1027 fission/m3, the bubble radius increases linearly with the fission density. Actually, the fission-density dependent factor ηF adopted in this study can be interpreted as the effect of bubble coarsening [8]. It should be mentioned that the model for the bubble density evolutions has been developed in Ref. [29], in which two constants are adopted to modify the obtained grain-boundary bubble concentration. So, the predicted bubble density there could not reflect the decrease of the bubble density after a maximum.





One can see the predicted results of current porosity and bubble pressure in Fig. 4. As shown in Fig. 4 (a), the average porosity is less than 5% before recrystallization. With the increase of fission density, the average porosity increases rapidly, which can reach ∼18% when the recrystallization process is completed. The current porosity curve can be approximately divided into two linear stages. Generally, the macroscale strength of a material decreases as the porosity increases [38,39]. A four-point bend tests for irradiated U-10Mo fuel foils [11] indicated that the bending strengths were much lower than the tensile strengths of un-irradiated U-10Mo fuels [40], even at low fission density levels. The substantial decline in the bending strength may be related to the locally enhanced porosity, in spite of the extremely small average porosity, which needs to be investigated in the future.





In addition, the irradiation embrittlement of the U-10Mo fuel may also degrade the strength of the material, because the failure appears to have a evident trait of brittle fracture [11]. The U-10Mo fuel foil is prone to failure because of high porosity, pore pressure and low strength at high burnup [41]. Fig. 4 (b) exhibits the evolution results of bubble pressure. It is noted that the evolution law of bubble pressure is opposite to the bubble radius. At the initial stage of burnup, the bubble pressure can reach ∼60 MPa, which decreases with the fission density due to the growth of bubble size.

## 3.2. The thickness increment results of the fuel foil in a U-10Mo/Al monolithic fuel plate




The fission gas swelling model is introduced into the finite element analysis of the thermal-mechanical behavior in a U-10Mo monolithic fuel plate. The irradiation-induced creep of U-10Mo fuels is also a critical factor to affect the thickness increments of the fuel foil in the monolithic fuel plates [4,13]. The creep deformations will cause the stress relaxation of the U-10Mo fuel foil, which will affect the hydrostatic-pressure dependent fission gas swelling. The deformation analysis of the U-10Mo monolithic fuel plates has been performed in Refs. [13,21,35], with the same form of U-10Mo creep rate model, expressed as ε˙ cr = Aσ ˙f . However, the porosity dependence of the fuel creep rate is significant [42], especially for the case of high porosity. So, the corresponding predictions of bubble radius and fission gas swelling mainly depend on the gas atoms in the bubbles, which have a positive correlation with the fission density.

In the part from 0 to ∼2 mm, the hydrostatic pressures reach firstly an extremely large value of ∼113 MPa, then decrease quickly to a small magnitude of ∼11 MPa at the location with a distance of 0.10462 mm away from the path origin, then maintain at a pressure level of ∼20 MPa, and then decrease toward zero. So, the fission gas swelling at the path origin reaches a value of ∼7% despite of a very large fission density, then rises fast to ∼32% because of the relatively large fission density and low external pressure there, and then decreases to a local minimum, and subsequently increases to a larger value due to the competition of the fission density and external pressure. One can see that an external pressure of ∼113 MPa can make the fission gas swelling reduce from a value of more than 32% to 7%. This is consistent with the report in Ref. [19] that a fission gas swelling of ∼18% can vanish under the external pressure of 40 MPa. This demonstrates that the currently developed fission gas swelling model can well capture the external-pressure dependence.





In our previous studies [21,29], the predictions of fission gas swelling are not very sensitive to the external pressure, because the pressure Hp in Eq. (14) has a weaker contribution than that from the surface tension, owing to the lowered bubble radius predictions by several orders of magnitude. It should be mentioned that the predicted fission gas swelling results in Ref. [21] differ from those in Fig. 8 (a), in the aspects of the distribution trait and the magnitudes. The predictions of external pressure in Ref. [21] also have some discrepancies from those in this study, with negative values in the middle region there. These differences stem from the adopted fission gas swelling and creep models. In fact, the mechanical fields are also influenced greatly, which will be investigated in the future work.





From Fig. 8, it can be known that the conditions of external hydrostatic pressure in the central region of fuel foil are similar to those of fuel particles in U-10Mo/Al dispersion fuels [45], so the predictions from empirical models in Ref. [1] can agree well with those of fuel foils in the central region of monolithic fuel plates. It is noted that the conditions of external hydrostatic pressure in the other regions have obvious differences. So, in this sense, the general predictions from the empirical models of irradiation swelling are possible to have deviations from reactivity in the monolithic fuels.

## 4. Effects of model parameters and irradiation conditions on fission gas swelling




The irradiation temperatures are usually non-uniform within the fuel plate. The induced local in-homogeneities in the irradiation swelling, thermal expansion and creep deformations will trigger the complex mechanical interactions among the fuel foil or the fuel particles, the metal matrix and the cladding. One can know that the external pressures within the fuels will vary with the locations and fission density. For different fuels, the critical model parameters, such as the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor in Eq. (9) will be different. In order to better understand the fission gas behaviors of U-10Mo fuels or the other fuels, the influences of the irradiation temperatures, the external pressures and the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factors will be analyzed in this section.





## 4.1. Effects of irradiation temperature




Fig. 9 (a) gives the evolution results of fission gas swelling under different irradiation temperatures. It can be seen that the fission gas swelling increases with the irradiation temperature. The absolute differences are gradually enlarged with an increase of fission density. When the temperature rises from 323 to 423 K, the fission gas swelling increases by 37% at the fission density of 8.64 × 1027 fission/m3. Compared to the results in Ref. [15], the effect of temperature before initiation of recrystallization is predicted to be more notable in this study. From Eqs. (13) and (14), one can know that the temperature effects can be really reflected when the actual magnitude order of the bubble radius is taken into account. In the previous studies [5,15], the predictions of fission gas swelling were matched with the experimental data, by using very large values of bubble density and very small values of bubble radius. So, the pore pressure resulting from the surface tension is amplified by several orders of magnitude. Hence, the temperature effect cannot be precisely captured.

It is noted that the bubble density is not affected by temperature, and the bubble radius is governed by the modified Van Waals gas law. So, the increase of fission gas swelling with the irradiation temperature stems from the expansion of bubble size. As shown in Fig. 9(b), the bubble radius seems to have no very dominant variation. With a temperature increase from 323 to 473 K, the bubble radius at the fission density of 8.64 × 1027 fission/m3 has an enlargement of ∼16%. However, the corresponding fission gas swelling has an increase of ∼57%. In general, the fission gas swelling is considered to be un-sensitive to the irradiation temperature at the range lower than 250 °C [7]. From the predictions in this study, it can be recognized that the temperature effect should be well considered. Therefore, it is very important to maintain a good heat transfer performance of the reactor core. Thus, the fission gas swelling and the related porosity can be restrained in the acceptable ranges.





![](images/10b1fa50bb5674cfeb83216d1d53768db0349da523747346dbce9e975ba361da.jpg)  
Fig. 10. Comparison results of fission gas swelling with previous study [29].



The distinct temperature effect of fission gas swelling has not been predicted previously for U-10Mo fuels. As shown in Fig. 10, the predictions of fission gas swelling at two temperature values are obtained with the model established in this study and that in Ref. [29], respectively. It should be mentioned that the model in Ref. [29] is similar as those in Refs. [5,15]. One can find that the temperature dependence of fission gas swelling before recrystallization disappears in the results calculated with the model in Ref. [29]. At the end of the investigated burnup, the deviation of fission gas swelling from 323 to 423 K in this study can reach ∼60%, but only about ∼30% when using the model in Ref. [29]. Simultaneously, distinct differences appear in the, respectively predicted results at higher burnup levels, for the temperature case of 473 K. It is noted that the gas swelling curve predicted with the model in Ref. [29] presents an unsmooth feature at the fission density close to 8 × 1027 fission/m3, after this fission density a linear relation appears. In fact, this appearance is induced by the definition of N there.




## 4.2. Effects of external hydrostatic pressure




The effects of external pressure can be found from Fig. 11. The evolution results of fission gas swelling under four different hydrostatic pressures indicate that the fission gas swelling will be well inhibited even under a low hydrostatic pressure of 10 MPa. At the fission density of 8.64 × 1027 fission/m3, the fission gas swelling has a reduction of ∼46% with the external pressure increasing from 2.5 to 10 MPa. Once the hydrostatic pressure is higher than 100 MPa, the fission gas swelling will be less than 5.1%. As shown in Fig. 12, the reported effect of external pressure in the experimental research [19] has been simulated in this study, which cannot be captured with the previous fission gas swelling model [29]. In Ref. [29], the effect of external hydrostatic pressure is slight before recrystallization, which results from the amplified bubble density. When the hydrostatic pressure increases from 2.5 to 100 MPa, the reduction of fission gas swelling reaches ∼30% using the model in Ref. [29], which is much smaller than the predicted value of ∼85% in this study. From these predictions, one can recognize that the external pressure has a prominent influence on the fission gas swelling. If the U-10Mo fuel particles are dispersed in a matrix with the ability of providing enough constraining forces, the fission gas swelling of fuel particles can be restrained greatly. In the current U-10Mo/Al dispersion fuel plates, considerable fission gas swelling has been examined [7], which is induced by the weakened constraint capability of the matrix, because of its chemical reactions with the fuel particles.

![](images/07ba6f6f4569e0921ce23eb242e41da20420fadfb439eac3df748ab5c01e7618.jpg)  
Fig. 12. Comparison of the fission gas swelling predictions for different hydrostatic pressures using different models.



As mentioned in Section 3.2, the developed models in this study can capture the effect of external pressure, which is attributed to the objective consideration of the magnitude orders of the bubble density and bubble radius. For these reasons, the actual effect cannot be well predicted in Refs. [15,46], although the correlation of external pressure has been considered. From the currently developed theoretical frame in Section 2, one can know that the external pressure does not affect the evolution results of bubble density. Similar to the analysis in Section 4.1, the variations of bubble size under different external pressures will contribute to the change in the fission gas swelling. As depicted in Fig. 11 (b), the bubble size is sensitive to the external pressure in the range from 2.5 to 50 MPa, and then decreases slowly with the further increase of external pressure. At the fission density of 8.64  1027 fission/m3, the bubble radius decreases from ∼0.32 to ∼0.19 um, while the corresponding fission gas swelling has a significant reduction from 33% to 7%. It can be obtained that the bubble size has a great influence on the fission gas swelling.

## 4.3. Effect of grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor and F\_d0




The grain boundary diffusion enhancement factors in Eq. (9) directly relate to the bubble density. It is reported that the density of fission-gas induced bubbles in UO2 fuels is one order of magnitude lower than the one found in U-Mo fuels, and the bubble sizes of the former fuels are much larger [8,28]. It implies that the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factors for UO2 fuels will be larger than those for U-Mo fuels, which are diverse for different fissile materials. A region of Molybdenum-depletion existed in the fuel foil nearby the interface with the cladding, and the fission gas swelling there was enhanced [14,47]. This might result from the varied grain boundary diffusion enhancement factors in different U-Mo fuels. Hence, it is necessary to investigate the effects of grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor on the fission gas behaviors.





As shown in Fig. 13 (a), the fission gas swelling increases with the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factors, with an increase of ∼29% when the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor is enlarged 5 times of 1 107. It should be mentioned that the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor of U-10Mo fuels is re-identified to obtain a new bubble density model according to the experimental data [8], which are different from those in our previous studies. It is noted that the value of 2  107 adopted in this study is much larger than the one used in Refs. [5,15], so that the predicted bubble density can have the same order of magnitude as those of similar U-Mo fuels [8]. It can be found from Fig. 13 (a) that the fission gas swelling will increase with the decrease of grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor. Different from the influence mechanisms of temperatures and external pressures, the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factors will change both the bubble density and the bubble size, as shown in Fig. 13 (b) and (c), which are governed by Eqs. (6), (7) and (9).





It can be found from Fig. 13 (b) that the bubble density decreases with the increase of grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor, while the influences will become weaker and weaker in the investigated range of this study. With the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor enlarged from 1 × 107 to 4 × 107, the maximum bubble density reduces from 7 to 3.5 um−3. When the factor increases from 4 107 to 6 107, the maximum bubble density has a slight reduction of 0.6 um−3. Due to that the total fission gas atoms in all the inter-granular bubbles have no difference for the four cases of grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor, the average fission gas atoms in a bubble is higher for a smaller bubble density. Based on Eq. (13), the corresponding bubble size will become larger, as shown in Fig. 13 (c). With the dominant influences of bubble radius, as analyzed in the previous sections, the fission gas swelling grows with the grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor despite the decrease of the bubble density. Thus, higher grain boundary diffusion enhancement factors for UO2 fuels will lead to enhanced fission gas swelling in the rim regions of fuel pellets [28] or fuel particles in dispersion fuels [48], especially at high temperature conditions. At present, the relationship between grain boundary diffusion enhancement factor and the material composition is not clear. More experimental data will be needed for the further researches. It should be mentioned that the differences of fission gas swelling behaviors in different fuels possibly stem from diverse swelling mechanisms, which deserve deep researches.

It should be mentioned that the values of F in Eq. (8) have some uncertainties, for U-10Mo fuels fabricated with different processing parameters. The effects of this parameter should be investigated. Fig. 14 gives the evolution results of bubble density and fission gas swelling for four values of Fd0. From Fig. 14 (a), it can be found that the evolutions of bubble density are similar with each other before the saturation of bubble density. When F is relatively small, the bubble density will start to reduce earlier. Under the same burnup larger than 4.0  1027 fission/m3, the bubble density decreases with the reduction of Fd0. From Fig. 14 (b), one can find the fission gas swelling has a tendency to rise as F decreases, having an increase of 13% at the fission density of 8.64 × 1027 fission/m3, after a reduction of Fd0 from 5.0 × 1027 fission/m3 to 4.0 1027 fission/m3. It can be obtained that the fission gas swelling is relatively insensitive to the value of Fd0 in the considered range.




It should be mentioned that this value of Fd0 in Eq. (8) has been fitted out to be 4.7 × 1027 fission/m3, in order to make the calculated fission gas swelling results match well with the experimental results for U-10Mo fuel particles. This value is close to the measurement of U-7Mo dispersion fuel particles [8], which demonstrates that the corresponding values of these two fuels are close to each other.





## 5. Conclusions




In this study, a mechanistic model of fission gas swelling for U-10Mo fuels is developed with a new evolution model of bubble density involved, considering the fission gas diffusion behavior and the grain recrystallization. The evolution rules of the fission swelling, bubble density and the bubble size, together with the thickness deformations of fuel foils are obtained, and match well with diverse experimental data, validating the effectiveness of the fission gas swelling model. The effects of temperature, external hydrostatic pressure and grain boundary enhancement factor on the fission gas behaviors are analyzed. The main conclusions are as follows:

(1) The predictions of fission gas swelling and total irradiation swelling for U-10Mo fuels are in good agreement with experimental data, and the evolution traits and magnitude orders of bubble density and bubble size are similar with the postirradiation experimental results of U-7Mo alloy.





(2) The thickness increment predictions of the U-10Mo fuel foil in a U-10Mo/Al monolithic fuel plate are consistent with the measured data, thanks to the new fission gas swelling model and the porosity-dependent irradiation creep model.





(3) Compared to the previous results in the references, the effects of temperature, hydrostatic pressure and grain-boundary enhancement factor on the fission gas swelling are predicted to be more significant, even before the recrystallization initiation.

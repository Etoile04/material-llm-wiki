## A B S T R A C T

The microstructural progression of very high-burnup (>8 × 1021 fissions/cm3 ) monolithic uranium-molybdenum (U-10wt.%Mo) was analyzed, providing crucial insights into the behavior of post-recrystallized nuclear fuel, where scant data exists. Three focused ion beam cuboids sourced from a fuel plate with varying local burnups of 8.86 × 1021, 9.05 × 1021, and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 were characterized. The porosity and composition of the samples were evaluated to characterize the evolution of the microstructure as a function of fission density and different locations on the fuel plate, while simultaneously isolating plate-specific parameters such as Zr diffusion barrier thickness, hot-isostatic-pressing conditions, enrichment, and reactor conditions. The porosity was segmented, and the three-dimensional distribution of the porosity was extrapolated from the two-dimensional segmentation. The composition was assessed and quantified using energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy areal mapping. The porosity fraction increased as a function of the burnup from 27.77±0.51, 35.12±1.54, and 37.71 ±0.44 % for 8.86 × 1021, 9.05 × 1021, and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , respectively. When compared to literature, the porosity volume fraction plateaus at burnups greater than 6 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , while the pore size grows linearly as a function of fission density. The number of large pores increased in number density as a function of burnup, while the smallest pores (<0.3 µm) increased up to 9.05 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , followed by a decrease at 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . The delamination and cracking in the fuel plate propagated through an interconnected porosity sublayer identified \~5 µm from the diffusion barrier. The local swelling of the specimens was within or near the prediction bounds of the Robinson-Williams model for local swelling. The fission products, strontium,

barium, cerium, and cesium, precipitated into the pores, while neodymium accumulated adjacent to the pores. These findings have direct implications for the development of fuel performance codes and the accurate documentation of the microstructure evolution in high burnup U-Mo, thus enhancing the safety and efficiency of nuclear fuel usage.

## 1. Introduction

## 1.1. Uranium-10wt.% molybdenum (U-10wt.%Mo) monolithic plate behavior

Low enrichment (<20 at. % 235U) uranium-molybdenum (U-Mo) alloys are considered a candidate for the conversion of highperformance research and test reactors to a low-enrichment fuel alternative due to the higher U-loading of the metallic alloy allowing for similar in-reactor performance [1–6]. The proposed United States fuel design consists of a monolithic fuel foil of alloyed U-10wt.%Mo [1,3,6]. The addition of Mo allows the metastable γ-U body-centered cubic phase to be quenched in and retained, enabling desired isotropic swelling as compared to the orthorhombic α-U phase [5]. The alloy is cold rolled to the desired thickness of \~250 µm and then co-hot-rolled with a zirconium (Zr) barrier of \~25 µm [3]. The Zr barrier mitigates the Al-fuel interaction region that develops porosity and deteriorates during irradiation, leading to delamination or pillowing [3]. Finally, the fuel, the Zr barrier, and the aluminum (Al) 6061 cladding assembly are hot isostatically pressed (HIPed) before insertion in the reactor to bond the cladding to the fuel [3].

The HIP bonding during the fuel assembly causes an interaction between the U-Mo fuel and the Zr-diffusion barrier. The resulting interaction microstructure is multi-layered, consisting of the Zr diffusion barrier followed by layers of U-Zr2, γ-U stabilized by Zr, a thin δ-Zr layer, and a low-Mo content sublayer with α-U and Mo2Zr precipitates at the interface [7,8]. During irradiation, the multi-layered structure dissipates, with only the U-Zr2 and low-Mo content sublayer visible up to 5.2 × 1021 fission/cm3 . However, the U-Zr2 sublayer at 9.8 × 1021 fissions/cm3 was observed to be discontinuous with breaks caused by Zr penetrating into the fuel [9]. At a high burnup of 9.8 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , the Mo-depleted zone was not reported using scanning electron microscopy-energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (SEM-EDS) [9]; however, it has been recently characterized using synchrotron micro-computed tomography (S-µCT) [10].

The as-fabricated U-Mo fuel microstructure has an average grain size of \~10 µm [11,12]. During irradiation, the fuel swells from the generation of solid and gaseous fission products [13]. The generation of fission gas, primarily xenon (Xe), is of concern since it results in bubbles and gas nucleated porosity, which can increase the swelling rate, lower the mechanical stability of the fuel, and cause cracks and delamination between the fuel and cladding [14–17]. Initially, the fission gas in the U-Mo fuels is contained in a nano-bubble superlattice within the γ-U-Mo phase [18–21]. After reaching a saturation limit, the nano-bubble superlattice collapses, coinciding with irradiation-induced recrystallization of the fuel microstructure, a phenomenon termed irradiation-induced grain refinement [22]. This results in a nano-grained structure with grain sizes of \~200–500 nm with pores that can exceed 500 nm [23–26]. Currently, no systematic studies have investigated the progression of irradiation-induced recrystallization in monolithic U-10wt.%Mo. However, a systematic study of a similar fuel in dispersion form consisting of U-7wt.%Mo, has indicated that irradiation-induced recrystallization progresses between \~3–6 × 1021 fissions/cm3 [22,27,28], which is consistent with the completion of monolithic fuel recrystallization measurements [29,30]. The increase in the grain boundary area and a reduction of the diffusion path length for point defects are postulated to cause an increase in porosity nucleation and growth resulting from both fission gas and vacancy accumulation at the grain boundaries [22,27,31]. The current study investigates the effect of the fully recrystallized microstructure, or high burnup structure, on the porosity, swelling, and composition of the monolithic fuel, since limited data exists within this regime.

## 1.2. Monolithic fuel swelling model limitations

Due to the irradiation-induced recrystallization of the microstructure and the change in the porosity behavior, current mechanistic swelling models suggest that the fuel undergoes a two-part primary swelling behavior: an initial linear swelling rate, followed by an increased second-order swelling rate caused by the accumulation and growth of porosity resulting from the increase in grain boundary area due to irradiation-induced recrystallization [13,22,27]. However, recent fuel plate measurements show a discrepancy between the older semi-mechanistic models and the more modern profilometry results at high burnup [32], as well as variations in local swelling due to location-specific behavior [32,33]. Up to this point in time, modeling efforts dedicated to the recrystallized porosity evolution models [34] have relied on limited data which encompassed dispersion fuels only and with limited fission densities irradiated up to 5.7 × 1021 fissions/cm3 [13], which is near the completion of irradiation-induced recrystallization [22,27,28]. Therefore, the implications of the recrystallized microstructure on the porosity evolution in monolithic fuel at high burnups are not fully realized. To better understand the porosity evolution of the post-recrystallized microstructure and its impact on swelling for monolithic fuels, the current study examines an irradiated U-10wt.%Mo monolithic fuel plate to characterize the high-burnup porosity and swelling evolution as a function of fission density to aid in the current modeling efforts.

## 1.3. Monolithic fuel experimental limitations

To the best of the authors’ knowledge, a systematic study has not investigated the porosity evolution in fully recrystallized (high burnup) U-10wt.%Mo monolithic fuels. Several porosity fraction data points were obtained from the AFIP-6-MkII experiment above the recrystallization regime [35], however this fuel plate had a fabrication flaw, specifically an uneven distribution of Mo in the fuel plate [6], resulting in uneven fission density and thermal conductivity. The uneven distribution of Mo content and the atypical spatial heating of the fuel plate during operation lead to increased phase decomposition and uncertainty in the local fission density based on the MCNP fission density modeling [35]. Therefore, porosity fraction measurements from the AFIP-6-MkII experiment are considered suspect. The behavior of pore evolution in high burn-up U-7wt.%Mo dispersion fuels has been experimentally analyzed. However, chemical differences between the dispersion coatings, matrix-fuel interactions, and other plate-specific manufacturing and irradiation parameters add complexity to the dispersion fuel porosity measurements that are irrelevant to monolithic fuels [36–38], and therefore not directly comparable.

The current study eliminates the plate-specific parametric influences, including temperature, time in reactor, fuel plate orientation, and power density, on pore evolution by isolating experimental parameters via selecting a single fuel plate that experienced a wide range of fission densities. In general, fuel plates also experience different creep behaviors at the fuel ends and the centerline location of the fuel [33]. Therefore, the current study also isolates the location-specific impact on porosity and solely captures the influence of fission density on pore evolution. Three locations of a high-burnup fuel plate with varying local fission densities were selected by removing focused ion beam (FIB)-cuboid specimens. Although previous researchers published a report on porosity evolution up to 3.74 - 6.23 × 1021 fissions/cm3 in monolithic fuels, which encompasses the recrystallization regime, the microstructural implications (i.e., grain size and porosity) of a fully recrystallized high burnup fuel were not captured [35]. The current study assesses the progression of the porosity fraction, pore morphology, pore size distribution, swelling, and fuel composition of a fully recrystallized monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuel as a function of burnup to characterize the high-burnup effects on pore evolution and chemical composition where little to no data exists.

## 2. Materials and methods

## 2.1. Specimen description, irradiation history and local fission density

The specimens used in the current study were obtained from a neutron-irradiated monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuel plate (ID# L1P7A0). The fuel was enriched to 70 % 235U and irradiated face-on in three cycles for a total of 137.2 days in the Advanced Test Reactor (ATR) at Idaho National Laboratory (INL) as part of the Reduced Enrichment for Research and Test Reactors experiment number 12 (RERTR-12), experiencing an average burnup of 7.71 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . Further irradiation history and manufacturing specifications are found in references [30,39,40]. The fuel completely recrystallized during irradiation [30], removing the grain-specific influence on the measured porosity due to the selection of the lift-out location. Grain-specific influence, such as intragranular nucleation of porosity in contrast to intergranular nucleation and grain boundary sink behavior, is of concern when directly comparing between samples since the unirradiated grains, which measure \~10 µm in diameter [11,12], are close in size to the FIB-cuboid lift-outs obtained for this study. However, the recrystallized grains are \~200–500 nm in size [2,4,18,19,22,24–26,28]; therefore, the specific grain impact is negligible due to the large number of grains contained within the FIB-cuboid specimens.

Three FIB-cuboid specimens (\~75 × \~75 × \~75 µm) were removed from the fuel plate cross section using a shielded Helios G4 plasma-FIB (P-FIB). Stitched optical micrographs encompassing the entire fuel plate are shown in Fig. 1(a) along with a colorimetric overlay of the fission density. Locations for the specimen selection were chosen at the fuel plate edges, as the most significant fission gradients are expected here from the face-on irradiation direction, giving the largest differences in fission density for the specimens, while maintaining similar reactor conditions. The regional plate selection was utilized to directly compare the fission density and the sample conditions while isolating as many physical parameters as experimentally possible, including creep behavior, temperature, local composition, and diffusion barrier thickness. The three lift-out locations are indicated by the red squares in the optical micrograph of the fuel plate section shown in Fig. 1(a). The respective backscattered electron (BSE)-SEM overview images of the FIB-cuboid are given in Fig. 1(b-d) with fission densities of 8.86 × 1021, 9.05 × 1021, and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , respectively.

The local fission density of the FIB-cuboid lift-out locations was determined with 137Cs measurements from a precision gamma scanner (PGS) and Monte Carlo N-Particle simulations of the plate [30,40]. Due to the relatively long half-life of 137Cs and its relative lack of thermal and mechanical transport, it is used to estimate the local fission density trends of the fuel [30]. The vertical PGS scan of the fuel plate was scaled by the MCNP calculated average fission density of the entire plate of 7.71 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , then used to determine the average fission density of the plate section based on the sectioning location of \~12.7 mm from the cladding edge. Since this location is between two points in the PGS 137Cs grid [30,40], interpolation was used, giving an average fission density of 8.27 × 1021 fissions/cm3 for the fuel section. The horizontal PGS 137Cs scan was then scaled to the fission density using the average fission density previously calculated and then interpolated to create a colorimetric map of fission density across the fuel plate section. The fission density map showed a peak fission density of 9.39 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . The locations where the FIB-cuboids were extracted were then compared to the colorimetric map, indicating the local fission density, as shown in Fig. 1(a), giving. 8.86 × 1021, 9.05 × 1021, and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , as shown in Fig. 1(b-d), respectively. The highest burnup specimen was taken near the constrained region of the fuel plate.

## 2.2. Specimen preparation, image capture, and image processing

Each of the three fuel-containing faces of the FIB-cuboid was prepared using a Helios G4 P-FIB-SEM and characterized with BSE imaging and EDS. The BSE-SEM images were taken using an Everhart-Thorney detector at the highest possible magnification while still having the complete specimen in the field-of-view, resulting in a pixel pitch of 0.0337 or 0.0450 µm, depending on the size of the face image. Despite the differences in SEM parameters, no difference in image quality or resolvability of features was observed. The difference in the imaging parameters was conducted to maintain a significant signal-to-noise ratio and to account for changes in the degree of curtaining on the specimen surfaces. As a result of the P-FIB fabrication and the substantial number of pores in the specimens, the surfaces contained notable curtains. A plug-and-play de-curtaining and denoising algorithm [41,42] was used to remove the physical artifacts and reduce the Poisson noise in the image to allow for accurate microstructural characterization.

## 2.3. Microstructural characterization

The Zr diffusion barrier image contrast overlapped with the image contrast of the porosity, which could cause errors in the measurement of the porosity. Therefore, the Zr diffusion barrier was masked using the EDS intensity map to allow the fuel to be segmented independently. Details on the algorithm used to overlay the EDS intensity map with the BSE image are provided in Appendix A.

The porosity exhibited a wide range of contrast due to the differing depths of the pores. Because of this behavior, standard thresholding or auto-local thresholding caused errors in the segmentation of the microstructure. The segmentation errors primarily result from shallow pores or pores with an internal structure, such as precipitates or interconnectedness, that creates uneven depth. The shallowness of the porosity causes the segmentation to identify the higher contrast regions of the internal features are part of the matrix, leading to pore segmentations that are not fully enclosed. Therefore, a multi-step segmentation procedure was implemented to alleviate this. The procedure and methodology of the multi-step algorithm are detailed in Appendix B.

The ASTM E562 systematic point counting method [43] with an area per point of 10 µm2 was carried out on a representative face of U-10wt.% Mo specimens with an average burnup of 8.86 × 1021 and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 to verify the precision of the aforementioned segmentation process to measure the porosity fraction. After segmentation, the volume fraction (Vv) of the porosity was calculated by dividing the measured pore area by the total fuel area for each micrograph. The average Vv of porosity per fission density was determined by calculating the mean of the three fuel-containing faces, and the error was defined using the standard error. To determine the pore size distribution per fission density, the area of each pore was calculated by counting all the pixels that constituted the object (pore). The equivalent diameter of each pore was then calculated using the measured area. The pore area distribution of each sample was calculated using all three faces, and the number of pores per area (NA) was determined by dividing by the total area of the fuel of each specimen. The NA was then converted to the number per volume (Nv) using an automated Schwartz-Saltykov algorithm adapted from [44] with a bin size of 0.15 µm to depict the pore size distribution accurately.

## 2.4. EDS analysis

The EDS spectral maps of the fuel-containing faces of the specimens were obtained with a 512-pixel horizontal width at eight frames per scan using 20 keV beam energy with an Octane Elite SiN 25 mm2 detector with an energy resolution of Mn Kα of 131.04 eV, azimuthal angle of 29◦, and a lifetime of 660 s. The summed spectral image signal for the fuel section, corresponding spectral maps, and specimen knowledge were used to identify the fuel elements. Each elemental map was calculated from the integrated intensity of the Lα or Mα line using two times the full-width at half maximum (FWHM) of the X-ray line using the Python module Hyperspy [45].

The average composition of each sample was calculated using areal maps of each fuel face in EDAX TEAM using standardless quantification [46,47]. The quantification method used was the peaks background standardless method with atomic number correction, absorption correction, and fluorescence correction or PeBaZAF [46]. The PeBaZAF method was selected over the default net counts method, eZAF method, due to the uncertainty of the identity of the fission products. The eZAF method relies on the unity function to identify the composition of the individual components, making it more sensitive to unknown constituents, as compared to the PeBaZAF method [47]. The error in the average composition for each sample was estimated using the average error of each face reported in EDAX TEAM and the statistical error between the fuel faces.

## 3. Results

## 3.1. Image processing and characterization

The specimens pictured in Fig. 1(b-d) were re-imaged using BSE-SEM on three faces. Fig. 2 shows the raw BSE-SEM images of each of the three fuel-containing sides of each FIB cuboid with (a, d, g) representing 8.86 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , (b, e, h) representing 9.05 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , and (c, f, i) representing 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . The FIB-cuboid faces (a, b, c, g, h, and i) have the Zr-diffusion barrier present to show the interfacial effects of the Zr-fuel interaction on the porosity. Each FIB cuboid shows significant porosity with indications of interconnectivity and heavy curtain artifacts from the FIB milling in both the fuel and the Zr-diffusion barrier. The FIB cuboid irradiated to 8.86 × 1021 fissions/cm3 shows cracking in Fig. 2(a, g) that propagates within a region of higher porosity located \~5 µm from the diffusion barrier and then continuing through the diffusion barrier. Fig. 2(f) shows a hole created by unintentional over-milling from the FIB. The hole and impacted regions were meticulously removed during the analysis, so as not to impact the results of this study.

Due to the inherent uneven milling of the FIB resulting in heavy curtain artifacts in Fig. 2, a de-curtaining and denoising algorithm [41, 42] was used to mitigate the impact of the curtains on the porosity microstructure. The resultant de-curtained and denoised reconstructions of the BSE-SEM images in Fig. 2 are shown in Fig. 3, indicating that the curtains were successfully removed with minimal impact on the microstructure.

As the Zr-fuel interface is complex, the EDS registration algorithm was used to segment the Zr from the fuel to isolate the porosity for further segmentation. The effectiveness of this approach in segmenting the complex Zr-fuel boundary is shown in Fig. 4(a, b). The EDS intensity map of Zr was directly overlayed onto the BSE microstructure, as noted in red. The magnified view of the interface in Fig. 4(b) shows the complexity of the interface with Zr tendrils extending into the fuel.

Fig. 5(a) shows a representative FIB-cuboid BSE-SEM image of a high burnup U-10wt.%Mo microstructure. Fig. 5(b) shows the porosity segmentation using a Phansalkar and edge segmentation [48,49], indicating the inaccuracy of this attempt, as the algorithm only segmented pores along certain borders of the feature. The cracks in the samples were removed to limit the impact on the porosity segmentation due to the size and shape of the feature. The successful multi-step segmentation procedure, consisting of a Phansalkar and edge segmentation, convex overestimation, and then followed by both a Chan-Vese and edge region merging algorithm, as detailed in Appendix B, is visible in Fig. 5(c). Unlike in Fig. 5(b), the pores with complex internal structures are fully encompassed in Fig. 5(c), showing a more accurate representation of the pore boundaries. The effectiveness of the segmentation procedure is shown in Fig. 5(d) indicating the segmentation results overlayed onto the fuel microstructure. Most of the pores in Fig. 5(d) are accurately segmented, however, there are some cases of overestimated porosity. The most evident overestimation is a series of pores bisected by a fine crack, indicated in Fig. 5(a). Due to the crack, these pores were treated as interconnected porosity in the final segmentation as indicated by an ellipse in the segmentation overlay in Fig. 5(d). The instances of overestimation can cause errors in the porosity analysis presented in the current study; however, as these instances are few compared to the quantity of pores analyzed in the current research, their impact is minimal.

## 3.2. Delamination, cracking, and the Zr diffusion barrier

Delamination and cracking are visible from the optical image of the fuel plate in Fig. 1(a), indicated by white arrows. The fuel plate delamination is visible in the center of the fuel cross-section near the diffusion barrier on the right, occurring within a couple of microns of the diffusion barrier inside the fuel. Cracking is observed in the 8.86 and 9.05 × 1021 fissions/cm3 specimens, pictured in Fig. 2(a, b, g) and Fig. 3 (a, b, g), at opposite sides of the fuel plate section.

The large cracks in the 8.86 × 1021 fissions/cm3 specimen, shown in Fig. 2(a, g), that originated near the interface bisected several large pores, suggesting that the crack propagated through the large, interconnected porosity region that exists \~5 µm from the fuel-diffusion barrier interface. The Zr-fuel interface in all three specimens appears irregular, with Zr protruding into the U-Mo fuel, visible in Fig. 2(a-c, gi), suggesting an interaction between the fuel and the Zr diffusion barrier, as previously observed using S-µCT in a very high burnup U-10wt.% Mo monolithic fuel [10]. The Zr penetration is bisected by the large crack in the sample, indicating that the cracking occurred after the Zr penetration formation, and the Zr does not arrest the crack.

## 3.3. Porosity observations and statistics

The porosity of each sample was segmented using the procedure described in Section 2.3 and Appendix B. Fig. 6 shows the segmentations of the porosity displayed in all three faces from Fig. 3, the de-curtained and denoised images. All curtains shown in Fig. 2 were removed successfully with minimal impact on the segmentation of the features in the specimen, including the crack. The large hole pictured in Fig. 1(d), Fig. 2 (f), and Fig. 3(f) was caused by over-milling with the FIB. Meticulous care was taken to mask this region to eliminate the impact on the results, as is visible in the porosity segmentation in Fig. 6. No effect on the pores is evident as the damage is contained within the boundary of the hole in Fig. 2(f).

From the BSE-SEM images in Fig. 3 and the segmentation in Fig. 6, several observations of the porosity trends were made. A larger quantity of small pores (<0.45 µm) with sparse intermediate-sized pores (0.45–1.35 µm) appear near the Zr-diffusion barrier interface. Large pores (1.35–2.25 µm) and interconnected pores (>2.25 µm) begin to appear \~5 µm from the diffusion barrier in all three specimens, shown in Fig. 3 and Fig. 6. The specimen irradiated to 9.05 × 1021 fissions/cm3 has the most extensive interconnected porosity, as seen in Fig. 3(b) and Fig. 6(b). Most interconnected pores are not fully coalesced and retain their original shapes, as indicated by the aspherical nature of the pores in this region, in contrast to the large spherical morphologies expected of fully coalesced, over-pressurized pores [21,52]. This behavior, along with the observation that most of the porosity appears aspherical, suggests that many of the pores are interconnected and/or distorted by internal fission products. The specimen in Fig. 2(b, e, h) and Fig. 3(b, e, h) is located closest to the delamination region of all the specimens, suggesting a correlation between the increased interconnected porosity and the delamination occurrence.

Fig. 7 displays the porosity characteristics measured for each sample, specifically the porosity fraction, average pore size, and pore number density as a function of fission density. The average volume fraction (Vv) of porosity increases as a function of fission density, with each specimen measuring an average Vv of 27.77 ± 0.51, 35.12 ± 1.54, and 37.71 ± 0.44 % porosity, corresponding to fission densities of 8.86 × 1021, 9.05 × 1021, and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , respectively, as shown in Fig. 7(a). From the segmentations in Fig. 6, it is visually apparent that the quantity of large pores also increases as a function of fission density. On the other hand, the small porosity increases from 8.86 × 1021 to 9.05 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , but then decreases from 9.05 × 1021 to 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . From the segmentation in Fig. 6, the number of small pores increases as a function of fission density in selected areas between large pores, as indicated by the red ellipses in Fig. 6 (a-c). However, the overall quantity of small pores decreases as the distance between large pores reduces as a function of fission density, as seen in Fig. 6.

Fig. 7(b) shows that the average size of the pores initially decreased between 8.86 × 1021 to 9.05 × 1021 fissions/cm3 going from 0.45 ± 0.01 µm to 0.40 ± 0.01 µm, followed by an overall increase from 8.86 × 1021 to 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 of 0.45 ± 0.01 µm to 0.49 ± 0.02 µm. There is an inverse relationship with respect to the number density of the pores and the average pore size. The number density increased initially between 8.86 × 1021 to 9.05 × 1021 fissions/cm3 from 0.97 ± 0.05 to 1.19 ± 0.03 pores/µm2 , as shown in Fig. 7(c). Overall, the number density was within error for 8.86 × 1021 and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 ranging from 0.97 ± 0.05 to 0.98 ± 0.03 pores/µm2 , respectively. The inverse relationship of the average pore size and pore density measurements suggests that the nucleation stage dominates over the growth stage at 9.05 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . An overall increase in the average pore size as a function of fission density is observed as the growth and coalescence phases dominate at the highest fission density.

To verify the accuracy of the segmentation in measuring the porosity fraction, point counting with an area per point of 10 µm2 was conducted on a representative face of the lowest and highest burnup specimens in Fig. 3(a, c), respectively. An average of 29.84 ± 1.24 and 33.76 ± 0.36 % porosity was determined via point counting, while segmentation identified 28.53 and 36.84 % porosity for the lowest and highest burnup specimens, respectively. Thus, the segmentation methodology was deemed accurate as the segmented values bounded the point counting measurements and were within 1–3 % of the measured values.

## 3.4. Porosity distribution

Fig. 8 shows the 3D porosity size distribution calculated using the Schwartz-Saltykov extrapolation of the two-dimensional pore size distribution of each sample and normalized by the volume analyzed. Several trends are observed from the porosity distribution. All three samples exhibited a bimodal porosity distribution, with each mode evolving differently as a function of fission density. The first porosity mode is centered at an equivalent pore diameter of 0.23 µm for all three specimens, while the second is centered at 0.98 µm for the 8.86 × 1021 fission/cm3 sample and 1.13 µm for the two higher burnup samples. As the size of the smallest pores increased up to 0.30 µm, the number density remained the highest for a fission density of 9.05 × 1021 fissions/ cm3 . However, at the highest fission density of 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , the number density of the smallest pores remained the lowest of all burnups compared, demonstrating consensus with the observations in Fig. 6. In general, for pores less than an equivalent diameter of 1.28 µm, the number decreased as a function of fission density. The number of intermediate-sized pores, between 0.6–1.5 µm, increased between 9.05 × 1021 and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . However, the number of pores per unit volume decreased from 8.86 × 1021 to 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 for the same size range. The large pores (> 1.5 µm) increased in quantity as a function of burnup, and all specimens showed discrete interconnected porosity greater than 2.55 µm, with the largest quantities observed in the 9.05 × 1021 and 9.36 × 1021 fissions/cm3 specimens.

## 3.5. EDS mapping and composition

The EDS maps for all three burnups in Fig. 9(a-c, g-i) show a pure Zr diffusion barrier along with the U-Mo fuel, while Fig. 9(d-f) shows the U-Mo fuel faces. Representative EDS spectra and the corresponding element X-ray lines are shown in Appendix C. In the fuel, the U and Mo concentrations are homogeneously distributed. The Zr-fuel interface has an irregular morphology for all three burnups, with Zr tendrils penetrating into the fuel, suggesting an interaction between the Zr and fuel. In contrast to the post-HIP microstructure of the unirradiated fuel, which shows a Mo-depleted zone at the Zr-fuel interface [8,9,12], a low-Mo content sublayer and U-Zr intermetallic phases were not observed in the current study.

Individual EDS elemental maps (U, Mo, Zr, Sr, Ce, Nd, Ba, and Cs) of a representative sample of bulk fuel irradiated at 9.05 × 1021 fissions/ cm3 with the maximum contrast for each map are shown in Fig. 10. An overlap between the Pt coating and the Zr intensity map is visible in Fig. 10(d) and Zr shows an overall homogeneous concentration within the bulk fuel. The fission products, which are displayed in Fig. 10, demonstrate distinct behaviors, as exemplified in the magnified view in Fig. 11. For example, Sr and Ba fission products, visible in Fig. 10(e, h) and indicated by red arrows, co-precipitate within the pores, shown as primarily yellow precipitates in Fig. 11(d-f). Whereas Ce and Cs fission products in Fig. 10(f, i) also accumulate within some pores, but not to the extent observed with the Sr and Ba fission products, as indicated by the purple discoloration near select pores in Fig. 11(d-f). The accumulation of fission products in the pores is more prominent in the larger pores, shown in Fig. 10(d-f). In contrast, the Nd fission products visible in Fig. 10(g), accumulate on the pore peripheries, visible as magenta regions near the large pores, shown in Fig. 11(d-f).

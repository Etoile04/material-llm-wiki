# The role of UC inclusions in the development of fission gas bubble superlattice neutron-irradiated monolithic U-10Mo fuels

## A B S T R A C T

Uranium Carbide (UC) inclusions are the most prevalent impurities in Uranium-Molybdenum (U-Mo) fuel and are considered undesirable because they could potentially affect fuel performance. This work revealed that, like grain boundaries, UC inclusions could help facilitate the formation of the fission gas bubble superlattice (GBS). The GBS is a highly organized complex defect structure that can effectively store fission gases, thereby inhibiting fuel swelling. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) showed that GBS self-organization can initiate at the UC/ U-Mo interfaces in U-10Mo fuel irradiated to low fission density. This study also revealed that the UC/U-Mo phase boundary in U-10Mo irradiated to low fission density is wavy and periodic in morphology and that GBS formation is semi-coherent with the UC boundary. Because the initiation of GBS preferentially occurs at grain boundaries and UC/U-Mo phase boundaries, it is expected that following GBS collapse at higher fission densities, high burnup structure development may initiate first at grain boundaries and UC/U-Mo interfaces.

## 1. Introduction

U-Mo is the prime low-enriched fuel candidate for high-performance research and test reactors because of its stable irradiation behavior and advantageous mechanical properties [1,2]. Alloying U with Mo allows the stabilization of the microstructure in isotropic γU-Mo (body-- centered cubic) phase; however, during irradiation γU-Mo can be transformed to α-U+ γ-U Mo or α-U + Mo-enriched γU-Mo via eutectoid or discontinuous precipitation, respectively [2]. U-Mo fuels can also be comprised of undesirable non-metallic inclusions including UCs and U MoSi C believed to be gathered from the feedstock materials and or during casting [3]. UC second-phase particles are the most common inclusions found in as-fabricated U-Mo fuel and have been studied via experiments and modeling [4–8]. The presence of UC in U-Mo fuel could not only affect the fuel fabrication process but also U-Mo’s microstructural evolution and performance during irradiation.

The influence of UC inclusions on the irradiation behavior of U-Mo is an ongoing research effort in the community. However, based on available literature UCs can affect U-Mo’s microstructural evolution to a degree. Density functional theory (DFT) studies have shown that the mechanical strength of the UC/U-Mo interface can slightly decrease if the UC hyper-stoichiometry is increased or if there is a decline in Mo content [5]. Experimental studies reported that U235 enrichment in UC inclusions can vary compared to U-Mo because of the inhomogeneous mixing of depleted and enriched uranium feedstocks [7]. The properties of U-Mo can be influenced by the number of inclusions because they can alter the transformation kinetics and collect U235 from the U-Mo fuel matrix [7]. U2MoSi2C is a recently discovered inclusion phase in U-Mo and has been reported to possess a tetragonal lattice; however, little is known about its influence on irradiated U-Mo microstructure [7]. C and Si-rich inclusions have been identified as the most prominent inclusions found within the grains and at the grain boundaries [3,5,8,9]. For example, U2MoSi2C found at grain boundaries can influence the discontinuous precipitation of γU-Mo during sub-eutectoid annealing [8, 10]. Characterization studies performed on as-fabricated U-10Mo confirmed the presence of other minor inclusions including Al, B, O, P, Ti, Ca, and Mn which tend to segregate to the Si-rich inclusions. Interestingly, it was also shown that the segregation of these minor inclusions at the Si-rich phase does not alter Mo content at the interface between U-Mo and the Si-rich inclusions. Further, characterization showed that

Si itself can segregate to the UC/U-Mo interface without Mo depletion. Based on the DFT findings in Ref [5], if the Mo content at the U-Mo-inclusion interfaces is not altered, then the mechanical properties at the U-Mo-inclusion interfaces will not change.

One of the most unique characteristics of irradiated U-Mo fuel is its ability to form a highly ordered complex defect structure known as the fission GBS [11–16]. Since its discovery, the GBS in neutron-irradiated U-Mo fuel has garnered attention for its capability to store high-energy fission gasses like xenon (Xe) and krypton (Kr). Typically, superlattices formed in metals are isomorphic to their host materials; however, there are some exceptions including U-Mo where the superlattice structure differs from the host material [13]. The formation of the GBS provides irradiation-resistant advantages in U-Mo fuel in the role it plays to delay the release of fission gasses that could lead to breakaway swelling. Fission gasses not only cause swelling in nuclear fuel but can also influence U-Mo’s thermal conductivity and the neutron economy of the reactor [17]. The GBS structure can be considered a highly organized storage cabinet for fission gasses that forms naturally in U-Mo fuel during irradiation. However, like storage cabinets, it does not possess an infinite amount of space because of the increased production of fission gasses at high fission densities. The GBS can become supersaturated and collapse, releasing the stored fission gasses that later agglomerate in and contribute to the growth of micron-sized pores. The formation and collapse mechanisms of the GBS are still not well understood; however, diffusion anisotropy has been proposed by computational studies to explain its formation [18,19]. Most of the available experimental research on GBS characterization is of U-Mo fuel irradiated to high fission densities where the GBS is already completely formed [14, 20–24]. There is a lack of characterization studies available at low fission densities that capture the initial formation of the GBS. In recent contributions, TEM characterization of U-7Mo dispersion fuels irradiated to low burnup revealed that GBS initiation begins at the grain boundaries [25,26].

The jury is still out on the influence of inclusions such as UC and U2MoSi2C on the overall irradiation performance of U-Mo fuel; however, UCs do not appear to have a significant impact on U-Mo fuel degradation in reactor environments [5]. Computational studies have shown that carbide inclusions have a minor impact on the mechanical property of U-Mo fuel when the volume fraction is between 0.5 and 1% [5]. More literature is available on the microstructural characterization of UC inclusions than U2MoSi2C because the latter was only recently discovered [2,4,5,8,27]. It remains unclear how the presence of these inclusions could impact early GBS formation and evolution. Within the context of the GBS, the role of inclusions in the progressive development of the GBS has not been studied. This work reports the TEM characterization on irradiated monolithic U-10Mo irradiated to low fission density to study how the presence of UC inclusions can accommodate or hinder GBS self-organization.

## 2. Materials and methods

The U-10Mo fuel specimen investigated in this study was irradiated as a part of the MP1 irradiation experiments. The U-10Mo fuel crosssection is identified by KGT-3706 with an average fission density of \~1.6 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . TEM lamellae samples were obtained from the midplane of the fuel cross-section where the local fission density in \~1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3 . The TEM lamellae sample each measuring 20 µm × 10 µm × 100 nm were prepared using the FEI quanta focused ion beam scanning electron microscope (FIB/SEM). Samples from each fuel plate were prepared by depositing platinum (Pt) protective layers of 20 µm × 2 µm × 2 µm at 10 kV and 0.2nA near the fuel midplane. The samples were then coarse-trenched to obtain using the focused ion beam in-situ lift-out system at 10 kV and 6nA and later mounted on a copper (Cu) grid. Following mounting, the samples were iteratively thinned on both sides at 10 kV and low beam currents with the final polish performed at 1nA to minimize Ga beam ion damage. The Thermo Fisher

Scientific Titan TEM equipped with scanning/TEM (STEM) capability was utilized to acquire bright field (BF), dark field (DF), high resolution (HR) micrographs, as well as diffraction patterns of UC inclusions and the fuel microstructure.

## 3. Results and discussion

The current work investigated GBS formation at U-Mo-inclusion interface boundaries in U-10Mo fuel at 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3 using TEM. In this regard, Fig. 1 shows an overview of the fuel microstructure highlighting the interface between U-Mo and a 1µm-sized UC inclusion where the development of GBS is apparent. One notable observation is the periodic boundary of the UC phase at the U-Mo/UC interface. Large dislocation networks are observed in the U-Mo phase with some dislocation lines connecting the UC boundary to the U-Mo phase. Despite evidence of dislocation-precipitate interactions near the UC inclusion boundary, the highly ordered GBS structure is well-defined at the UC/U-Mo interface.

Fig. 2 further depicts BF micrographs of the interface boundary between U-Mo and a 1µm-sized UC inclusion where the ordered GBS is observed to originally nucleate as it extends towards the U-Mo grain interior. At 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3 , the GBS along the interface has a high degree of ordering and expands up to 140 nm towards the grain interior from the UC/U-Mo interface; however, complete distribution of GBS in U-Mo had not yet occurred. Interestingly, the expansion direction of the GBS appears to be consistent with the wavy periodicity of the UC boundary in Fig. 2 (d,e,f). Although UC has a face-centered cubic (fcc) crystal structure while U-Mo possesses a bcc crystal structure, there is some coherency in the apparent alignment between the GBS and the wavy boundary of the UC inclusion in Fig. 2(e,f).

Selected area diffraction (SAD) patterns of the UC and U-Mo phases in Fig. 2(a) are provided in Fig. 2(b,c), respectively. The SAD patterns of the U-Mo and UC phases indicate the orientation between the UC and U-Mo phase at the phase boundary with both phases oriented near the 〈110〉 family of zones. While studies have shown that GBS selforganization begins at grain boundaries and is further confirmed in the BF micrograph in Fig. 3, the development of GBS at the U-Mo/UC interface provides evidence that GBS early stage-formation is not limited self-organization along grain boundaries.

While early-stage GBS self-organization appears on the UC/U-Mo phase boundaries and grain boundaries, GBS formation was not evident along the MoSi-rich grain boundary shown in Fig. 3(a). STEM micrograph and EDS maps in Fig. 4 provide the elemental context of the Si-rich grain. Based on the EDS maps, the authors suspect that it is a U2MoSi2C inclusion phase. U2MoSi2C reported in as-fabricated U-10Mo, is a wetting phase that can form within the homogenized microstructure of as fabricated and can change in morphology depending on homogenization temperature [2]. Previous studies have reported that during high-temperature homogenization treatments between 1000 ◦C, the U2MoSi2C inclusion is a thin intergranular wetting phase at the as-fabricated γU-Mo grain boundaries; however, during low-temperature homogenization treatments of 900 ◦C, U2MoSi2C often forms at the interface boundaries of γU-Mo/UC or as faceted precipitates along the grain boundary. [2]. Atom probe tomography (APT) investigations of U2MoSi2C second phase precipitate in as-fabricated U-10Mo revealed minor elements including Al, B, O, P, Ti, Ca, and Ni. However, the segregation of those minor elements could not be confirmed in the irradiated U-10Mo specimen investigated in this work. The irradiated U-10Mo sample presented in this study is amorphous, unlike its initial crystalline morphology previously reported in as-fabricated U-10Mo fuel [2]. The change in the morphology could be possibly induced by neutron irradiation. Albeit the influence of the U2MoSi2C inclusion phase on microstructural evolution during irradiation is still unclear, it does not appear to play a significant role in microstructural degradation at 1.30 × 1021 fissions/cm3 .

Previous APT characterization of as-fabricated U-10Mo showed Si segregation up to 3at% at the UC boundary while Mo depletion was not evident [8]. STEM/EDS maps were also taken of the GBS at the UC/U-Mo interface at a higher resolution in Fig. 5. The map shows that the GBS is carbon-depleted; however, several fission products in the GBS are observed including Xe. At the magnification presented in Fig. 5, no obvious chemical segregation or depletion was observed along the UC’s periodic boundary in Figs. 4 and 5. High-resolution APT characterization analysis of the UC/U-Mo interface could provide a better understanding of the chemical behavior at the UC periodic boundary.

Because the GBS has two identified modes of initiation: (1) from the U-Mo grain boundary [26] and (2) from the UC/U-Mo interface boundary. This study found no notable differences between the GBS bubble size at the grain boundaries and the UC/U-Mo interface boundary in the same grain (see Fig. 3(a)). However, there is a clear difference in bubble size between the disordered bubbles and the ordered GBS along the UC/U-Mo interface within the same grain in Fig. 6(a) and (b), respectively. The bubble size distributions shown in Fig. 6(c) infer that bubble ordering could initiate at \~3 nm, consistent with a recent study [26]. Small distortions in the GBS are attributed to dislocations as outlined in the dashed white line in Fig. 6(b). These dislocations are formed and become mobile when stress is applied to the system. In this case, it is possible that the stress from the migration of fission products to form the GBS may contribute to the observed distortion.

Other smaller UC inclusion phases were captured in the U-10Mo microstructure in the BF micrograph montage in Fig. 7. These smaller UC phases consisted of similar boundary morphology as the 1µm-sized carbide shown in Figs. 2 and 3. Each row in Fig. 7 depicts a different UC inclusion phase whose magnification is increased from left to right. The first row in Fig. 7 shows a 400 nm-sized UC of a circular nature with dislocation networks clustered around the boundary of the UC inclusion. However, despite the large dislocation network along the boundary between the UC and the U-Mo phase, the highly ordered GBS is developed along the interface and seemingly extends 100–140 nm towards the grain interior from the UC/U-Mo interface boundary. The second row in Fig. 7 also highlights the GBS development at the UC/U-Mo interface boundary of a more rectangular-shaped 982 nm-sized UC inclusion. A similar dislocation interaction at the UC boundary is noted here with a clear wavy periodicity characteristic like that noted in the UC inclusion in Fig. 2. At such high magnification, at least 2 layers of the wavy-like boundary can be seen in the second row of Fig. 7. Further, the GBS development at the UC/U-Mo boundary is consistent with 118–175 nm growth towards the U-Mo phase from the UC boundary.

Another UC inclusion is represented in the third row of Fig. 7, where the inclusion size was measured at \~1.1 µm. GBS development from the U-Mo/UC interface was observed between 75 and 100 nm into the U-Mo phase. The interactions between the dislocation networks, bubble ordering, and the UC/U-Mo interface boundary may contribute to GBS development and evolution in the fuel microstructure. A study reported that there was no obvious orientation relationship between the U-Mo and UC phases in as-fabricated U-10Mo fuel [4]. However, in this work, the GBS orientation in irradiated U-10Mo appears to be semi-coherent with the orientation of the wavy periodic feature for the UC boundary. Because the two phases have different crystal structures, the interface boundary misorientation cannot be readily calculated using the zone axes. Nevertheless, the presence of UC inclusions in the microstructure seems advantageous for fission gas retention via the preferential GBS formation at the UC/U-Mo interface. Detailed studies on the interface boundary strength are necessary to investigate the degree of coherency between at U-Mo and UC interface boundaries to understand the stress distribution across the boundary. Fig. 8 shows a BF micrograph of two

UC inclusions inside a U-Mo grain and the corresponding SAD pattern captured at the UC/U-Mo interface where the GBS is fully developed at the boundary. It is expected that the GBS will continue to extend towards the U-Mo phase until the GBS is fully developed in the grain separating the two UC inclusions. Some GBS connection is visible in the region where the UC inclusions are closest in distance in Fig. 8.

The wavy periodicity of the UC boundary is a new and peculiar observation and is possibly suggestive of the role of the concentration wave mechanism discussed in Ref [18]. Concentration waves are known to develop along planes that are crystallographically equivalent thereby advancing the spatial organization of vacancy nodes. There are two ways in which superlattices can form: (1) all at once via spinodal decomposition or (2) gradually via preferential nucleation, growth, and coarsening [18]. Concerning GBS formation in U-Mo fuel our study agrees with the latter with preferential nucleation occurring at UC/U-Mo interfaces and U-Mo grain boundaries. It is possible that the semi-coherency of the GBS and the UC boundary, as well as the periodicity of the UC boundary, could be due to the concentration wave mechanism. Because UC has a uranium component, fission can also occur in the UC phase within the U-Mo system, the simultaneous release of Xe fission gas from the UC phase and the U-Mo bulk system could contribute to observed preferential nucleation at the UC/U-Mo interface trapping site. It is also possible that Xe solubility in U-Mo and UC could be another contributing factor to the directional spread of the GBS from the UC towards the U-Mo grain interior.

The presence of UC inclusions can be beneficial in the U-Mo fuel system because they help to facilitate GBS growth. Authors believe that the UC/U-Mo boundary is a high-stress region which is one of the characteristics that enables GBS nucleation at the interface. Since GBS formation initiates at grain boundaries and the UC/U-Mo interface during the early stages of irradiation, it is expected that at higher fission densities, the boundaries will become saturated such that degradation via grain refinement will initiate. The presence of inclusions in nuclear fuel systems is generally undesirable; however, during the early stages of irradiation, GBS formation favors U-Mo/UC interfaces. However, at higher fission densities, grain boundaries and U-Mo-UC interfaces could act at preferential sites for high burnup structure (HBS) formation. It follows that although the presence of UC inclusions facilitates GBS formation, the presence of the UC inclusion itself may not be favorable for overall fuel performance at much higher fission densities.

HBS is the result of restructuring that occurs in nuclear fuels during irradiation and is characterized by sub-divided grains and micron-sized fission gas pores [28]. In a previous publication, authors postulated that there may exist more than one mechanism responsible for HBS formation in U-Mo fuels [28]. Polygonization is the dominant mechanism during early grain refinement [28–30]; however, it has been proposed that at higher fission densities, dynamic recrystallization becomes the more dominant mechanism [28]. This work investigated U-Mo microstructure irradiated at a low fission density which provides insight into early GBS development. Because early GBS formation is observed at grain boundaries and U-Mo/UC interfaces, it is expected that as the fission density increases, the superlattice will collapse first in those regions. Authors deduce that the collapse of the GBS will accelerate grain refinement and thus the formation of high burnup structure [28,31].

## 4. Conclusion

In a novel contribution to the early self-organizing behavior of GBS in U-Mo fuels, this work discovered that GBS formation can not only develop at the grain boundaries but also at U-Mo/UC interfaces. The UC boundary, characterized by its wavy periodicity, appears semi-coherent with the directional spread of the GBS. There is no notable difference in the bubble size distribution of the GBS formed at the UC/U-Mo interface and U-Mo grain boundary. During the early stages of irradiation, GBS formation is preferential to U-Mo grain boundaries and UC/U-Mo interfaces before expanding to U-Mo grain interiors; therefore, at higher fission densities, the grain boundaries and UC/U-Mo will continue to increase their gas inventory while the GBS develops. As a result, it is expected that GBS saturation and eventual collapse will occur first at the grain boundaries and UC/U-Mo interfaces, creating conditions for HBS initiation.

## 1. Introduction

The analysis of the swelling performance of MXtype nuclear fuels (carbides,carbonitrides and nitrides) is entering a new phase,in which basic premises and approaches are being re-examined and attempts at consolidation of the physical models are being made. Our understanding of the swelling effects in these fuels in the earlier stage of investigation was based on relatively few experimental observations, however, a causal reconstruction of the process was attempted, starting from simple,elementary mechanisms.These first interpretations have now to be reconsidered in the light of more complete experimental data.Furthermore,today,as important as the attempt to decide which causal sequences, among the imaginables ones,correctly define the phenomenon,is another question: to what extent are our attempts at describing fuel swelling by means of mathematical models justified? Eventually, does a more sophisticated and detailed analysis represent any better tool that could be suited to fuel optimization, than those in use?

Even a cursory glance at the present state of the fuel performance modelling work,reveals that there has been an unprecedented growth in complexity of the computer codes.The formidable problem of describing the strain history of a fuel pin during its irradiation lifetime could only be approached by an attempt to codify and systematize observed or expected effects,covering partial aspects of the field of investigation.The reliability of the theory predictions is thus determined both by the extent to which a code is able to account for the complexity of the system,and by the validity of the physical models. A unique improvement of one of the two aspects does not bring actual benefits to the analysis.

On the other hand the analysis that encompasses such a complex field should be numerically tractable within reasonable computing time and storage requirements. This poses the fundamental question of the greatest permissble simplification of the physical description of the phenomena involved. Moreover, the code must be able in each subfield to sort out from the imaginary matrix of mechanisms-by-situations the shortest way of coming to a reasonably correct evaluation of the effects.

This paper is an attempt to analyze and collect the mechanisms of fission gas swelling in such a manner that,although treated independently, they can be organized together to construct swelling models of different but in any case exhaustive complexity, in compliance with the needs arising in the various situations.

The analysis of fuel swelling has been conducted following a mechanistic scheme,whereby the properties of the atomic lattice, the ceramographic structure and the geometrical shape of the fuel are sequentially considered.

First a system of rate equations for the fission gas was developed, whose solution enables the concentration of gas in dynamic solution, the amount precipitated into intragranular bubbles and the rate of migration of gas to deep sinks (pores and grain boundaries) to be calculated. The mathematics and the physics of this first part of the treatment,merely dealing with fission gas kinetics, is presented and discussed in section 2.

Having defined the fisson gas behaviour, the second step consists of calculating the strain rates following precipitation of gas into bubbles and pores. This argument is treated in section 6.In practice, bubble (or pore) growth (or shrinkage) rates occurring by microscopic plastic deformation processes, generated by the pressure of the precipitated gas, are calculated, taking into account any existing internal stress field.Logically,this second stage of the treatment replaces the equation of state which,under assumption of equilibrium conditions,enables the concentration of fission gas in bubbles to be transformed into fractional volumes representing the effective change in density of the fuel.

In this basic framework,other aspects are considered in the analysis which,under certain conditions, must be taken into account to obtain a correct evaluation of the swelling rate.Thus,the size distribution and the mobilities of.the gas sinks (bubbles and pores) are considered with their mutual interactions (sections 3,5 and 6).Furthermore,biased gas diffusion in heterogeneous bubble populations and ripening effects are discussed in section 4.

Finally,in section 7 the gas release conditions are considered,taking into account the fuel morphology. The concept of pore interlinkage and of open porosity is introduced,to enable the ratio of gas retained in the grain boundaries and that escaping from the pellets to be calculated.

All the items treated are formally compatible and interdependent. In practice they have been organized together in the form of subroutines in a computer code, the flow chart of which is schematized in fig.1. Thanks to the structure of the analysis,different swelling

mechanisms of decreasing complexity can be constructed,by short cutting calculation steps,when the effects considered are not expected to be relevant in the particular case analyzed.

## 3.Analysis of the properties of the bubble distribution in MX-type fuels

## 3.1.Analysis of the bubble size distributions and evolution of their concentration as a function of time and temperature

Afundamental question in the analytical treatment of the fission gas swelling is the degree of accuracy by which the concept of bubble populations and size distributions has to be introduced. Rigorous approaches have been proposed [3] trying to deduce the development of the bubble size distributions,starting from the nucleation processes,through all the possible coalescence events between individuals of the distribution. However,even assuming that the available theory for the nucleation mechanisms is valid and exhaustive \*, this approach presents two difficulties,a conceptual and a practical one.The first one consists in the intrinsic disadvantage of a too detailed description of an idealized system,where quantities are calculated which,inany comparison with reality,are redundant and even misleading if the limits of validity of the theoretical description are not well known. In fact, the experimental observation shows that there'is a limit in the possibility of constructing an analytical picture of bubble formation in nuclear fuels.Even in the simplest cases,the heterogeneity of the system on the microscope scale is so large that all the quantities concerned (sizes,concentrations and rates) have to be averaged over relatively large volumes and the comparison with the analysis prediction has to be based on these values.An analytical model for swelling should also consider this stochastic aspect of the phenomenon,which sets a limit to the deterministic analysis.The second difficulty is inherent in the formalism for the calculation of the coalescence events. Even with the more recently formulated rate equations[4],where the mathematical analysis has been significantly improved,size distributions extending over several orders of magnitude (as observed in some cases in MX-type fuels) require long computing times or result in important loss of precision in the calculations.

Analternative method to describe the evolution of the bubble distribution is based on the concept of statistical population.This concept has been formally introduced in the basic kinetics equations (2),without a further definition,a population being simply identifiedby its label k andby the three variablesnk,rk andbx.The choice of thismethod instead of the deterministic one resulted mainly from the difficulties arising in the description of the MX-fuel swelling.There,intragranular bubbles grow to extremely large sizes,which eventually may akin to the intergranular and sintering pores.A first question in the description of these widely dispersed sizes is the degree of accuracy by which the concept of size class (or distribution) are to be introduced.This is not,as it seems,an inoffensive formal problem,but involves the significance of the experimental observations as well as their analytical prediction.In fact,starting from the former point of view,it can be realized that each experimental determination of bubble size histograms is strictly related to the experimental method used for the observation: depending on the magnification and on the optical resolution,parameters are defined like average sizes,concentrations,relative frequencies,etc., which,strictly speaking,are meaningless,since they are always referred to a limited number of visible features,which is usually very small compared to the true total number of possibly existing smaller individuals. Enlarging the magnification on the observed field does not solve the problem,first, because the true size distribution can in principle extend down to non-detectable atomic sizes,secondly,with a countingat very high magnification,the statistical relevance of features of large size inevitably loses any importance and,in consequence,phenomena of paramount importance in the macroscopic size range could pass unnoticed or be inadequately represented.The problem is also faced when,in some cases,a correlation has to be established between available porosity measurements carried out with different methods (TEM,REM and optical microscopy). In practice,considering the problem from an engineering point of view,many of the measurements should not be taken too seriously and a typical step size would be given which does not necessarily coincide with that needed in a study of the atomistic gas behaviour. Therefore,any observation would not be entirely objective,because the observer intervenes in its definition. Nevertheless, in spite of these difficulties,one recognizes that for results obtained in different limited observation fields,the said size distribution parameters,as conventionally defined,are representative and significant for the phenomena investigated. Furthermore, size histograms measured at different magnifications show strikingly similarcharacters (fig.3):the distributions are always skew and their maximum is not well defined but it depends on the (rather imprecise) frequencies in the lowest classes.The fact that pictures taken at different magnifications show identical features is not astonishing.This kind of self-similarity of the size distribution functions of certain existing set of individuals is often occurring in natural phenomena. The reason for this peculiarity has to be searched in the laws of discrete statistical aggregation of very large number of particles-in our case of voids [6]. This suggest a new approach in the representation of this kind of distributions,basing their description on a general formulation of the size generating mechanisms and on a statistical treatment of their field of occurrence.The advantage of this approach is that the concept of expected (or observed) population enables any order of description (or observation) to be accounted for.

## 4. Interaction between bubbles of different size classes: ripening effects and limit for continual nucleation

The range of stability of the bubble sizes is a fundamental aspect of the swelling analysis of MXtype fuels.In fact,it has been emphasized that the temperature variations occurring in these fuels during irradiation produce substantial modifications in the fission gas bubble populations.In particular,it has been shown that'when temperature decreases,new bubble populations with higher spatial concentration are predicted to nucleate and grow.Based on mere kinetics arguments,new bubbles should be continuously produced in this case,and the new nuclei,of increasing concentration,should capture the largest part of the gas created,preventing growth of the larger pre-existing bubbles.The effect is obviously of great significance for swelling,so that the conditions must be studied beyond which this analysis ceases to bevalid.Two questions have thus to be

answered in this connection,namely: - to which extent can the bubble nucleation process be affected by the presence of preexisting bubbles; -how can the minimal size of a distribution of stable bubbles be determined.

It is known from the theory of nucleation and growth of precipitates from oversaturated solutions, that the stability of small precipitates can be affected by the presence of larger ones,which“suck”from them atoms of solute,growing at their expense. The phenomenon, commonly called ripening effect,occurs according to relatively slow diffusion processes,activated by the difference in thermodynamic potential between precipitates of different sizes.This phenomenon can also be expected in the system formed by fission gas-in-solid and gas bubbles,taking into account the typical environment condition of a nuclear fuel.

There the ripening mechanism can be described as follows:

The fission gas concentration gradient in the vicinity of a bubble surface decreases with the pressure of the gas within the bubble.Bubbles of larger sizes have thena precipitation flux (i.e.a flow of gas per unit surface area) correspondingly higher than smaller ones.Under steady-state conditions (gas created at constant rate) and when large oversaturation levels are attained (low temperature),this difference is negligible,but it becomes significant when the concentration of gas in solution approaches the solubility value (high temperature).Therefore,if two bubbles are sufficiently close together(high spatial concentration) and their inner pressures differ sufficiently,the bubble at higher pressure (usually that of smaller size) is expected to emit gas,which will flow into that at lower pressure (see fig.10).

The results show that,although the ripening mechanism occursat arelatively low rate,it can play an important role for fission gas swelling.

Finally,the question arises why the coarsening effect,whose mechanism is based upon general thermodynamic considerations,should be effective in MX-type fuels and not,for instance in oxide fuels. The answer can be reasonably found in the different nature of the fission spike resolution processes occurring in the two types of materials.The observations made in studying there-solution effects indicate that fission fragments in oxides do not only re-eject single gas atoms from bubbles which are close to the fragment path,but also produce relevant material displacements into the struck voids.This is in agreement with the results of the study of the surface tracks of fission fragments in UO2 [14].Collsions events between fragments and bubbles are believed to cause the immediate destruction of the bubbles if they are sufficiently small[5,15]and to create new bubble nuclei within the spike volume [5]. If the lifetime of theindividual bubbles is consequently drastically reduced,slow processes like ripening become insignificant.In fact,the simple situation observed in oxide fuels (little variation of bubble concentration with time and temperature and relatively uniform sizes [5,16,17]) is thought to be the net effect of counteracting bulk resolution and coalescence phenomena. On the other hand,although no experimental data are presently available concerning the resolution mechanism in MX-type fuels,theoretical calculations of the temperature and stress fields in the spikes [18] exclude that sputtering processes and bulk displacements can be significantly produced in these compounds.The interaction of the fission fragments with bubbles in MX-type fuels should therefore merely result in re-ejection of gas atoms by primary and secondary collisions.

## 6. Dynamics of the porosity growth

In the previous treatment, nothing has been said about the mechanisms of bubble and pore growth following the fission gas precipitation.Porosity does grow until the pressure of the contained gas p, the resulting capillar stress due to the surface tension γ and the external hydrostatic stress Oo achieve the equilibrium condition satisfying the equation:

![](images/75019ddbc27c617fecf0fdb34e01a712ab7caaacf5b68ac5f4a0ef95d125848e.jpg)

Assuming a proper equation of state for the gas, which enables the pressure p to be expressed as a function of the bubble size r,the so called bubble equilibrium radius can be readily calculated.In most of the swelling models,the effective bubble size is simply assumed to be equal to its equilibrium value r.Actually,this hypothesis deserves a careful discussion.The forces driving the bubble growth to the equilibrium configurations are finite and decrease when the effective radius approachesr°,so that in principle the equilibrium size is only asymptotically attained with time.Therefore,before making simplifying assumptions,an analysis has to be performed of the time scale of the growth kinetics under the different specific conditions of the irradiated fuels. The situation of the fuel porosity during irradiation is non-stationary;gas atoms are continuously precipitating at variable rate and with the increase of bubble size,each precipitated gas atom requires an increasing number of vacancies in order that the equilibrium condition is established.Therefore,it is to be expected that when vacancies are not sufficiently mobile,a net overpressure is created in the bubbles,which are constantly prevented from achieving their equilibrium size.The need for such an analysis is evident when transient swelling has to be described; however,because of the very low selfdiffusivities involved,a dynamic model for bubble growth becomes essential in MX-type fuels also under steady-state regime.

## 7. The properties of the intergranular pores during irradiation

In the preceding paragraph,the growth of the coarse porosity in the fuel is calculated on the basis of pressure of the fission gas precipitated and of the external restraint.The fission gas kinetics equations (2)enable the gas flux to the grain boundaries to be calculated as a function of irradiation time.This flux is in part collected by sintering porosity (of concentration Np)and in part by large grain boundary bubbles,whose concentration Np is calculated with a procedure analogous,to that described in section 3. By knowing the concentrations N and Np, the calculation of local swelling rate can be performed according to the model described above.However, pores and bubbles growing on the grain boundaries have a distinctive property which has to be accounted for; this is the high probability that contact points are established between neighbouring individuals. If the density of contact points between pores is large enough,it can be intuitively deduced that chains of interlinked pores are formed,and that,in the limiting case,the chains may be as long as the macroscopic linear dimensions of the sample.The possible existence of these chains leads to the definition of a new class of pores,called open porosity,whose development must be treated separately.In fact, the fission gas pressure in the open pores falls very quickly to the level of the plenum pressure,because the interlinking channels provide a direct release path of the gas.The calculation of the open pore fraction within a given fuel local structure is therefore necessary to analyze the mutually dependent phenomena of intergranular swelling and gas release.

To solve this problem, two approaches are in principle possible.With the first one,based on physical considerations,one tries to explain pore interlinkage as an equilibrium configuration attained during growth,under the action of inner pressure and grain boundary and surface energies.The models developed on this basis [34] have brought few practical advantages,because the geometry assumed is rather complicated and finally of little significance in concrete applications.However, these models have put forward physical arguments of the structural stability of the interlinkage configurations,so that,based on these proofs,a second more practical approach can be assumed,dealing with the usually available parameters,characterizing the fuel structure.

A very general way of defining the concept of interlinkage between pores is that based on the statistical definition of percolation probability through an ordered spatial distribution of nodes.Assuming a lattice of sites (pores) connected with bonds (channels or contact points)randomly distributed, with an average concentration P= (number of bonds)/(number of sites),a total interlinkage is thought to occur when a finite probability is calculated for an infinite chain of bonds starting from a given site.The analysis of the interlinking probability can be carried out without regard to how the bonds are created between the pores,but simply assuming that they exist and are stable.The model, previously developed for LWR fuels [35],has now been extended to FBR advanced fuels.

## 7.2.Release through percolation and intergranular swelling

The knowledge of the open pore fraction enables a calculation of the gas release rate to be made with more precision. The introduction of the concept of open chains and partial retention of gas in the grainboundary porosity is also extremely important to calculate swelling and hot pressing in temperature ranges where a significant flow of gas is arriving at the grain boundaries,but pores are not mobile enough to act as gas carriers over long distances. This is in practice the situation encountered in MX-type fuels for a large number of cases.

The calculation of gas release and intergranular swelling (or hot pressing) is made in the model following a simple scheme:

(i) An initial distribution of pore and grain sizes is taken as input.This distribution has to be determined by an accurate ceramographic analysis of the starting material.In cases where grain growth occurs during irradiation,the mode and the extent of this process is accounted for.

(ii) After calculating the interlinkage factor,a fraction F of the porosity is considered open and thegas arriving there released.In the remnant (1-Fo)of the pores,the gas contributes to increase the inner pressure so that they are forced to grow.

(iii) The pore volume increase under the gas thrust is calculated according to the model described in the preceding paragraph.The evolving porosity produces a change inF,so that new situations are created,by which closed pores become open releasing their gas content. The pressure collapse in these pores further activates hot pressing.

The first question arising from the above considerations,regards the optimum local distribution of the fabrication porosity in order to obtain the highest percolation factor.A study of the function (59) as a function of the fabrication parameters,shows that for a given porosity interlinkage increase with the ratio s (grain to pore size).However, the value of s required to obtain a high interlinkage ratio increases steeply with decreasing porosity(fig.17),so that in practice onlywith fabrication porosities above 10% there is a chance of obtaining sintered pellets with more than 5O% open pores.On the other hand,an indefinite increase of the grain size is only a hypothetical way of increasing interlinkage,because with increase of grain size more and more pores are in fact left within the grains.Moreover,large grains are often subdivided into subgrains on the boundaries of which preferential trapping of gas is observed to occur as on grain boundaries.

In practice,the largest suitable value of s can only be estimated from an accurate ceramographic analysis of the fuels in connection with the results obtainable with the fabrication methods adopted.In any case, least recommendable are structures which present a high percentage of pores located within the grains. This porosity does not contribute to formation of open chainsand from itself it represents a gas reservoir of negligible capacity: small amounts of gas can in fact fillarge pores up to pressures at which hot pressing is made effectively impossible.

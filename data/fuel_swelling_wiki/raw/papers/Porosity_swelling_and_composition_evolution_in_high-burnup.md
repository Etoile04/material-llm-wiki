## A B S T R A C T

The microstructural progression of very high-burnup $( > 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 ) monolithic uranium-molybdenum (U-10wt.%Mo) was analyzed, providing crucial insights into the behavior of post-recrystallized nuclear fuel, where scant data exists. Three focused ion beam cuboids sourced from a fuel plate with varying local burnups of 8.86 × $\phantom { + } 1 0 ^ { 2 1 } , 9 . 0 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ , and $9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 were characterized. The porosity and composition of the samples were evaluated to characterize the evolution of the microstructure as a function of fission density and different locations on the fuel plate, while simultaneously isolating plate-specific parameters such as Zr diffusion barrier thickness, hot-isostatic-pressing conditions, enrichment, and reactor conditions. The porosity was segmented, and the three-dimensional distribution of the porosity was extrapolated from the two-dimensional segmentation. The composition was assessed and quantified using energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy areal mapping. The porosity fraction increased as a function of the burnup from 27.77±0.51, 35.12±1.54, and 37.71 ±0.44 % for $8 . \dot { 8 } 6 \times 1 \dot { 0 } ^ { 2 1 } , 9 . 0 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ , and $9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , respectively. When compared to literature, the porosity volume fraction plateaus at burnups greater than $6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , while the pore size grows linearly as a function of fission density. The number of large pores increased in number density as a function of burnup, while the smallest pores (<0.3 µm) increased up to $\dot { 9 . 0 5 } \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , followed by a decrease at $9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 . The delamination and cracking in the fuel plate propagated through an interconnected porosity sublayer identified \~5 µm from the diffusion barrier. The local swelling of the specimens was within or near the prediction bounds of the Robinson-Williams model for local swelling. The fission products, strontium,

barium, cerium, and cesium, precipitated into the pores, while neodymium accumulated adjacent to the pores. These findings have direct implications for the development of fuel performance codes and the accurate documentation of the microstructure evolution in high burnup U-Mo, thus enhancing the safety and efficiency of nuclear fuel usage.

## 1. Introduction

## 1.1. Uranium-10wt.% molybdenum (U-10wt.%Mo) monolithic plate behavior

Low enrichment (<20 at. $\% \ ^ { 2 3 5 } \mathrm { U } )$ uranium-molybdenum (U-Mo) alloys are considered a candidate for the conversion of highperformance research and test reactors to a low-enrichment fuel alternative due to the higher U-loading of the metallic alloy allowing for similar in-reactor performance [1–6]. The proposed United States fuel design consists of a monolithic fuel foil of alloyed U-10wt.%Mo [1,3,6]. The addition of Mo allows the metastable γ-U body-centered cubic phase to be quenched in and retained, enabling desired isotropic swelling as compared to the orthorhombic α-U phase [5]. The alloy is cold rolled to the desired thickness of \~250 µm and then co-hot-rolled with a zirconium (Zr) barrier of \~25 µm [3]. The Zr barrier mitigates the Al-fuel interaction region that develops porosity and deteriorates during irradiation, leading to delamination or pillowing [3]. Finally, the fuel, the Zr barrier, and the aluminum (Al) 6061 cladding assembly are hot isostatically pressed (HIPed) before insertion in the reactor to bond the cladding to the fuel [3].

The HIP bonding during the fuel assembly causes an interaction between the U-Mo fuel and the Zr-diffusion barrier. The resulting interaction microstructure is multi-layered, consisting of the Zr diffusion barrier followed by layers of U- $- \mathrm { Z r } _ { 2 } ,$ γ-U stabilized by $\mathrm { Z r , }$ a thin δ-Zr layer, and a low-Mo content sublayer with α-U and Mo2Zr precipitates at the interface [7,8]. During irradiation, the multi-layered structure dissipates, with only the $\mathrm { U } { - } \mathrm { Z r } _ { 2 }$ and low-Mo content sublayer visible up to 5.2 $\times \ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fission/cm3 . However, the $\mathrm { U } { - } \mathrm { Z r } _ { 2 }$ sublayer at $9 . 8 \ \times \ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 was observed to be discontinuous with breaks caused by Zr penetrating into the fuel [9]. At a high burnup of $9 . 8 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , the Mo-depleted zone was not reported using scanning electron microscopy-energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (SEM-EDS) [9]; however, it has been recently characterized using synchrotron micro-computed tomography (S-µCT) [10].

The as-fabricated U-Mo fuel microstructure has an average grain size of \~10 µm [11,12]. During irradiation, the fuel swells from the generation of solid and gaseous fission products [13]. The generation of fission gas, primarily xenon (Xe), is of concern since it results in bubbles and gas nucleated porosity, which can increase the swelling rate, lower the mechanical stability of the fuel, and cause cracks and delamination between the fuel and cladding [14–17]. Initially, the fission gas in the U-Mo fuels is contained in a nano-bubble superlattice within the γ-U-Mo phase [18–21]. After reaching a saturation limit, the nano-bubble superlattice collapses, coinciding with irradiation-induced recrystallization of the fuel microstructure, a phenomenon termed irradiation-induced grain refinement [22]. This results in a nano-grained structure with grain sizes of \~200–500 nm with pores that can exceed 500 nm [23–26]. Currently, no systematic studies have investigated the progression of irradiation-induced recrystallization in monolithic U-10wt.%Mo. However, a systematic study of a similar fuel in dispersion form consisting of U-7wt.%Mo, has indicated that irradiation-induced recrystallization progresses between $\sim 3 \ – 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 [22,27,28], which is consistent with the completion of monolithic fuel recrystallization measurements [29,30]. The increase in the grain boundary area and a reduction of the diffusion path length for point defects are postulated to cause an increase in porosity nucleation and growth resulting from both fission gas and vacancy accumulation at the grain boundaries [22,27,31]. The current study investigates the effect of the fully recrystallized microstructure, or high burnup structure, on the porosity, swelling, and composition of the monolithic fuel, since limited data exists within this regime.

## 1.2. Monolithic fuel swelling model limitations

Due to the irradiation-induced recrystallization of the microstructure and the change in the porosity behavior, current mechanistic swelling models suggest that the fuel undergoes a two-part primary swelling behavior: an initial linear swelling rate, followed by an increased second-order swelling rate caused by the accumulation and growth of porosity resulting from the increase in grain boundary area due to irradiation-induced recrystallization [13,22,27]. However, recent fuel plate measurements show a discrepancy between the older semi-mechanistic models and the more modern profilometry results at high burnup [32], as well as variations in local swelling due to location-specific behavior [32,33]. Up to this point in time, modeling efforts dedicated to the recrystallized porosity evolution models [34] have relied on limited data which encompassed dispersion fuels only and with limited fission densities irradiated up to $5 . 7 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 [13], which is near the completion of irradiation-induced recrystallization [22,27,28]. Therefore, the implications of the recrystallized microstructure on the porosity evolution in monolithic fuel at high burnups are not fully realized. To better understand the porosity evolution of the post-recrystallized microstructure and its impact on swelling for monolithic fuels, the current study examines an irradiated U-10wt.%Mo monolithic fuel plate to characterize the high-burnup porosity and swelling evolution as a function of fission density to aid in the current modeling efforts.

## 1.3. Monolithic fuel experimental limitations

To the best of the authors’ knowledge, a systematic study has not investigated the porosity evolution in fully recrystallized (high burnup) U-10wt.%Mo monolithic fuels. Several porosity fraction data points were obtained from the AFIP-6-MkII experiment above the recrystallization regime [35], however this fuel plate had a fabrication flaw, specifically an uneven distribution of Mo in the fuel plate [6], resulting in uneven fission density and thermal conductivity. The uneven distribution of Mo content and the atypical spatial heating of the fuel plate during operation lead to increased phase decomposition and uncertainty in the local fission density based on the MCNP fission density modeling [35]. Therefore, porosity fraction measurements from the AFIP-6-MkII experiment are considered suspect. The behavior of pore evolution in high burn-up U-7wt.%Mo dispersion fuels has been experimentally analyzed. However, chemical differences between the dispersion coatings, matrix-fuel interactions, and other plate-specific manufacturing and irradiation parameters add complexity to the dispersion fuel porosity measurements that are irrelevant to monolithic fuels [36–38], and therefore not directly comparable.

The current study eliminates the plate-specific parametric influences, including temperature, time in reactor, fuel plate orientation, and power density, on pore evolution by isolating experimental parameters via selecting a single fuel plate that experienced a wide range of fission densities. In general, fuel plates also experience different creep behaviors at the fuel ends and the centerline location of the fuel [33]. Therefore, the current study also isolates the location-specific impact on porosity and solely captures the influence of fission density on pore evolution. Three locations of a high-burnup fuel plate with varying local fission densities were selected by removing focused ion beam (FIB)-cuboid specimens. Although previous researchers published a report on porosity evolution up to $3 . 7 4 \textrm { - } 6 . 2 3 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fiss $\scriptstyle { \overline { { \cos / \cos } } } ^ { - }$ in monolithic fuels, which encompasses the recrystallization regime, the microstructural implications $( \mathrm { i . e . } ,$ , grain size and porosity) of a fully recrystallized high burnup fuel were not captured [35]. The current study assesses the progression of the porosity fraction, pore morphology, pore size distribution, swelling, and fuel composition of a fully recrystallized monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuel as a function of burnup to characterize the high-burnup effects on pore evolution and chemical composition where little to no data exists.

## 2. Materials and methods

## 2.1. Specimen description, irradiation history and local fission density

The specimens used in the current study were obtained from a neutron-irradiated monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuel plate (ID# L1P7A0). The fuel was enriched to $7 0 \% ^ { 2 3 5 } \mathrm { U }$ and irradiated face-on in three cycles for a total of 137.2 days in the Advanced Test Reactor (ATR) at Idaho National Laboratory (INL) as part of the Reduced Enrichment for Research and Test Reactors experiment number 12 (RERTR-12), experiencing an average burnup of $7 . 7 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 . Further irradiation history and manufacturing specifications are found in references [30,39,40]. The fuel completely recrystallized during irradiation [30], removing the grain-specific influence on the measured porosity due to the selection of the lift-out location. Grain-specific influence, such as intragranular nucleation of porosity in contrast to intergranular nucleation and grain boundary sink behavior, is of concern when directly comparing between samples since the unirradiated grains, which measure \~10 µm in diameter [11,12], are close in size to the FIB-cuboid lift-outs obtained for this study. However, the recrystallized grains are \~200–500 nm in size $[ 2 , 4 , 1 8 , 1 9 , 2 2 , 2 4 - 2 6 , 2 8 ] ;$ ; therefore, the specific grain impact is negligible due to the large number of grains contained within the FIB-cuboid specimens.

Three FIB-cuboid specimens $( \sim 7 5 \times { \sim } 7 5 \times { \sim } 7 5 \mu \mathrm { m } )$ were removed from the fuel plate cross section using a shielded Helios G4 plasma-FIB (P-FIB). Stitched optical micrographs encompassing the entire fuel plate are shown in Fig. 1(a) along with a colorimetric overlay of the fission density. Locations for the specimen selection were chosen at the fuel plate edges, as the most significant fission gradients are expected here from the face-on irradiation direction, giving the largest differences in fission density for the specimens, while maintaining similar reactor conditions. The regional plate selection was utilized to directly compare the fission density and the sample conditions while isolating as many physical parameters as experimentally possible, including creep behavior, temperature, local composition, and diffusion barrier thickness. The three lift-out locations are indicated by the red squares in the optical micrograph of the fuel plate section shown in Fig. 1(a). The respective backscattered electron (BSE)-SEM overview images of the FIB-cuboid are given in Fig. 1(b-d) with fission densities of $8 . 8 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ , $9 . 0 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ 1, and $9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , respectively.

The local fission density of the FIB-cuboid lift-out locations was determined with $^ { 1 3 7 } \mathrm { C s }$ measurements from a precision gamma scanner (PGS) and Monte Carlo N-Particle simulations of the plate [30,40]. Due to the relatively long half-life of $^ { 1 3 7 } \mathrm { C } s$ and its relative lack of thermal and mechanical transport, it is used to estimate the local fission density trends of the fuel [30]. The vertical PGS scan of the fuel plate was scaled by the MCNP calculated average fission density of the entire plate of $7 . 7 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , then used to determine the average fission density of the plate section based on the sectioning location of \~12.7 mm from the cladding edge. Since this location is between two points in the $\mathrm { P G S } \ ^ { 1 3 7 } \mathrm { C s }$ grid [30,40], interpolation was used, giving an average fission density of $8 . 2 7 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 for the fuel section. The horizontal PGS $^ { 1 3 7 } \mathrm { C s }$ scan was then scaled to the fission density using the average fission density previously calculated and then interpolated to create a colorimetric map of fission density across the fuel plate section. The fission density map showed a peak fission density of $9 . 3 9 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions $/ { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { 3 } .$ . The locations where the FIB-cuboids were extracted were then compared to the colorimetric map, indicating the local fission density, as shown in Fig. 1(a), giving. $8 . 8 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 } , 9 . 0 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ , and 9.36 $\times 1 0 ^ { 2 \bar { 1 } }$ fissions/cm3 , as shown in Fig. 1(b-d), respectively. The highest burnup specimen was taken near the constrained region of the fuel plate.

## 2.2. Specimen preparation, image capture, and image processing

Each of the three fuel-containing faces of the FIB-cuboid was prepared using a Helios G4 P-FIB-SEM and characterized with BSE imaging and EDS. The BSE-SEM images were taken using an Everhart-Thorney detector at the highest possible magnification while still having the complete specimen in the field-of-view, resulting in a pixel pitch of 0.0337 $\operatorname { o r } \ 0 . 0 4 5 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , depending on the size of the face image. Despite the differences in SEM parameters, no difference in image quality or resolvability of features was observed. The difference in the imaging parameters was conducted to maintain a significant signal-to-noise ratio and to account for changes in the degree of curtaining on the specimen surfaces. As a result of the P-FIB fabrication and the substantial number of pores in the specimens, the surfaces contained notable curtains. A plug-and-play de-curtaining and denoising algorithm [41,42] was used to remove the physical artifacts and reduce the Poisson noise in the image to allow for accurate microstructural characterization.

## 2.3. Microstructural characterization

The Zr diffusion barrier image contrast overlapped with the image contrast of the porosity, which could cause errors in the measurement of the porosity. Therefore, the Zr diffusion barrier was masked using the EDS intensity map to allow the fuel to be segmented independently. Details on the algorithm used to overlay the EDS intensity map with the BSE image are provided in Appendix A.

The porosity exhibited a wide range of contrast due to the differing depths of the pores. Because of this behavior, standard thresholding or auto-local thresholding caused errors in the segmentation of the microstructure. The segmentation errors primarily result from shallow pores or pores with an internal structure, such as precipitates or interconnectedness, that creates uneven depth. The shallowness of the porosity causes the segmentation to identify the higher contrast regions of the internal features are part of the matrix, leading to pore segmentations that are not fully enclosed. Therefore, a multi-step segmentation procedure was implemented to alleviate this. The procedure and methodology of the multi-step algorithm are detailed in Appendix B.

The ASTM E562 systematic point counting method [43] with an area per point of $1 0 \mu \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ was carried out on a representative face of U-10wt.% Mo specimens with an average burnup of $\phantom { 0 } { 8 . 8 6 } \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ and $9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions $/ \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ to verify the precision of the aforementioned segmentation process to measure the porosity fraction. After segmentation, the volume fraction $\mathrm { ( V _ { v } ) }$ of the porosity was calculated by dividing the measured pore area by the total fuel area for each micrograph. The average $\mathrm { V _ { v } }$ of porosity per fission density was determined by calculating the mean of the three fuel-containing faces, and the error was defined using the standard error. To determine the pore size distribution per fission density, the area of each pore was calculated by counting all the pixels that constituted the object (pore). The equivalent diameter of each pore was then calculated using the measured area. The pore area distribution of each sample was calculated using all three faces, and the number of pores per area $\left( \mathrm { N } _ { \mathrm { A } } \right)$ was determined by dividing by the total area of the fuel of each specimen. The $\mathrm { N _ { A } }$ was then converted to the number per volume $\left( \mathrm { N } _ { \mathrm { v } } \right)$ using an automated Schwartz-Saltykov algorithm adapted from [44] with a bin size of 0.15 µm to depict the pore size distribution accurately.

## 2.4. EDS analysis

The EDS spectral maps of the fuel-containing faces of the specimens were obtained with a 512-pixel horizontal width at eight frames per scan using 20 keV beam energy with an Octane Elite SiN 25 mm2 detector with an energy resolution of Mn Kα of 131.04 eV, azimuthal angle of 29◦, and a lifetime of 660 s. The summed spectral image signal for the fuel section, corresponding spectral maps, and specimen knowledge were used to identify the fuel elements. Each elemental map was calculated from the integrated intensity of the Lα or Mα line using two times the full-width at half maximum (FWHM) of the X-ray line using the Python module Hyperspy [45].

The average composition of each sample was calculated using areal maps of each fuel face in EDAX TEAM using standardless quantification [46,47]. The quantification method used was the peaks background standardless method with atomic number correction, absorption correction, and fluorescence correction or PeBaZAF [46]. The PeBaZAF method was selected over the default net counts method, eZAF method, due to the uncertainty of the identity of the fission products. The eZAF method relies on the unity function to identify the composition of the individual components, making it more sensitive to unknown constituents, as compared to the PeBaZAF method [47]. The error in the average composition for each sample was estimated using the average error of each face reported in EDAX TEAM and the statistical error between the fuel faces.

## 3.1. Image processing and characterization

The specimens pictured in Fig. 1(b-d) were re-imaged using BSE-SEM on three faces. Each FIB cuboid shows significant porosity with indications of interconnectivity and heavy curtain artifacts from the FIB milling in both the fuel and the Zr-diffusion barrier. The FIB cuboid irradiated to $8 . 8 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 shows cracking in Fig. 2(a, g) that propagates within a region of higher porosity located \~5 µm from the diffusion barrier and then continuing through the diffusion barrier. The resultant de-curtained and denoised reconstructions of the BSE-SEM images in Fig. 2 are shown in Fig. 3, indicating that the curtains were successfully removed with minimal impact on the microstructure.

As the Zr-fuel interface is complex, the EDS registration algorithm was used to segment the Zr from the fuel to isolate the porosity for further segmentation. The cracks in the samples were removed to limit the impact on the porosity segmentation due to the size and shape of the feature. The successful multi-step segmentation procedure, consisting of a Phansalkar and edge segmentation, convex overestimation, and then followed by both a Chan-Vese and edge region merging algorithm, as detailed in Appendix B, is visible in Fig. 5(c). Unlike in Fig. 5(b), the pores with complex internal structures are fully encompassed in Fig. 5(c), showing a more accurate representation of the pore boundaries. The effectiveness of the segmentation procedure is shown in Fig. 5(d) indicating the segmentation results overlayed onto the fuel microstructure. Most of the pores in Fig. 5(d) are accurately segmented, however, there are some cases of overestimated porosity. The most evident overestimation is a series of pores bisected by a fine crack, indicated in Fig. 5(a). Due to the crack, these pores were treated as interconnected porosity in the final segmentation as indicated by an ellipse in the segmentation overlay in Fig. 5(d). The instances of overestimation can cause errors in the porosity analysis presented in the current study; however, as these instances are few compared to the quantity of pores analyzed in the current research, their impact is minimal.

## 3.2. Delamination, cracking, and the Zr diffusion barrier

Delamination and cracking are visible from the optical image of the fuel plate in Fig. 1(a), indicated by white arrows. The fuel plate delamination is visible in the center of the fuel cross-section near the diffusion barrier on the right, occurring within a couple of microns of the diffusion barrier inside the fuel. Cracking is observed in the 8.86 and $9 . 0 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 specimens, pictured in Fig. 2(a, b, g) and Fig. 3 $( \mathbf { a } , \mathbf { b } , \mathbf { g } ) ,$ at opposite sides of the fuel plate section.

The large cracks in the $8 . 8 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 specimen, shown in $\operatorname { F i g . 2 } ( { \mathbf { a } } , { \mathbf { g } } ) ;$ that originated near the interface bisected several large pores, suggesting that the crack propagated through the large, interconnected porosity region that exists \~5 µm from the fuel-diffusion barrier interface. The Zr-fuel interface in all three specimens appears irregular, with Zr protruding into the U-Mo fuel, visible in Fig. 2(a-c, gi), suggesting an interaction between the fuel and the Zr diffusion barrier, as previously observed using S-µCT in a very high burnup U-10wt.% Mo monolithic fuel [10]. The Zr penetration is bisected by the large crack in the sample, indicating that the cracking occurred after the Zr penetration formation, and the Zr does not arrest the crack.

## 3.3. Porosity observations and statistics

The porosity of each sample was segmented using the procedure described in Section 2.3 and Appendix B. Fig. 6 shows the segmentations of the porosity displayed in all three faces from Fig. 3, the de-curtained and denoised images. All curtains shown in Fig. 2 were removed successfully with minimal impact on the segmentation of the features in the specimen, including the crack. The large hole pictured in Fig. 1(d), Fig. 2 (f), and Fig. 3(f) was caused by over-milling with the FIB. Meticulous care was taken to mask this region to eliminate the impact on the results, as is visible in the porosity segmentation in Fig. 6. No effect on the pores is evident as the damage is contained within the boundary of the hole in Fig. 2(f).

From the BSE-SEM images in Fig. 3 and the segmentation in Fig. 6, several observations of the porosity trends were made. A larger quantity of small pores (<0.45 µm) with sparse intermediate-sized pores (0.45–1.35 µm) appear near the Zr-diffusion barrier interface. Large pores (1.35–2.25 µm) and interconnected pores (>2.25 µm) begin to appear \~5 µm from the diffusion barrier in all three specimens, shown in Fig. 3 and Fig. 6. The specimen irradiated to $9 . 0 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 has the most extensive interconnected porosity, as seen in Fig. 3(b) and Fig. 6(b). Most interconnected pores are not fully coalesced and retain their original shapes, as indicated by the aspherical nature of the pores in this region, in contrast to the large spherical morphologies expected of fully coalesced, over-pressurized pores [21,52]. This behavior, along with the observation that most of the porosity appears aspherical, suggests that many of the pores are interconnected and/or distorted by internal fission products. The specimen in Fig. 2(b, e, h) and $\mathrm { F i g . 3 ( b , e , }$ h) is located closest to the delamination region of all the specimens, suggesting a correlation between the increased interconnected porosity and the delamination occurrence.

Fig. 8 shows the 3D porosity size distribution calculated using the Schwartz-Saltykov extrapolation of the two-dimensional pore size distribution of each sample and normalized by the volume analyzed. ## 4. Discussion

## 4.1. Fuel plate delamination and cracking

It can be seen from Fig. 1(a) that the fuel plate underwent onset delamination and cracking. However, a debate exists whether the plate failed, as the cladding did not breach and fission gas was not observed in the coolant [16,30,53,54]. Moreover, the plate showed 98 % swelling and onset delamination [16,30,53,54]. The delamination and cracking contributed to the increased profilometry-measured swelling despite no apparent indications of pillowing, as seen in other failed high burnup plates from the RERTR-12 irradiation campaign that experienced blistering [30]. Finite element analysis (FEA) of the fuel plates that experienced excessive swelling in the RERTR-12 experiments, including L1P7A0, suggests that the excessive swelling of these plates was caused by a combination of mechanical degradation from porosity and shutdown stresses in the reactor [53,54]. The conclusion of the shutdown stress causing the fuel cracking is corroborated by the degradation of bending strength and embrittlement of the fuel due to increasing fission damage and porosity [17].

Observations of the crack in Fig. 1(a) and closer inspections of the crack regions observed in Figs. 2(a, g) and 3(a, g) suggest that the crack traveled through the high porosity sublayer and the Zr-diffusion barrier. However, it is uncertain if the crack originated in the high porosity sublayer of the fuel, albeit the separation is the largest in the high porosity sublayer. Additional SEM observations of separate locations within the same plate support this conclusion [16]. The sublayer of high porosity occurs \~5 µm away from the diffusion barrier in high burnup fuel which is visible in all of the specimens investigated in this study ranging from $8 . 8 6 { - } 9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 . These observations are consistent with the sublayer of interlinked porosity occurring \~5 µm from the Zr-diffusion barrier identified via S-µCT in a similar U-Mo monolithic fuel plate irradiated to $9 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 [10] and FIB-SEM of the same fuel plate [16]. These results suggest that the high porosity sublayer will cause delamination in the fuel and is likely the initiation point in the plate analyzed herein. The high-porosity sublayer phenomenon should be further examined and modeled to understand when the interlinked porosity sublayer forms and where the threshold for delamination exists.

The lack of a pore depletion zone alongside the crack in Fig. 3(a, g) suggests that the cracking occurred during the shutdown of the reactor, as supported by FEA analysis of the fuel plate [53,54]. The current results suggest that the cracking does not influence the measured porosity since the cracking occurred during the final shutdown of the fuel plate and can, therefore, be ignored in the pore-morphology evolution measurements investigated in this study. Since no fission products were observed in the coolant [30,39], the fuel plate in this study did not experience actual failure in the fuel assembly. However, the delamination and cracking of the plate is less desired as it may influence fuel performance depending on when it occurs in the fuel cycle [30], and the impact of the interlinked porosity layer that develops \~5 µm from the Zr diffusion barrier [10] is a precursor for fuel delamination.

## 4.2. Porosity evolution

To assess the accuracy and the trends of the porosity measurements, it is necessary to compare them to similar studies. Fig. 13 shows the porosity fraction for monolithic U-10wt.%Mo as a function of fission density obtained from literature [16,28,30,35,55] compared to the current results. The porosity fraction increases relatively linearly from its initiation until $\sim \dot { 6 } \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , when it begins to saturate or plateau, only showing a slight increase up to the maximum fission density. The plateau is likely due to the porosity reaching full interconnection at around 30–35 % porosity. This is corroborated by synchrotron µCT of very high burnup fuel $( 9 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 ) that showed nearly fully interconnected pore networks [10]. The interconnected porosity alleviates gas-driven pore growth by allowing the gas to diffuse throughout the fuel. The saturation of the fission gas volume fraction has been observed in other fuel systems, such as U-Zr [56,57]. However, unlike U-Zr fuels, the plate-type U-Mo fuel is not designed to have gas escape the fuel via a plenum, and therefore, a maximum limit of porosity is not reached [3,56,57]. The higher pore density also limits vacancy-driven pore growth by minimizing the pathlength distance, mitigating the vacancy mobility impact on porosity growth [52].

There is a gap in the literature between $6 - 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , so the exact porosity saturation point is uncertain. The single plate data from the current study shows that the overall porosity fraction is within the expected envelope of the slower growth rate regime which occurs at fission densities greater than $6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 . Although the highest burnup specimen was taken near the constrained region at the edge of the fuel plate, it did not experience the expected reduction in swelling that was shown by Smith et al. $[ 1 6 , 3 8 ]$ in the constrained region. Therefore, it was concluded that the sample was taken away from the constrained region.

There is a concurrence of the emergence of SEM-visible porosity at ${ \sim } 2 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 and the approximate initiation of irradiationinduced recrystallization at $1 . 5 \ \textrm { - } 2 \ \times \ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 [21,28]. Within this fission density range the microstructure evolves, resulting in the collapse of the nano-bubble superlattice and the increase grain boundaries from the recrystallization, facilitating the emergence of large pores visible by SEM. The saturation or plateau in the porosity in Fig. 13 also coincides with the termination of irradiation-induced recrystallization in dispersion U-7wt.%Mo fuel [21,38]. However, a systematic study has not been conducted for monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuel to identify the complete recrystallization regime, including the effect of fabrication and processing conditions, such as annealing [11,58] and variations in HIP parameters [59], therefore the exact start and end of recrystallization in monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuels are uncertain.

The studies by Smith et al. [16] and Verner et al. [35] shown in Fig. 13 were conducted by measuring different locations of a fuel plate with various fission densities using FIB-SEM or standard SEM porosity segmentation, similar to the current study, and are compared in more detail to the current study. The study by Rice et al. [30] shown in Fig. 13, measured the average porosity of different fuel plates using a point counting method on representative areas from optical micrographs, and therefore, is less comparable to the methodology of the current study.

Verner et al. [35] measured the porosity fraction of a fuel plate from a monolithic fuel irradiated between $4 . \dot { 4 } 5 - 6 . 2 3 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 from the RERTR-12 experiment. The porosity fraction was measured using BSE-SEM and porosity segmentation of select locations of the fuel plate with various fission densities [35]. The porosity was segmented using a Phansalkar local threshold-focused segmentation technique provided in reference [60], and is shown in Fig. 13 as a function of the fission density [35]. The porosity fraction measured by Verner et al. [35] consistently increases as a function of fission density, consistent with the current study. The discrepancy in the overall trends suggests that plate-to-plate variation affects the correlation between fission density and porosity fraction, due to differences in manufacturing, reactor conditions, or measurement parameters. Despite the differences between the three studies, there is general agreement on the porosity results.

Dispersion fuel studies that examined the porosity evolution of highburnup U-7wt.%Mo up to $1 . 4 9 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 2 2 }$ fissions/cm3 [21,36,38,61] concluded that varying the chemistry of the fuel particle-matrix interaction layer has a significant impact on the pore evolution results [21, 36,61]. These assertions are corroborated by porosity evolution models [62], which demonstrate that the chemistry of the interaction layer and matrix substantially affects the porosity evolution by decreasing the thermal conductivity, increasing the local temperature, and lowering the surface tension of the amorphous interaction layer [61]. This facilitates porosity nucleation and growth in dispersion fuels. Molybdenum concentration can also affect the recrystallization kinetics and the phase decomposition of the metastable $\gamma { - } \mathbf { U }$ phase [3,63], possibly impacting the porosity evolution. These assertions are corroborated by porosity evolution models [62], which demonstrate that the chemistry of the interaction layer and matrix substantially affects the porosity evolution by decreasing the thermal conductivity, increasing the local temperature, and the decreasing surface tension of the amorphous interaction layer [61] facilitates porosity nucleation and growth in dispersion fuels. Thus, direct comparisons between dispersion and monolithic fuels are difficult because of differences in fuel designs and chemical interactions. Therefore, they are not directly comparable to the results from the current study. However, a similarity exists within the plateauing porosity growth rate, observed above $\mathbf { \dot { 8 } } \times \mathbf { 1 0 ^ { 2 1 } }$ fissions/cm3 in dispersion fuels at \~35–40 % porosity [38], and corroborates the rate stabilization above $6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 observed in the current study, as

illustrated in Fig. 13.

## 4.3. Pore size evolution

To further understand the effect of irradiation on porosity, it is necessary to understand the evolution of individual pores as a function of fission density, which is shown in Fig. 14 as a compilation of previous works $[ 1 6 , 3 5 , 5 5 ]$ and the current study. Few studies have investigated pore size as a function of fission density for monolithic U-10wt %Mo. Due to the limited data and the broad differences in results from each study visible in Fig. 14, the available monolithic pore size data do not establish a consistent trend between studies. The pore size measurements in the current study are significantly lower than those from Verner et al. [35] and Smith et al. [16] despite the higher fission density. The lower overall size observed in the current study is due to the emergence of bimodal porosity that exhibited a first mode at 0.23 µm and a second mode between 0.98–1.13 µm, as indicated in Fig. 8. The lower porosity mode is an order of magnitude more numerous than the second mode, resulting in a smaller average size. The lower mode is also near the resolution of the current study. Resolution, imaging parameters of the SEM images, and segmentation routines used to acquire the data shown in Fig. 14 have also been documented to impact the resolvability of the porosity [64], and can similarly affect these results.

The pore distribution in Fig. 8 and the segmentations in Fig. 6 show that the quantity of large pores (>1.5 µm) increases consistently as a function of burnup, while the number of small pores varies. The bimodal porosity distribution observed in this study is corroborated by FIB-SEM analysis by both Jue et al. [9] and Smith et al. [16] at similar burnups. However, this is the first documentation of the bimodal progression as a function of fission density in high burnup monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuel. The bimodal porosity suggests a more complex pore size evolution behavior than that observed at lower burnups [35] and pore evolution modeling in post-recrystallized U-Mo [34].

The pore behavior in the current study indicates some discrepancies within the current models for recrystallized U-Mo pore evolution [34]. The gaseous swelling growth rate and average pore size growth rate were higher than initially predicted by Hu et al. [34]. Linearly extrapolating the cluster dynamics and phase-field models by Hu et al. [34], resulted in an underprediction of 17.44 %, 37.98 %, and 45.63 % gaseous swelling at $8 . 8 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 } , 9 . 0 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ , and $9 . 3 6 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , respectively. Similarly, the average pore size increased faster than the model predicted, showing an initial difference of 259.6, 203.96, and 285.4 nm in average size for $\mathbf { \widetilde { 8 . 8 6 } \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 } } , 9 . 0 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ , and 9.36 × $1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , respectively. The discrepancy in the model and the measured porosity from this study arise from the model using porosity data from onset and early completion of recrystallization in dispersion fuels ranging from U-6–10wt.%Mo $( 2 . 5 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 } ~ { \cdot } ~ 5 . 7 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 ) by Kim et al. [13] to fit the model constants due to the limited availability of high burnup porosity data [34]. During irradiation-induced recrystallization, the initial nano-grained low-angle grain boundaries (LAGB) continually rotate to high-angle grain boundaries (HAGB) as the recrystallization progresses, forming 85 % HAGB by $4 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 [28]. If this trend persists, the larger quantity of HAGBs will increase both the porosity growth rate and nucleation rate due to increased nucleation sites and lower diffusion path lengths. Therefore, Kim et al. [13] would not have captured this increase in porosity evolution during the initial recrystallization regime. The data from the current study will aid in developing more precise values to enable the prediction of the pore evolution of high-burnup fuel.

Other predicted behaviors from the Hu model [34], such as the loss in Xe/vacancy fraction in pores as a function of fission density, cannot be verified directly in this study. However, the observed pores appear to be mostly aspherical, and the interconnected pores persist, rather than coalescing into large sphere-like pores. The precipitation of solid fission products can also cause similar distortions to the pore shape, and further work needs to be conducted to understand the effect of interconnection and precipitates on the pore shape.

## 4.4. Fuel swelling

The Kim-Hofman U-Mo fuel swelling model has been used to predict gaseous and total (gaseous and solid fission products) swelling in U-Mobased fuels for nearly a decade in the United States [13]. The model was derived from post-irradiation thickness measurements from monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuel plates obtained from the RERTR 6–9 experiments (irradiated from $2 . 6 \textrm { -- } 6 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 ). The thickness measurements from the monolithic fuel plate data were then compared to the local porosity measured from the RERTR 1–5 dispersion U-6–10wt.% Mo fuel plates (irradiated from $2 . 0 \mathrm { ~ - ~ } 5 . 7 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 ) [13]. These two different fuel types were finally used to generate a gaseous, solid, and total swelling model for U-Mo plate-based fuels. The Kim-Hofman model suggested a two-part swelling behavior in which a lower linear swelling rate occurred between $0 - 3 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , followed by a higher second-order swelling rate above $3 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , where SEM-visible porosity and irradiation-induced recrystallization emerge [13], as shown in Fig. 15. However, a more recent swelling model by Robinson-Williams et al. [32] was developed solely for monolithic U-10wt.% Mo fuel plates with a Zr-diffusion barrier (RERTR 9b-12 and AFIP 4, 6, and 7 irradiated from $0 . 2 5 - 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ $\mathrm { f i s s i o n s } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 } )$ using total and local post-irradiation profilometry measurements. The Robinson-Williams model suggests that the swelling can be statistically predicted using a single second-order polynomial with one set of prediction bounds for total swelling and a second broader prediction bounds for local swelling measurements [32], as shown in Fig. 15. The modern profilometry measurements of high burnup $( > 6 \times$ $1 0 ^ { \overline { { 2 1 } } }$ fissions/cm3 ) plates [32] show higher swelling rates than the classical Kim-Hofman model [13]. Despite the accuracy of the Robinson-Williams model [32] in bounding the total swelling and local swelling data, it does not distinguish between solid and gaseous swelling like the previous models, such as the classical Kim-Hofman model [13]. Therefore, it is necessary to understand the discrepancy at high burnup between the empirical Robinson-Williams model [32] and the classical, semi-mechanistic Kim-Hofman model [13].

Using the Kim-Hofman model for the solid fission product correlation [13], the total swelling of each FIB-cuboid was estimated. The estimated total swelling was compared to both the Kim-Hofman gaseous swelling [13] and the Robinson-Williams [32] models extrapolated to the burnup experienced by the FIB-cuboids studied herein, as shown in Fig. 15. The lowest burnup specimen is within the total swelling bands of the Robinson-Williams model. In contrast, the higher burnup specimen is above the upper local swelling band by an average of $1 1 . 7 3 \pm 3 . 0 0$ %. Moreover, all three samples exceed the 53–56 % total swelling predicted by the Kim-Hofman model [13] by an average of $4 5 . 6 5 \pm 8 . 1 1$ % total swelling. The current results suggest that the discrepancy between the Kim-Hofman [13] and the Robinson-Williams models [32] is the contribution of higher gaseous swelling at high burnups, which was underpredicted by the Kim-Hofman model [13]. In contrast, the small discrepancies between the current specimens and the Robinson-Williams model are attributed to the extrapolation of the model beyond its intended limits to the fission densities experienced in this study between $8 . 8 6 - 9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 . Another possible discrepancy can be attributed to sampling from the highest burnup locations in the fuel plate, which would experience local deviations from the mean plate behavior modeled by Robinson-Williams. More detailed discussions detailing the differences between local sampling and bulk fuel behavior are found in [10,65]. The methodology of the current study, which used the Kim-Hofman model [13] for the solid fission product correlation and the SEM-measured porosity, rather than direct measurements of the fuel plate due to the substantial delamination, likely led to discrepancies between the model and the measured data.

An additional discrepancy of the Kim-Hofman model [13] is the limited access to porosity data, up to $5 . 7 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , representative of the early completion stages of the recrystallized microstructure [22,27,28]. Therefore, the model does not capture the long-term implications of the nano-grained structure on the gaseous swelling. Another possible explanation for the difference in gaseous swelling observed in this study and the Kim-Hofman [13] model is the resolution difference between studies. Kim et al. [13] had a resolution of 0.1 µm, while the current study had a resolution up to 0.037 µm. Among the current samples, 2338.7 ± 548.6 pores were measured below the resolution limit of Kim et al. [13], contributing to $0 . 0 0 2 7 \pm 0 . 0 0 0 3$ of the porosity $\mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { v } } .$ Therefore, the amount of porosity below the resolution limit of Kim et al. [13] did not significantly contribute to the gaseous swelling in the current study, but substantially impacted the average size.

## 4.5. Fuel constituents and fission product evolution

The expected reduction in U from fission was estimated and compared to the measured reduction using EDS. The anticipated decrease in U was estimated by calculating the average density and molar mass of the fuel from the individual elements identified via EDS of the FIB-cuboid irradiated to $8 . 8 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 as a starting point. The average density and molar mass of the fuel were estimated to be $1 3 . 7 1 \ \mathrm { g / c m ^ { 3 } }$ and 190.05 g/mol, respectively. The method was then used to calculate the atomic density using the at. % U at $8 . 8 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/ $\mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 } { \mathrm { . } }$ , estimated to be $2 . 7 3 \times \dot { 1 } 0 ^ { 2 2 }$ U $\mathsf { a t o m s } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ of the $4 . 3 4 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 2 }$ total atoms/cm3 . Using the assumption that one U atom is lost per fission and two fission product atoms are gained per fission; the expected reduction in at. % U was calculated for each higher burnup, assuming a negligible volume gain over $< 3$ % change in the at. % U with increasing fission density. The predicted composition of U was 62.10 at. % and 60.96 at. % for $9 . 0 5 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ and $9 . 3 6 ~ \times ~ 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , respectively. These composition estimations fall within error of the measured values of $6 1 . 8 3 \pm 0 . 6 1$ at. % U and $6 0 . 0 8 \pm 0 . 9 9$ at. % U for the samples at 9.05 × $1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ and $9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , suggesting confidence in the calculations using the standardless EDS measurement.

When extrapolating this method up to $9 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fis $\mathrm { s i o n s } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ to correlate with the results by Gan et al. [66] using TEM-EDS, an estimated value of 59.33 at. % U fell within the measured bulk U concentration error of $5 9 . 9 2 \pm 1 . 3 8$ at. % U. The results showed a consistent drop in U concentration as a function of burnup regardless of the sample or the study. The consistent U loss indicates that there is no individual fission product or fuel constituent redistribution between the foil end and the peak thickness region. This agrees with the work by Kim et al. [33] that suggests that the fuel itself redistributes to the peak thickness region. Therefore, differences in U concentration are solely a result of the local fission density.

However, a discrepancy between the literature and the current results was identified in the Mo concentration. The bulk fuel Mo measurements by Gan et al. [66] conducted using TEM-EDS showed a higher concentration, 28.07 ± 0.65 at. % Mo, compared to \~20 at. % Mo in the current study. The discrepancies between Gan et al. [66] and the current study are attributed to the TEM-EDS point scan method focusing on the fuel matrix, while ignoring pores and fission product precipitates. Moreover, TEM-EDS is a more local method compared to SEM-EDS. The current study implemented an SEM-EDS area scan to assess the total fission product accumulation in the material, allowing for multiple fission products to be measured in bulk rather than just Ce in the matrix, as measured by Gan et al. [66]. However, SEM-EDS has a lower resolution than SEM wavelength dispersive spectroscopy (WDS) employed by other studies [9,16], and it can also contribute to discrepancies between studies.

Transmission electron microscopy on high burnup specimens irradiated to $9 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 indicated that Sr and Ba precipitated inside pores, with Nd accumulating near pores [66]. These observations are corroborated by Figs. 10 and 11, suggesting similar precipitation of Sr and Ba, with an accumulation of Nd near select pores. Moreover, Nd is not uniformly distributed, but only spatially located around the largest pores. The accumulation of Nd near pores was also documented by Smith et al. [16] via SEM measurements of the same plate, however Sr precipitation was not found. The current study observed that Ce and Cs also precipitated in the pores as well as in the matrix.

Fig. 9 does not show the post-HIP sublayers of $\mathbf { U } \mathbf { - } \mathbf { Z r } _ { 2 }$ and a Uconcentrated zone at the diffusion barrier fuel interface, suggesting its dissipation at high burnups. Similarly, Jue et al. [9], using SEM-EDS, established that the $\mathbf { U } \mathbf { - } \mathbf { Z r } _ { 2 }$ sublayer is barely visible and discontinuous at $9 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , but that the $\mathrm { U } \mathrm { - } \mathrm { Z r } _ { 2 }$ sublayer was visible at 5.6 $\times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 . The current study suggests that sublayer structure dissolution occurred at a burnup prior to 8.86 $\times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 since it was not observed in any of the specimens investigated, and is consistent with other SEM-EDS and SEM wavelength dispersive spectroscopy studies [9,16]. However, synchrotron absorption contrast µCT observed a U-concentrated region at $9 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 [10]. This discrepancy may be caused by the signal noise of the EDS measurements due to the significant porosity in the electron interaction volume. The U sublayer only had a 4wt.% increase in U at $9 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 , measured via synchrotron absorption contrast µCT [10]. This is within two standard deviations of the measured values within this study, suggesting that the U-concentrated region could easily be overlooked due to signal noise in the EDS measurements from the interference of the porosity in the electron interaction volume locally.

## 5. Conclusions

The current study analyzed three FIB-cuboid specimens of burnups ranging from $8 . 8 6 - 9 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 using SEM to characterize the effect of irradiation on the high burnup microstructure, specifically porosity, swelling, and composition. The FIB-cuboid samples were sourced from three different locations on a high-burnup neutron-irradiated monolithic U-10wt.%Mo fuel plate with an average burnup of $7 . 7 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 }$ fissions/cm3 (\~99 % LEU burnup) that had experienced cracking during reactor shutdown. The local fission density of each location was calculated using the average fission density and the interpolated PGS $^ { 1 3 7 } \mathrm { C s }$ scan for the irradiated fuel plate. The FIB-induced curtains were removed using a plug-and-play de-curtaining and denoising algorithm [41,42]. The fission-induced porosity was segmented using a model-based image technique developed in the current study for the BSE images.

Observations of the fuel in the current study showed that the cracking and delamination that the fuel plate experienced during reactor shutdown was propagated along the highly interlinked porosity region ${ \sim } 5$ µm from the Zr-diffusion barrier, correlating well with the interlinked porosity region observed by S-µCT of a higher fission density (9.8 $\times \ 1 0 ^ { 2 \bar { 1 } }$ fissions/cm3 ) fuel plate [10]. Understanding this interlinked porosity phenomenon and its onset is critical to understanding the cracking mechanism observed in this fuel plate, and predicting potential fuel failures.

The macroscopic porosity results correlate with the local fission density rather than spatial locations within the fuel. The porosity fell within the bounds of the plateaued region of the available literature

## Appendix B. Multi-step porosity segmentation procedure

To segment the porosity in the BSE image a multi-step segmentation procedure was developed. Fig. B1(a) shows a representative microstructure that was inputted to the segmentation algorithm, with Fig. B1 (b-d) showing individual steps of the segmentation algorithm. First, a Phansalkar local autothresholding algorithm [48] was used to estimate the pore boundaries using a local radius of 20 and 100 pixels to capture both small and extensive figure features, as shown in Fig. B1(b). The initial segmentation was then paired with a Canny edge segmentation [49]. The boundaries of the initial segmentation were then overestimated with a convex operator, shown in Fig. B1(c). Finally, the convex overestimation was used as a starting point for both Chan-Vese iterative region merging and edge-based region merging algorithms, which were summed to create the final segmentation in Fig. B1(d).

This methodology was successful since the Chan-Vese iterative region merging algorithm is designed to iteratively locate feature boundaries by matching the local average and standard deviation contrast of the pore to the non-local average and standard deviation contrast of all the segmented pores [50]. In contrast, the edge-based iterative region merging algorithm starts at a boundary position and then shrinks that boundary until the edge is found by minimizing an energy function [51]. As shine-through artifacts can create intensities equaling or exceeding that of the matrix, the Canny edge input and the edge-based region merging algorithm are able to capture these pores. Similar iterative two-avenue approaches [68–71] have demonstrated successful segmentation of highly porous media without extensive filtering, as was previously required [36,60].

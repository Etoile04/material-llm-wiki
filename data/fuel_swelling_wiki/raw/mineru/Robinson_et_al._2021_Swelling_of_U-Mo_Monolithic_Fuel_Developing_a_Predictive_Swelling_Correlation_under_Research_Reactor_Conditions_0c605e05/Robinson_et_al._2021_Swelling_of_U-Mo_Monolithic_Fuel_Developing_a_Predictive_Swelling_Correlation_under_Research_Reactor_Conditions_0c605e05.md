## a b s t r a c t

Understanding swelling behavior in monolithic, uranium-molybdenum, plate-type fuel is necessary to qualify the fuel for reactor use and for the conversion of high performance research reactors from highly enriched to low-enriched uranium. Multiple mechanisms influence plate dimensional stability, including solid and gaseous fission-product induced swelling, irradiation-assisted creep, fuel-phase transformation, and interaction-layer formation. Separating these phenomena remains a challenge, and current models do not appear to adequately predict experimental results at higher fission densities where it is most critical. To mitigate the mechanistic uncertainty, post-irradiation profilometry is used to increase the number of data points available over a range of in-reactor irradiation experiments conducted at the Advanced Test Reactor, and to provide a statistical precedent for a swelling-behavior model. This work establishes a predictive swelling correlation as a function of fission density, with associated confidence and prediction bounds, by analyzing more than 18,000 thickness data points collected on 74 irradiated U-10Mo monolithic fuel test plates over a range of irradiation conditions.

## 1. Introduction

A monolith fuel design based on uranium-molybdenum (U-Mo) alloy has been selected as the fuel type for conversion of the United States’ high performance research reactors (HPRRs) from highly enriched uranium (HEU) to low-enriched uranium (LEU). In this plate-type fuel design, a thin layer of zirconium is used to eliminate the direct interaction between the U–10wt%Mo (U-10Mo) fuel core and the 6061 aluminum alloy (AA6061) cladding during fabrication and irradiation. In order to be qualified for use in reactors, U-Mo monolithic fuel plates must exhibit swelling in a stable and predictable regime. The key phenomena that can impact the dimensional stability and robustness of a fuel plate during irradiation are fission-product-induced fuel swelling, transformation of radiation-stable phases into unstable phases, radiation-enhanced diffusion between dissimilar materials (resulting in the formation of unstable phases), and fuel relocation resulting from irradiationassisted creep.

## 1.1. General Fuel Swelling Behavior

During irradiation, swelling of nuclear fuel occurs due to an accumulation of gaseous and solid fission products. The swelling behavior of the fuel plate is a defining attribute of the fuel system that governs its suitability for use in a specific nuclear application. Swelling must be stable and predictable, and provide sufficient operating margin, for the range of fuel operating conditions.

As fission-gas production in the fuel increases with increasing fission density, most fuel materials have demonstrated a tendency toward accumulation of the gas into micrometer-scale bubbles that lead to volumetric swelling that escalates with further increases in fission density. Breakaway swelling is a term that has been used historically with reference to dispersion fuels to define a regime of stable fuel behavior followed by a rapid or unpredictable transition to high swelling behavior that is often associated with gas-bubble interconnection or large fission gas bubbles that have accumulated at the interface of the amorphous interaction layer and the aluminum matrix. If the fuel system exhibits breakaway swelling as a function of fission density, then a fission-density threshold that defines the fuel safety limit must be identified during the fuel development effort in order to ensure this region is never reached by the fuel.

At fuel operating temperatures of interest for research reactors, below \~250°C, the dominant swelling mechanism of U-Mo at low to medium fission density is volume expansion by solid fission products caused by the volume difference between uranium and the resulting solid fission products. This type of fuel swelling is only dependent on fission density and is largely independent of temperature, fabrication, irradiation conditions, and the phase of fuel. Based on the estimates of Hofman and Walter [1] as well as Kim and Hofman [2], who evaluated solid fission-product swelling in U-10Mo fuel, swelling should be between 2 and 4% per 1021 fissions/cm3.

The noble fission gases Xe and Kr, being largely insoluble, tend to precipitate into gas bubbles. In general, the magnitude of swelling depends primarily on the gas-bubble morphology that develops during irradiation. In U-Mo, the fission gas precipitates in an energetically stable face centered cubic superlattice of nanosize bubbles that are registered on the underlying body centered cubic U-Mo crystal lattice [3–7]. Gas bubbles are on the order of 2-3 nanometers in diameter, arrayed with a lattice parameter of approximately 11-12 nanometers [3,4,8]. The formed nano-bubble lattice remains stable up to a fission density of approximately 3 × 1021 fissions/cm3. Up to this fission density the swelling rate is low and both solid and gaseous fission products give rise to a linear solid-state swelling.

At a fission density between 2.5 1021 and 3.5 1021 fissions/cm3, gas-driven swelling gradually increases in importance relative to solid fission-product swelling. This transition is attributed to the onset of grain refinement, sometimes referred to as grain subdivision, which occurs as a result of irradiation-induced recrystallization of γ -U. An important impact of grain refinement is that it provides additional grain-boundary surface area on which fission-gas-bubbles nucleate and grow. The fuel recrystallization is accompanied by the collapse of the nano-bubble lattice in the affected regions, whose over pressurized gas inventory is then collected by the inter-granular bubble population developing at the boundaries of the newly refined sub-micron grains [9,10]. The fission density corresponding to the onset of grain refinement, as well as the rate at which grain refinement proceeds with increasing fission density, may significantly impact the total fuel swelling observed at higher fission densities. At the highest achievable fission densities, localized regions of the crystalline γ -(U,Mo) phase can become amorphous, as has been observed using transmission electron microscopy (TEM) [11]. In addition to the primary operating parameters (i.e., temperature and fission rate), many other variables may have an effect on fission-gas behavior. Among these are local variations in microstructure, fuel/cladding interfaces, secondary phases, and impurities.

The top left shows the bubble superlattice at fission densities \~2.0  1021 fissions/cm3, the top right shows the collection of fission-gas bubbles at grain boundaries at fission densities \~3.0 × 1021 fissions/cm3, the bottom left shows micrometer scale fission-gas bubbles at fission densities \~5.5  1021 fissions/cm3, and the bottom right shows larger micrometer scale fission-gas bubbles at fission densities \~7.5  1021 fissions/cm3.

## 1.2. Modeling Swelling Behavior

If fuel were mechanically unconstrained, volumetric swelling would be isotropic. For the plate-type fuels typical in HPRRs, however, significant lateral and longitudinal mechanical constraint is applied to the fuel by the aluminum cladding. This constraint, in concert with fuel relocation that occurs as a result of irradiationassisted creep, results in dimensional changes only in the plate thickness direction [12]. The swelling in aluminum clad plate-type fuel is thus evaluated primarily through examination of changes to plate thickness. ## 1.3. Calculating Fuel Swelling

## 1.3.1. Plate Average Fuel Swelling

Ascertaining the fuel swelling within each fuel plate is the first step to developing a new swelling correlation; however, there are multiple approaches to measure and calculate fuel swelling. One approach is to measure the average swelling of a fuel plate— i.e., its volumetric growth during irradiation. This may be determined through pre- and post-irradiation measurements of immersion density using the Archimedes method to establish the plate volume changes. The volume of the plate can be calculated using Eq. (13), and plate-average fuel swelling may then be calculated using Eq. (14), assuming no growth of the aluminum during irradiation. Immersion density provides bulk (i.e. plate average) U-Mo swelling values because it bypasses Eq. (1) by directly measuring the plate volume change. This has the advantage of eliminating local contributions to plate-thickness changes, such as pre-irradiation foil-thickness variations and fuel relocation, due to creep, instead accounting only for total volumetric growth of the fuel.

Care must be taken when interpreting local dimensional measurements, as local plate-thickness changes also include those changes resulting from mechanisms other than volumetric swelling due to fission products, especially fuel relocation due to irradiation-assisted creep. This is an added result of the plategeometry dimensional constraints that, in addition to limiting all swelling to the plate thickness direction, also prevent the fuel from increasing in thickness at its edge. As the fuel swells, the edge constraint forces the fuel to relocate, via irradiation-assisted creep, to adjacent regions, the result of which is both a fuel-zone region with negligible thickness increase and a region with additional thickness increase and no net volumetric change.

Despite these challenges, it is proposed that utilizing the significant increase in data provided through local fuel swelling calculations will improve resulting models. Further, it is suggested that these challenges may be overcome through statistical methods to maximize the data that can contribute to the calculations. This work establishes a predictive swelling correlation for a monolithictype uranium-molybdenum fuel for use in HPRRs using profilometry data collected over a series of irradiation tests in the Advanced Test Reactor (ATR) at Idaho National Laboratory (INL).

## 2. Materials and Methods

## 2.1. Irradiation Experiments

For this work, only irradiated fuel plates of the HPRR program’s down-selected fuel type [19] were included, comprising a data set acquired from 74 fuel test plates. The fuel design consists of a U-10Mo monolithic fuel core, a Zr diffusion layer metallurgically bonded to the faces of the fuel core by a hot co-rolling process, and AA6061 cladding, bonded using a hot isostatic pressing (HIP) process. A brief introduction to the geometry and irradiation conditions for each experiment is included here.

The AFIP–3 experiment contained one down-selected full-size fuel plate, approximately 570 mm long, 56 mm wide and 1.27 mm thick. Beginning-of-life peak power density was 16,000 W/cm3, and peak fission density was 4.6 × 1021 fissions/cm3 [20].

The AFIP–4 experiment contained six down-selected sub-sized fuel plates approximately 190.5 mm long, 56 mm wide and 1.27 mm thick. Beginning-of-life power densities ranged from 9,000 to 20,000 W/cm3, and fission densities from 2.5 × 1021 to 4.7 × 1021 fissions/cm3 [21].

The RERTR–9 and RERTR–10 experiments contained two and four down-selected mini-plates, respectively. Plates were approximately 101 mm long, 25.4 mm wide, and 1.27 mm thick. Beginning-of-life power densities varied from 12,000 to 36,000 W/cm3 and fission densities from 2.3 to 10.7 1021 fissions/cm3 [22,23].

The RERTR–12 experiment evaluated the fuel design over a wide range of operating conditions, using the same external plate dimensions as RERTR-9 and 10 and three different U-10Mo foil thicknesses: 0.254, 0.508, and 0.635 mm. Beginning-of-life power densities ranged from 2,200 to 40,000 W/cm3, and fission densities ranged from 0.35 × 1021 to 7.9 × 1021 fissions/cm3. Through the use of HEU in order to obtain desired test conditions, fission densities were in excess of those achievable with LEU, or 19.75% enriched fuel. In total, 56 mini-plate specimens were tested in the experiment; however, due to in-reactor pillowing of 4 plates, only 52 plates were used for this analysis [17].

The AFIP–6MkII experiment was designed to evaluate the high power, large-scale performance of monolithic U-Mo fuels. The AFIP–6MkII fuel plate design included a fueled region, 571.5 mm long that was axially centered in the ATR core. The 235U enrichment was approximately twice that of previous AFIP experiments (40% enrichment) in order to yield higher fission powers in the specimens [24]. Beginning-of-life peak power density was 34,000 W/cm3, and peak fission density was 5.1 × 1021 fissions/cm3.

The AFIP–7 experiment [25] was designed to evaluate the performance of monolithic U-Mo fuels in element-assembly geometry with curved fuel plates. The fuel was enriched to 19.75 wt.% 235U. The AFIP–7 plates were formed to a prescribed radius and swaged into a single fuel-element assembly. AFIP–7 plates were the largest irradiated to date: approximately 1016 mm long, 63.5 mm wide, and 1.27 mm thick, with a radius of curvature of 90.35 mm. Irradiation testing was performed at operating conditions similar to ATR driver fuel during normal, non-powered axial locator mechanism (PALM) cycle operation. AFIP–7 beginning-of-life fission power varied from 11,000 to 16,000 W/cm3, and fission densities varied from 2.0 to 3.1 1021 fissions/cm3 over the course of two irradiation cycles [26].

## 2.2. Local Fuel Swelling Determination

## 2.2.1. Calculating Fuel Swelling

Post-irradiation profilometry was performed on each of the 74 fuel plates of the selected design to determine the local fuel-plate thickness. All measurements were performed using calibrated measurement devices, and the local oxide-layer thickness was also measured using eddy current technique. In experiments in which the oxide-layer was statistically thick enough to affect the thickness of the plate measured in profilometry (AFIP-6MkII and AFIP-7), the contribution of the oxide-layer was subtracted to correct the total post-irradiation thickness to just that of the plate. This contribution is a fraction of the total oxide layer thickness, due to the aluminum surface consumed in its formation, and is determined based on the ratio of the density of the oxide formed (3.04 g/cm3) to the density of aluminum (2.7 g/cm3). Because there are 27 grams of Al per mole of AlO(OH) and 60 grams of AlO(OH) per mole of AlO(OH), there are (27/60) × 3.04 grams of Al/cm3 of AlO(OH) or 1.37 g Al/cm3 AlO(OH). This results in a volumetric increase of 97.5% [27]; therefore, multiplying the measured oxide thickness by 1/1.975 corrects for this volumetric increase.

The uncertainty of the calculated local fuel-swelling values using the above approach was investigated. Each term in the swelling equation has measurement uncertainty based on the measurement method, as well as location uncertainty. It was determined that the uncertainty of the swelling was dominated by the pre-irradiation foil-thickness term due to the small number of measurement data points and less-precise measurement reporting. Because the uncertainty varied significantly, not only from experiment to experiment, but also location to location within an experiment, this individual point uncertainty analysis was eventually abandoned in favor of using statistical methods for analyzing the resulting data sets, as explained below.

In order to correlate local fuel swelling with local fission density, local fission-density values for each of the 74 plates were estimated by calculating the local-to-average ratio (L2AR) fission-rate gradients using Monte Carlo N-Particle (MCNP) code, which could then be scaled with the average calculated fission densities.

Due to differences in the locations of the measurement sets and measurement densities, as well as fixed physics calculation grids, data sets were either interpolated or downsized as appropriate onto a common data grid. For all plates analyzed, the preirradiation thickness data (both plate and foil thicknesses) were the sparsest of the data sets. For RERTR-9, 10, and 12 and AFIP-4 plates, average pre-irradiation plate and fuel-foil thicknesses were used for the swelling calculations. Due to the small plate and foil sizes for these experiments, combined with very little variation in thickness prior to irradiation, this was a valid simplification. For larger-sized plates (AFIP-3BZ, AFIP-6MkII, and AFIP-7), pre-irradiation data sets were interpolated to create a grid size that matched the calculated fission-density grid. For AFIP-4 plates, the calculated fission-density grid was downsized to match the postirradiation thickness grid.

The resulting local fuel-thickness increases were then plotted against the calculated local fission density, as shown in Fig. 4. The data are grouped by experiment for visualization. The number of data points in each experiment data set is provided in the legend for reference; the totals include all measurements from each individual plate within that experiment. An important point to be made regarding the significant amount of apparent scatter in the data, shown in Fig. 4, is that some local fuel-thickness-change values observed at any given fission density may be considerably higher or lower than the underlying trend expected, based on accepted fuel swelling correlations. This is primarily as a result of fuel relocation due to irradiation-assisted creep and constrained regions at the edges of the plates.

## 2.2.2. Binning Data

In order to account for the uncertainties in variations in local fuel-thickness changes as well as uncertainties in the local calculated fission densities, which contribute to the scatter observed in the data, calculated fuel-thickness increases were averaged across experiment datasets within discrete steps, or “bins.” These bins for averaging data may be defined by fixed steps in fission density, such as every 0.1 × 1021 (fissions/cm3), allowing each data point within a given fission density range to contribute to the bin’s average value. Alternatively, bins may be defined by the number of data points averaged, such as averaging the closest 100 fission density and fuel thickness change data pairs, to create bins having equal weights. Both binning examples are shown in Fig. 5, and the resulting binned data are suggested as an effective approach for mitigating the inherent sources of uncertainty described above and allowing the underlying fuel-swelling correlation to be established from the experimental data.

Statistical analysis was performed to ensure that the choice of binning methods did not affect the results, comparing four methods: binning every 0.1  1021 (fissions/cm3), 0.25  1021 (fissions/cm3), 0.5 × 1021 (fissions/cm3), and 100 nearest data points. Linear regression was performed on the data sets resulting from each of the four different binning methods, generating prediction models and 95% confidence bounds. Fig. 6 shows the linear-regression models have overlapping confidence bounds, indicating the different binning methods do not statistically affect the trend models. Based on these results, the method utilizing 100 data points per bin was selected. This method has the benefit that each binned data point has the same statistical significance and eliminates the need for weighting the individual bins based on the number of raw data points within each bin. This binned data were used to evaluate different fitting methods and, ultimately, to define the predictive swelling correlation.

## 4. Discussion

## 4.1. Model Fit Comparison

As discussed previously, the solid fission product-dominated swelling that occurs at low fission densities is known to increase linearly with increasing fission density. There is a subsequent increase in the swelling rate as a function of fission density, typically observed at intermediate fission densities. This is due to irradiation-induced recrystallization and the associated transition from swelling behavior dominated by solid to that dominated by gaseous fission-products, which typically results in a non-linear swelling dependence on fission density when higher fission densities are reached.

Considering the complicated physical phenomena with multiple mechanisms involved and the observed dependence of swelling on fission density over the entire range of interest, swelling correlations for use in fuel performance analyses are frequently developed using empirical relationships that best fit the experimental data. This approach was employed here to develop a useful swelling correlation. In this case, various empirical correlations were evaluated by analyzing the quality of the fit to the binned data set with 100 data points per bin. Examining Fig. 7, the piecewise linear, quadratic, and cubic functions each appear to adequately represent the data, with R-squared values exceeding 96%, indicating that the fits are high quality. Additionally, the uncertainty bounds for the three model forms (not displayed) are overlapping, suggesting any of the models will adequately represent swelling.

![](images/dbd0fdcc8cf01892ec8a789a2b333090f9abcab64209353071340ef31a4553b9.jpg)  
Fig. 10. Recommended U-Mo fuel swelling model (Equation [17]) along with 95% prediction bounds for the raw data fit (Equations [20] and [21]) overlaid with the raw data prior to binning.

The piecewise linear function was used with a variable inflection point, optimized to best fit the data, in an attempt to capture the change in swelling rate at intermediate fission densities associated with the transition from solid to gaseous fission-productdominated swelling behavior. Unsurprisingly, this function has the highest R-squared value; however, the location of the transition point is highly dependent on the data located in the critical recrystallization range of fission densities, between 3.0  1021 and 4.5 × 1021 fissions/cm3. This is apparent in Fig. 8, where excluding the AFIP-6MkII experiment from the binned data results in the inflection point shifting from a fission density of \~3.3 × 1021 to \~4.5 × 1021 fissions/cm3. This data set appears possibly to have higher swelling values than the data obtained from other irradiation tests in that fission-density range. A number of hypotheses are being investigated to explain this apparent difference in swelling behavior. One possible explanation is that this higher swelling is due to the earlier onset of recrystallization and subsequent lowering of the transition point in the swelling relationship to lower fission densities, possibly due to molybdenum variations, impurities, or higher power density/temperature [28]. It should also be noted that recrystallization occurs gradually over a range of fission densities, rather than at a sharp threshold. Therefore, due to both the instability in the location and the unrealistic, sharp nature of the inflection point, a piecewise linear fit cannot provide an acceptable swelling correlation. Because there is no statistical distinction between the quadratic and cubic fits, the quadratic fit was selected to reduce the order of the swelling correlation. Additionally, while it is recognized that the AFIP-6MkII experiment had a higher power density and temperature envelope than the other experiments in the data set, its inclusion in or exclusion from the binned data does not appear to influence the stability of the quadratic fit. Therefore, the experiment is included in the binned data set as it encompasses the critical recrystallization fission density regime.

## 5. Conclusions

Post-irradiation profilometry data from seven U-Mo plate-type fuel irradiation experiments were used to develop a predictive swelling correlation model based on statistical binning of the data to reduce scatter and reveal the underlying intrinsic swelling trend of the fuel type. Multiple methods for binning the large quantity of data were compared, and binning every 100 data points was selected to provide equal weighting between bins, and different models for fitting this binned data were analyzed. Though a piece-wise linear model provided the highest R-squared fit, it is not recommend for the swelling correlation, due to instability in the inflection point and the gradual nature of the recrystallizationdriven transition from solid to gaseous dominated fission-product swelling. Instead a quadratic swelling correlation model was proposed, along with 95% prediction bounds for reactor-safety analysis, and was validated against separate experiment datasets.

The developed swelling correlation will be further modified as new data are obtained in future testing. Furthermore, if a power or temperature dependence of the swelling rate is found to exist in future testing, additional swelling correlations will be developed for applicable test conditions.

## a b s t r a c t

The European Mini-Plate Irradiation Experiment (EMPIrE) was designed to support the development and testing of a coated uranium-molybdenum (U-Mo) dispersion fuel for the conversion of select highperformance research reactors (HPRRs) to utilize low-enriched uranium (LEU). To aid in the development of the coated fuel form, the EMPIrE test included several plate designs and irradiated them in the Idaho National Laboratory (INL) Advanced Test Reactor (ATR) at a high meat power density (∼21 kW/cm3) and to high fuel particle fission densities (∼6.4 × 1021 fissions/cm3). These conditions mimic the bounding conditions of the BR-2 reactor in Belgium, where a concurrent irradiation experiment was performed, and exceed those previously explored in dispersion U-Mo fuel plates. A local fuel swelling analysis, as determined through high-fidelity, post-irradiation mini-plate profilometry, was used along with statistical methods to non-destructively evaluate the overall performance and separate the effects of convoluted fabrication variables. While some effects observed with this non-destructive analysis were subtle, others had more significant, and possibly competing, effects on the fuel swelling behavior. These observations will be examined further with destructive examinations to more fully assess them as the fuel design is developed and qualified.

## 1. Introduction

Uranium-molybdenum (U-Mo) alloy dispersion fuels are being tested as a potential fuel type for the conversion of highperformance research reactors (HPRRs) from highly enriched uranium (HEU) with >20wt% 235U to low-enriched uranium (LEU) with <20wt% 235U [1]. The plate-type fuel design consists of a fuel core (or meat) of uranium, alloyed with molybdenum, fuel particles that are dispersed in an aluminum (Al) matrix and clad with an Al-alloy. This dispersion fuel design is meant to closely mimic those of HEU dispersion fuel forms (UAlx particles or U3O8 particles dispersed in an Al-matrix) currently fueling the reactors targeted for conversion as well as the currently qualified silicide LEU dispersion fuel used to convert other reactors from HEU [1,2]. To evaluate the suitability of and ultimately qualify the fuel system for use in these reactors, experiments, such as the European Mini-

Plate Irradiation Experiment (EMPIrE), were performed to test the performance of U-Mo under different design, fabrication, and irradiation conditions.

## 1.1. U-Mo dispersion fuel performance in previous experiments

A primary metric for evaluating the performance of a new fuel form is developing an understanding of its dimensional response to irradiation. To be qualified for use in HPRRs, a fuel must exhibit stable and predictable swelling behavior, well beyond the normal operational regimes used by the reactors. The mechanisms by which U-Mo fuel swells have been discussed previously in detail [3–7]. In summary, at low fuel particle fission densities (fission densities for brevity), the swelling is dominated by a solid fission product regime as gaseous fission products reside in fuel materials as gas atoms or precipitate to form a nano-bubble superlattice. Gaseous fission product driven swelling begins to dominate from intermediate fission densities (2.5–3.5  1021 fissions/cm3) as the nano-bubble lattice collapses and micrometer-scale bubbles form, a process largely attributed to grain refinement, or subdivision, during irradiation [3]. While these mechanisms for understanding the swelling behavior of the U-Mo fuel itself are fairly well defined, the dispersion of U-Mo fuel in an Al-matrix presents additional challenges for the dimensional stability.

It is well understood that U-Mo and aluminum readily form an amorphous Al-U-Mo interaction layer, a process that increases with the greater interface area offered by particle fuel dispersed in a matrix. The formation of this interaction layer during the current fabrication processes and subsequent irradiation of dispersion U-Mo fuel forms is well documented [1,5,8–10]. A primary detriment of this amorphous layer is fission gases are insoluble within it, which causes its interfaces to serve as sinks for fission gases. This allows the gases to accumulate outside the fuel particles themselves as large bubbles that can eventually interlink and compromise the mechanical integrity of the fuel meat, which can significantly increase the overall swelling rate of the fuel plate. As was observed in the FUTURE experiment, a breakaway swelling regime may result from this, eventually “pillowing” the plate [1,5,9,10].

Through several irradiation experiments, it was demonstrated that the formation of this Al-U-Mo interaction layer may be reduced through the inclusion of silicon (Si) in the Al-matrix to preferentially form a stable Si-U layer around the fuel particles [1,8,11– 17]. From the results of these experiments, it was postulated that the unfavorable Al-U-Mo interaction layer could be further controlled through the direct application of a diffusion barrier to the fuel particles themselves. The Surface Engineering of Low ENrIched Uranium-Molybdenum (SELENIUM) experiment was designed to compare Si and, based on the experience of the Russian MIR irradiated mini-rods containing coated U-Mo [18], zirconium nitride (ZrN) as potential coatings to inhibit interaction layer growth. These coatings were applied, via rolling drum physical vapor deposition (PVD), to the as-atomized U-7wt%Mo (U-7Mo) fuel powder prior to plate fabrication [1,16]. Where intact, the ZrN coating was demonstrated to virtually eliminate Al-U-Mo interaction layer formation; however, at higher fission densities an acceleration in swelling rate was observed [17,19]. This led to a primary hypothesis that controlling the pre-irradiation microstructure, specifically increasing the initial grain size, may further delay the increase in swelling observed at high fission densities [17,20,21].

## 1.2. EMPIrE and concurrent irradiation experiment goals

To study the application of a diffusion barrier and other engineering remedies to limit or control the swelling of the fuel plate, two subsequent irradiation tests were performed, the EMPIrE and SEMPER FIDELIS experiments [22,23], in the US and Europe, respectively. The EMPIrE irradiation experiment represents the first experiment to test the behavior of coated fuel particles, dispersed in an Al-matrix, in mini-plates and compare with the results to the concurrent SEMPER FIDELIS full-size plate experiment. The EMPIrE test also takes advantage of the increased number of samples possible in a test train to evaluate several design, fabrication, and irradiation conditions in a single irradiation test.

In both tests, different fuel particle coatings were employed as diffusion barriers, and as the SELENIUM experiment demonstrated that PVD can provide uniform ZrN coverage of fuel particles [16,20], PVD was again employed in both experiments. Additionally, atomic layer deposition (ALD) was proposed as an alternative method for the diffusion barrier application of ZrN [20,24] and was explored in the EMPIrE test as both a single diffusion barrier and as part of a two-coating system with a secondary AlN diffusion barrier applied via ALD to mitigate interaction between ZrN and the Al-matrix. To test the pre-irradiation microstructure influence, both the EMPIrE and SEMPER FIDELIS experiments applied a heat treatment to some of the fuel powder prior to coating application, in an attempt to increase the initial grain size of the fuel, and thus delay grain refinement and the onset of gaseous fission product dominated swelling [17,20–23].

In addition to testing different coating methods and the delay grain refinement hypothesis, the EMPIrE test explores other variables within the dispersion fuel fabrication process to evaluate their impact on performance. The particle size distribution of the fuel has historically been tightly controlled, with fuel particles above and below a target diameter range sieved from the powder prior to fabricating plate fuel core batches. Fabrication would be eased if the smaller particles, so-called “fines,” could be included in the fuel core, increasing the fuel packing and reducing fuel powder batch waste. However, the additional surface area from the use of a high fraction of fines presents an opportunity for a larger volume of the problematic Al-U-Mo interaction layer to form. To evaluate the influences of fine fraction on fuel performance in the EM-PIrE test, the fuel particles were subdivided into “coarse” (44–125 μm) and “fines” (25–44 μm) particle sizes, and plate-types were fabricated with 0, 10, or 25wt% fines [17,20,22,23].

As in the SELENIUM experiment, U-7Mo was selected as the primary fuel material in both the EMPIrE and SEMPER FIDELIS tests, as 235U-loading targets are more easily achieved because of the higher U-density than in U-10wt%Mo (U-10Mo), another alloy of interest for conversion of HPRRs. Increased Mo-alloying content was previously observed to result in a slight delay in the recrystallization behavior described above [7], so some coated U-10Mo dispersion fuel mini-plates were included to explore this microstructural behavior in the EMPIrE test. Additionally, a new, laboratoryscale (lab-scale) procedure in atomizing U-7Mo powder was evaluated in a single powder batch for comparison to large-scale atomized fuel powder [20].

The EMPIrE test was irradiated at the Idaho National Laboratory (INL) Advanced Test Reactor (ATR) at a high meat power density ( 21 kW/cm3) and to high particle fission densities ( 6.4  1021 fissions/cm3). The conditions were similar to the bounding conditions of the SCK CEN BR2 reactor, where the concurrent SEM-PER FIDELIS test was irradiated [17]. The SEMPER FIDELIS experiment also investigates the effects of fission rate across the full-size geometry [22], so the EMPIrE test explores these effects, through strategic positioning of mini-plates within each test train. The full irradiation conditions details are discussed further below.

## 1.3. Fuel swelling overview

Understanding of fuel swelling behavior over a wide range of conditions is vital to determining the irradiation performance of a new fuel form, as the dimensional stability of the fuel plates factors into establishing reactor operational safety limits. As was noted above, dispersion fuel systems add a challenge to this process because the dimensional changes observed are a convolution of swelling contributions from both the U-Mo fuel particles and the Al-U-Mo interaction layer. Previous studies indicate that the formation of the interaction layer itself does not have a significant volumetric effect on the fuel meat. However, the interaction layer interfaces are sinks for fission gases, resulting in large bubble formation and growth that both weakens the fuel meat structure and represents a significant contribution to the plate dimensional changes, external to the swelling of the fuel particles [8,17]. As such, it is appropriate to define the swelling behavior of this fuel system in a way that incorporates these additional contributions. Therefore, assuming that the Al-matrix does not contribute to the swelling, fuel particle swelling is defined as all dimensional growth within the fuel meat, relative to the initial volume of the fuel particles.

In general, a plate-type fuel geometry presents some conveniences as well as challenges when determining the fuel swelling behavior. Constraint of the fuel zone by the cladding, in both the axial and transverse directions, results in volumetric swelling of the fuel being confined to the thickness direction [3,25]. Recently, Robinson et al. demonstrated that local swelling analysis, determined through 2D post-irradiation plate profilometry, may be used to significantly increase available data and use statistical methods to quantify the underlying fuel swelling behavior [3]. Therefore, it is proposed that a local swelling analysis be used to increase the available swelling data for individual plate-types within the EMPIrE test and that the same statistical methodologies be used to interpret the behavioral trends. ## 2.1. Mini-plate fabrication and variables

## 2.1.1. Powder fabrication

A summary of plate-types and their combinations of fabrication variables are listed in Table 1, along with irradiation cycle allocation details, which are discussed further in the next section. Largescale atomized LEU U-7Mo powder with a nominal enrichment of 19.75 ± 0.2wt% 235U and a target average diameter of 80 μm was obtained from the Korea Atomic Energy Research Institute (KAERI) and supplied to SCK CEN and Argonne National Laboratory (ANL) for heat treatment and coating. Additionally, an independent labscale process was used to fabricate LEU U-7Mo powder at Framatome CERCA with the same 235U target enrichment, provided as a separate batch to SCK CEN. Y-12 also supplied LEU with a target enrichment of 19.75 ± 0.2wt% 235U to ANL, where it was alloyed with molybdenum and atomized to form U-10Mo powder.

At SCK CEN, the large-scale and new lab-scale U-7Mo fuel powders were separated into batches for annealing heat treatment (HT) at 1000°C for 1 hr. The heat treatments were performed in an Aratmosphere tube furnace in Al2O3 boats surrounded with a tantalum foil. Some of the powder was also retained in the as-atomized condition without heat treating (NoHT). All fuel powder batches were coated with ZrN by PVD, using a rotating drum method [16], with a target coating thickness of ∼1 μm. The coated fuel powder batches were provided to Framatome CERCA for mini-plate fabrication [20,23,28].

At ANL, U-7Mo powder produced by KAERI and lab-scale U-10Mo powders produced at ANL were separated into batches for annealing (HT) at 1000°C for 1 hr under vacuum, again retaining some of the U-7Mo powder batches in the as-atomized condition (NoHT). All powder batches were coated via ALD, and to aid in the ZrN adhesion process, all fuel powder was coated with ∼8-9 nm of Al2O3 prior to being coated with ZrN with a target thickness of ∼1 μm [29]. Additionally, some of the annealed U-7Mo powder was ALD coated with a secondary AlN diffusion barrier layer on top of the ZrN layer, with a target AlN thickness of 100 nm. This AlN-coated U-7Mo powder and the U-10Mo powder was retained at ANL for independent mini-plate fabrication while the remaining ALD-coated U-7Mo powder batches were provided to Framatome CERCA for commercial mini-plate fabrication [20,28].

## 2.2. Irradiation conditions

The 44 dispersion EMPIrE mini-plates were loaded at the Materials and Fuels Complex (MFC) at INL into 12 irradiation capsules, which can hold four mini-plates each. These capsules were irradiated in the South Flux Trap (SFT) of ATR over two irradiation cycles with eight capsules irradiated in each cycle. The distribution of plate-types in each irradiation cycle is detailed in Table 1.

In the first irradiation cycle, eight EMPIrE mini-plate capsules were inserted in the Dual Test Train Holder in the SFT, four per test train in each side, and were irradiated in ATR Cycle 162B for 38 days. One test train of four capsules was retained in the SFT of ATR while the other four-capsule test train was exchanged with the remaining unirradiated four capsules. These eight capsules were then irradiated in ATR cycle 164A for 54 days, resulting in a total of 92 irradiation days for the four capsule test train irradiated in both ATR cycles. The axial flux distribution of the test trains results in capsules at the top and bottom of the test train receiving a lower fission rate as compared to the two capsules in the middle of the test train. Therefore, mini-plates were allocated to strategic axial positions within each capsule test train in an attempt to provide a range of irradiation conditions for each plate-type.

Neutronics analysis for the EMPIrE experiment was performed using the Monte Carlo N-Particle (MCNP5) Version 1.60 radiation transport code and ORIGEN Version 2.2 depletion code (ORIGEN2). The experiment was modeled explicitly in MCNP, where MCNP5 was coupled with ORIGEN2 using an inhouse developed Python script. MCNP5 was used to calculate neutron flux and 1-group cross-sections to provide to ORIGEN2, which performs depletion calculations and provides updated compositions to MCNP5 for the next transport calculation.

The MCNP ATR full core model has simplified ATR fuel geometry. Each of the 19-plate fuel elements are homogenized into three radial regions and seven axial regions; fuel cladding and coolant are homogenized as well. The actual fuel loading of the ATR, a mixture of fresh and irradiated elements, is used as input in the MCNP material cards. Two types of simulations are performed using MCNP. The first is an eigenvalue simulation with depletion of the ATR fuel. The second is a fixed source calculation, where the fuel compositions are based on linear depletion of fuel elements, given the beginning of cycle and end of cycle loading provided by the ATR reactor engineers. The power distribution is entered using a source definition card in MCNP and distributed based on calculated fuel element powers also provided by the ATR reactor engineers [31].

Depletion and fission power of the experiment is dependent upon the incident thermal neutron flux. Nielsen et al. provides a summary of the methodology for validating the two MCNP models for neutron flux. Using a cobalt and nickel wire, the thermal and fast neutron flux is measured in ATR. A rigorous statistical analysis was performed to identify and quantify bias and uncertainty in the thermal neutron flux. The adjusted or best-estimate of the neutron flux generally was within 3 to 5% of the a priori or calculated neutron flux, with an uncertainty of 5.5%. This provides validation of the neutron flux for the MCNP model and provides confidence in the calculations [31].

Ultimately, the analysis resulted in a 4 × 16 mesh of nodes over the nominal fuel zone (19.05 mm  82.55 mm) of each EM-PIrE mini-plate, with a nominal meat thickness of 0.508 mm and 0.5334 mm for U-7Mo and U-10Mo plates, respectively. For each node the fuel meat power density, fuel meat fission density, and surface heat flux were calculated at multiple time steps from beginning of life (BOL) to end of life (EOL) [31]. The calculated EOL meat fission density values were converted to EOL particle fission density, referred to as particle fission density for brevity, using the as-fabricated volume fraction of fuel in each fuel plate, determined from powder mass recorded during fabrication. Examples of the 4  16 nodes for the EOL particle fission density and BOL surface heat flux are provided as contour heat-maps in Fig. 2 for the Type 5 plate EMPI0512. In both contour heat-maps, the axial and transverse positions are relative to the corner of the mini-plate at its ID end. This mini-plate was irradiated in a central test-train position in the ATR SFT during cycles 162B and 164A and is representative of typical mini-plate behavior. There was little variation within the mini-plate for either parameter and no unusual deviations between the EOL particle fission density and BOL surface heat flux nodes.

## 2.3. Fuel swelling determination

Following visual examination of the EMPIrE mini-plate capsules at the MFC Hot Fuels Examination Facility (HFEF), the capsules were disassembled, and a suite of non-destructive examination (NDE) post-irradiation examination (PIE) was started on the individual plates, which included visual examination, neutron radiography, immersion density, precision gamma spectrometry, profilometry, and oxide thickness measurements. Destructive examination (DE) PIE will be guided from the NDE-PIE results, including the swelling analysis presented in this work.

## 2.3.1. Measurements, data pre-processing, and calculations

Post-irradiation profilometry was gathered for each mini-plate in HFEF using the BONA4INL measurement bench, developed by SCK CEN. The measurement bench, which can accommodate multiple fuel-cladding geometries, was configured to measure platetype fuel, and a cassette assembly was used to support the EM-PIrE mini-plates for each measurement. Plate thickness was measured using opposing Sony Magnascale probes, with a thickness resolution of ±3 μm, in a 1 × 2 mm grid across the surface of each mini-plate and a location resolution of ±20 μm. Thickness calibration was performed before and confirmed after the measurement of each mini-plate, using certified gage block thickness standards. The cassette assembly was geometrically aligned in the measurement bench clamps, using alignment pins. The known offsets for plate-type geometries, dimensions of the mini-plate cassette assembly, and as-built dimensions for each mini-plate were then used to convert the measurement coordinates to a consistent reference frame. The reference frame selected was relative to each mini-plate’s top-left corner, where the “top” and “front” of the mini-plate are defined by its ID-stamp, referred to as the IDreference frame.

After gathering the profilometry data, the measurement bench was used again to check the local oxide thickness using eddy current measurements, with calibrations performed using oxide standards from identical cladding material. However, oxide thicknesses were measured to be less than 10 μm on the mini-plates. Once a correction factor of 1/1.975 is applied to account for the Alcladding consumed in the oxide formation [3], the oxide contribution to plate thickness was below the resolution limit for the profilometry measurements. Additionally, since visual examination of mini-plates showed no indication of oxide spalling, there is no bias in the PIE plate thickness on a geometric basis. In concert, this means the oxide thickness is insufficient to statistically influence the post-irradiation plate swelling, so the measured plate thickness in Eq. (1) does not need to be corrected for oxide layer formation [3].

The pre-irradiation plate thickness measurements were gathered using a Zeiss Calypso Coordinate Measurement Machine, using a consistent 4.13 mm  2.72 mm measurement grid. The measurement locations were converted to the same ID-reference frame as the post-irradiation profilometry measurements, again using the as-built dimensions of each mini-plate. The pre-irradiation thickness measurements were then interpolated, using Delaney triangulation, to the x/y coordinate location of each post-irradiation profilometry measurement. For any post-irradiation measurement locations outside pre-irradiation measurement grid, a linear extrapolation, bounded by the maximum fuel zone, was used to mimic the taper anticipated at the edge of dispersion plate fuel zones and observed previously in fresh fuel characterization [28].

As noted previously, the equivalent fuel thickness was determined indirectly using x-ray radiography. For the mini-plates produced by each fabricator, similar, but not identical, methods were used to accomplish this. For mini-plates fabricated by ANL, x-ray radiography of whole mini-plates was performed at INL. These radiographs were then compared with either a 10-tab U-7Mo or U-10Mo staircase thickness standard, as appropriate. For mini-plates fabricated by Framatome CERCA, x-ray attenuation was measured in line-scans down the surface of each plate with a spot diameter of 3 mm and a track spacing of 3 mm. An Al-staircase x-ray attenuation standard was developed, using miniplates fabricated to nominal, maximum, and minimum specified Uloading. This staircase standard was measured prior to each miniplate to determine the fractional U-loading relative to a nominal mini-plate at each measured location. Since the equivalent fuel thickness is directly related to U-loading and nominal U-loading is equivalent for each fabricator, the fractional U-loading was directly converted to an equivalent fuel thickness.

The center of each 3 mm equivalent thickness measurement “spot” was considered the measurement x/y coordinate and converted to the ID-reference frame using the as-built dimensions. Then, using the same methods employed for pre-irradiation plate thickness, the equivalent fuel thickness was interpolated and extrapolated to each post-irradiation profilometry x/y measurement coordinate. Extrapolation for mini-plates fabricated by ANL was bounded by the specified maximum fuel zone since the U-loading for these mini-plates was relatively homogenous. However, due to variation in U-loading and fuel zone taper in mini-plates fabricated by Framatome CERCA, interpolation and extrapolation for these mini-plates were each bounded by the coverage of the x-ray attenuation line-scans, such that neither interpolation nor extrapolation was performed in unsampled regions of the mini-plates.

It should be acknowledged that there was variation in ZrN coating thickness between the powder batches used to fabricate the EMPIrE experiment, see Table 3 [20,23,28,32]. However, the ZrN contribution to x-ray attenuation during radiography is minor. A comparison between the calculated attenuation from the Zr foil in the thickness standard and the equivalent thickness in the fuel zone of 1 μm and ∼2 μm ZrN particle coatings, revealed a total xray transmission >99% in each case and a variation in transmission of <0.25%. This means that the change in x-ray attenuation, due to ZrN coating thickness variation between powder batches, was negligible. Therefore, the calibration thickness standards described above were appropriate surrogates for the mini-plates, regardless of powder batch and coating thickness, and no additional corrections for coating thickness were required between powder batches for U-loading and equivalent thickness determination.

Pre-irradiation porosity volume fraction was determined for each mini-plate in the as-built condition by comparing asfabricated volume of each plate, measured using the Archimedes method through immersion density, to the theoretical as-fabricated plate volume. The latter was calculated using the powder masses reported during fabrication, the volume averaged coated particle size distribution, estimated for each plate-type from un-irradiated archive plate characterization [28], and the average ZrN coating thickness for each fuel powder batch [20,23,28,32]. The average values for each plate-type are listed in Table 3. As noted previously, it was assumed that any pre-irradiation porosity in the fuel zone was consumed prior to external thickness changes being measurable in the fuel zone. Therefore, the volume fraction of pre-irradiation porosity, corrected by the volume fraction of fuel within the core, is included additively in Eq. (1). Local fuel particle swelling fraction values were calculated for each post-irradiation profilometry measurement that had corresponding interpolated and extrapolated pre-irradiation parameters falling within the bounds defined above. It should be noted that the inclusion of the pre-irradiation porosity term means any uncertainty in this term has a direct impact on the post-irradiation particle swelling fraction. Beyond the scope presented in this work, the post-irradiation porosity in the Al-matrix will be examined during DE-PIE, primarily through metallography, and on a case-bycase basis, it will be determined if the post-irradiation swelling fraction values reported here require adjustment. In particular, due to higher as-fabricated porosity in some of the plate-types in this experiment, as compared to previous studies on U-Mo dispersion fuel, the closure of the as-fabricated porosity must be investigated in future DE-PIE studies.

To have local fission density values to compare local fuel particle swelling fraction values against, the previously mentioned 4 × 16 mesh of particle fission density values, calculated through ORIGEN2 depletion analysis, was converted to the ID-reference frame for each mini-plate, see example in Fig. 2a. Then local particle fission density values were assigned to each post-irradiation profilometry measurement coordinate.

## 2.3.2. Data processing

As noted previously, scatter is anticipated when examining local fuel particle swelling fraction versus local fission density results, due to both the convolution of volumetric and non-volumetric contributions to plate thickness changes and variation in the as-built conditions. While the statistical methods demonstrated by Robinson et al. [3] were employed to manage this scatter, some initial filtering of the data is prudent to aid this process. It is known from the pre-irradiation characterization that there is some minor variation within the as-built mini-plates, in particular the precise location and taper of the dispersion fuel core. Therefore, with post-irradiation profilometry gathered consistently across each fuel plate, some measured values will be off the fuel zone, despite the bounds detailed above for the pre-irradiation interpolation and extrapolation.

Two additional filters were put in place to attempt to consistently limit measurements to the fueled regions of the mini-plates. First, because the minimum average fission density for miniplates irradiated in the EMPIrE test was 1.82 1021 fissions/cm3, well above operating regimes where any initial irradiation induced contraction will take place, fueled regions will demonstrate a detectable increase in plate thickness. Therefore, local particle swelling fraction calculations were limited to coordinates where the local Tplate,post≥Tplate,pre, and regions where Tplate,post<Tplate,pre were considered unfueled and excluded from the calculations as they would result in artificially low swelling fraction values. Second, calculated values are limited to those where the local Uloading ≥ 80% nominal U-loading, as determined by the x-ray attenuation for equivalent fuel thickness calculations. This removes values calculated using extrapolated equivalent fuel thicknesses from the absolute edge of the fuel zone taper, which have an artificially low equivalent fuel thickness, and thus artificially inflated fuel swelling fraction values. These two filters do not eliminate the scatter in local fuel particle swelling fraction versus fission density values, but they reduce some known sources of scatter, and ∼600 local measurements remain for each EMPIrE mini-plate.

To take advantage of the large data-sets possible through local measurements, Robinson et al. established that the statistical method known as “binning” could reduce visible scatter by limiting the influence of highly localized effects, such as the constrained fuel zone edges and regions affected by fuel relocation, driven by irradiation-assisted creep [3,25]. This allowed the underlying trend in swelling fraction as a function of fission density to be revealed for monolithic U-10Mo plate-type fuel, establishing a predictive swelling model. To remove local biasing effects in this literature example, the >18,000 swelling fraction versus fission density pairs were shuffled and sorted by fission density before the data was averaged, “binned,” every 100 nearest data points [3].

This methodology was adapted to the analysis in the EMPIrE test in multiple ways. Binning was performed within individual mini-plates with a bin size of 10 data points. A smaller bin size, relative to the literature example, was utilized because of the smaller relative data-sets for individual mini-plates. If binned every 100 data points, the resolution of swelling behavior as a function of fission density is effectively reduced to a plate-average scale. The smaller bin size results in ∼60 binned data pairs for each mini-plate, allowing the examination of variation in fuel particle swelling with fission density across the mini-plate surface while still reducing scatter from local biasing effects.

It should be acknowledged that some literature sources recommend reducing the scatter through geometrically filtering the constrained and fuel relocation regions from the data analysis, which amounts to 5 mm of effected data excised from the edge of the fuel zone [8,25]. This trimming methodology was compared against binning every 10 data points within individual EMPIrE mini-plates, and the resulting scatter was not visibly reduced. As trimming reduced the local data available for revealing behavior by >70%, with no observed benefit, binning within individual miniplates was selected over geometrically trimming the data.

While the binning method does reduce scatter on an individual mini-plate basis, the main strength of the method established in the literature was allowing data from several irradiation experiments, consisting of different plate and mini-plate geometries, to contribute to revealing the underlying behavior [3]. Likewise, in the EMPIrE test, data pairs from individual mini-plates were consolidated by combinations of fabrication variables, shuffled, and sorted by particle fission density to remove location and individual plate biases, before being binned every 50-nearest data points, as defined by particle fission density. Additionally, the full, unbinned consolidated variable data-sets were quadratically fit, constrained through the origin. When establishing a predictive model to describe the U-10Mo swelling behavior, Robinson et al. tested multiple functions, including quadratic and piecewise. It was demonstrated that the location of the piecewise function inflection point was unstable, shifting substantially depending on the fission density regimes of included consistent data while the quadratic fit was more stable and consistent. Therefore, given the variation in fission density regimes for consolidated data-sets in the EMPIrE experiment, the same fitting methods are used in this work [3]. The binned data along with these quadratic fits and 95% confidence bands of the full data-sets were methodically compared to separate the effects of individual variables on the fuel particle swelling behavior. Note that 95% confidence bands define the level of confidence in the quadratic model while 95% prediction bands define where new observations within the model may appear. For the comparisons in this work, 95% confidence bands are used to compare the behaviors of the fit data-sets [33]. It should be acknowledged that the statistical difference observed and discussed here will be examined further with DE-PIE to both verify variable effects and explore mechanisms.

## 4. Discussion

## 4.1. General swelling behavior

While there was significant scatter observed in Fig. 3a, both within data-sets and among plate-types, the overall swelling behavior appears consistent with historical U-Mo irradiations [3,7,17]. Recall, that swelling in U-Mo gradually transitions from a solid to gaseous fission product dominated regime with increasing fission density. This occurs as the fission gas nano-bubble lattice collapses during grain subdivision, with micron-size gas bubbles forming at fuel grain boundaries and large bubbles coalescing at the Al-U-Mo interaction layer interfaces, resulting in an increase in swelling rate [34]. At low-to-moderate fuel particle fission densities (<4.5 × 1021 fission/cm3), the swelling rate appears consistent with the behavior observed in the ZrN particle coated and nonheat treated SELENIUM experiment. As the swelling rates increase with fission density, the EMPIrE mini-plates appear to swell gradually and show no signs of excessive or breakaway swelling nor signs that plate pillowing is in progress [8,17]. These observations are more apparent when the visual scatter is reduced through binning within individual mini-plates, see Fig. 3b.

Fig. 4b also indirectly demonstrates a lack of observable fuel meat temperature effects on the fuel particle swelling as the variation in fission rates results in different BOL fuel meat temperatures, within the plate-types. The largest temperature variation observed in Fig. 4b is between the Type 8A plates, EMPI0820 and EMPI0822, which have BOL average fuel meat temperatures of 108°C and 149°C, respectively, and peak fuel meat centerline temperatures of 126°C and 166°C, respectively [35]. It is possible that the variation in the fission rate and BOL fuel meat temperatures within the EMPIrE test are insufficient to detect any influence on the fuel swelling behavior. The peak fuel temperature did not exceed 175°C, the threshold temperature identified for U-Mo-Al interdiffusion [36], so the temperature ranges noted here would not exhibit changes in interdiffusion behavior. Regardless, it should be emphasized that cross comparison between the plate-types is not possible with this figure because the EOL fission density ranges differ for each platetype.

## 4.2. Initial separation of the fabrication variable effects

While the binning performed in Fig. 3b and 3b are limited to within individual mini-plates, initial observations may be made on separating variable combinations or plate-types, see Table 1. For the entire range of fission densities tested, plate-types 1, 7A, and 9 show an increase in fuel particle swelling relative to other platetypes, and this also appears to be the case for Type 2 plates at higher fission densities. By contrast, plate-types 5, 8A, and 8B appear to show reduced swelling, relative to other plate-types, for the fission density ranges examined. While individual mini-plate performance is important, there is still overlap in the scatter as it relates to overall plate-types. Therefore, to truly separate variable effects, analysis is required across mini-plates, and this was performed in the binning and fitting operations of consolidated variable data-sets, shown in Fig. 5 through Fig. 8. The influence of each variable on the overall swelling performance is discussed in the following sections. In general, where there is no overlap between the 95% confidence bands of fits presented, there is a statistically significant difference in behavior between the data populations compared [33]. That said, it should be acknowledged that some statistical differences observed, while a potential indication of variable driven effects on fuel swelling fraction, may be within acceptable tolerances when selecting methods for fabrication and developing a safety basis. Additionally, substantial DE-PIE and fresh fuel microstructural characterization will be necessary to characterize mechanisms for the differences observed in this NDE-PIE swelling analysis.

## 4.2.1. Mo-alloying content

The overlap of the 95% confidence bands for the fits in Fig. 5 indicates that, in this case, an effect of Mo-alloying content on the swelling behavior cannot be detected. It should be reiterated that the U-10Mo plates were included in the EMPIrE test to investigate the changes in microstructural evolution with increased Moalloying in coated dispersion fuel mini-plates. However, it is likely that any slight delay in the recrystallization and subsequent transition from solid fission product dominated swelling for U-10Mo would result in very subtle effects on the external plate dimensional changes. Additionally, it should be noted that due to the limited number of U-10Mo dispersion mini-plates in the EMPIrE test, the consolidated heat treated ALD mini-plate data-sets in Fig. 5 each include plate-type fuel cores from both particle size distributions. Since the particle size distribution of the fuel core may indirectly affect the swelling behavior for this fuel-type, discussed in the sections below, it was allowed to contribute to and bias both data-sets. This is visualized through the data binning within the plate-types in Fig. 5. The mini-plates in the limited U-10Mo data-set also have a higher pre-irradiation porosity contribution to the swelling fraction than the mini-plates in the U-7Mo data-set (Table 3), so the conservative assumption that all preirradiation porosity was consumed in these plates may be occluding any difference. Therefore, it is suggested that the confluence of uncertainties introduced by the limited data-sets prevent the nondestructive detection of any subtle statistical effects on swelling that may result from Mo-alloying content. It should be emphasized that regardless of the statistical similarity in this test, the swelling behavior from using U-7Mo to more easily meet 235U-loading targets in the dispersion fuel core appears stable, predictable, and manageable.

## 4.2.2. Fuel powder source

The initial observations from Fig. 3b already indicated that mini-plates from Type 9, those fabricated with U-7Mo powder atomized via a newly established lab-scale process, appeared to show increased swelling relative to other plate-types. However, the scale of this increased swelling is accentuated when compared to other PVD plates containing heat treated fuel powder in Fig. 6b. The three powder batches used to fabricate this similar plate-type were sourced from the same large-scale fuel batch before receiving the same thermal annealing treatment and PVD coating process at SCK CEN. However, the swelling behavior of these mini-plates containing multiple, larger-scale fuel powder batches is significantly less than those containing the independent lab-scale fuel powder, for the range of fission densities in the EMPIrE test.

It is proposed that lab-scale powder inhomogeneity in the Type 9 plates contributes to the increase in swelling fraction. Fresh coated powder characterization conducted at SCK CEN prior to shipping the powder for mini-plate fabrication revealed the presence of uranium carbide inclusions and some grain boundary cracking in the fuel powder, following the thermal anneal while a more uniform grain structure was observed in the large-scale fabricated fuel powder [20]. A relatively weakly bonded interface exists between U-Mo and UC, due to their thermophysical property mismatch, and these interfaces and the pre-existing defects along the grain boundaries may act as favorable fission gas accumulation sites for bubble nucleation, increasing the swelling magnitude at lower fission densities [38]. The presence of these intergranular carbide precipitates, in the new lab-scale powder batch, also likely contributed to reducing metallurgical grain growth during powder heat treatment [21]. As will be observed in the later sections, this may have contributed to the increased observed swelling. Additionally, there were irregularly shaped particles present in the labscale atomized powder, which may have contributed to ZrN coating non-uniformity [20] and may have led to additional Al-U-Mo interaction layer interfaces for fision gas sinks.

## 4.2.3. Particle size distribution

The effect of particle size distribution on the swelling behavior in general is not trivial to isolate as there are several conflated fabrication variables; however, it is observed that plate-types containing increased inclusion of annealed “fines” have increased swelling, as compared to the no annealed “fines” containing counterparts. It can be observed in the Type 1 ALD plates in Fig. 6a, where the inclusion of 25wt% fines in the fuel cores containing annealed fuel resulted in a significant increase in fuel particle swelling fraction, at all fission densities tested in the EMPIrE experiment. A slight increase in swelling rate occurs at higher fission densities for the plates containing fines and as-atomized fuel powder. The observed differences are more subtle for the Type 8B PVD plates containing annealed powder in Fig. 6b. While there is a slight increase in the fuel particle swelling fraction for plates containing 10wt% fines and no overlap in the 95% confidence bands for the fits of those plates and those of the non-fines containing plates, this comparison can only be made at low-to-moderate fission densities.

The increases in swelling observed in these plate-types could be due to multiple factors. It cannot be ignored that the preirradiation ZrN particle coating thicknesses of the Type 1, 2, and 8B plates were thinner than their 0wt% fines containing counterparts. This may also explain the reduced increase observed in the Type 8B PVD plates that have a thicker coating as compared to the ALD plates (types 1 and 2). These coating thickness differences will be discussed further in the next section. Alternatively, since the ALD plates contained 15wt% more fines than the PVD plates, the quantity of fines included in the fuel core may also play a role in the differences observed. The potential interaction between the inclusion of annealed fines powder will be explored further below.

## 4.2.4. Coating methodology and composition

The coating methodology effects were initially explored by considering data from mini-plates that included no fines in their fuel cores, due to the difference in fines-wt% explored in ALD and PVD plates and no inclusion of fines in plates containing an additional AlN diffusion barrier. Examining Fig. 7a, it appears that PVD-coated plates swelled less than the ALD-coated plates when heat-treated fuel powder was used; however, the opposite appears to be the case when as-atomized fuel powder was used (Fig. 7b).

One competing parameter that may contribute to the behaviors observed in Fig. 7 is the difference in pre-irradiation porosity that is assumed to be fully consumed prior to external dimensional changes, see Table 3. The pre-irradiation porosity calculated for the Type 22 and 7A mini-plates were much higher (∼5-7%) than the other plate-types (∼1-4%). In Fig. 7a, the difference in increased swelling fraction observed for ALD plates containing the additional AlN diffusion barrier as compared to ALD plates with the single ZrN layer, is close to the difference in their porosity terms. This is also the case for the difference observed between the PVD and ALD plates that contained as-atomized fuel in Fig. 7b. However, the differences observed between PVD (Type 8A) and ALD (Type 5) plates in Fig. 7a may not be attributed to pre-irradiation porosity as the PVD plates contain more than double the porosity of the ALD plates (Table 3).

The primary difference that should be acknowledged when comparing between the behaviors of PVD and ALD plates is the ZrN coating thicknesses attained were very different, see Table 3. Particles coated by PVD received an average thickness between ∼1- 2 μm while particles coated by ALD received a coating between ∼0.3-1 μm [20,23,28,32]. This difference in coating may contribute to the differences in swelling observed in Fig. 7a. While the Type 22 plates had the secondary AlN barrier coating, the total average particle coating thickness was less than the average in the Type 5 plates. Further, the lowest fuel particle swelling fraction behavior in the EMPIrE test was observed in the Type 8A plates, which had the thickest ZrN coating. There was a large range of coating thicknesses observed within this plate-type (∼1.3-2 μm); however, it is not clear whether there is a minimum coating thickness beyond which there is not a discernable benefit for increasing that thickness. It should be noted that subsequent to the fabrication of the EMPIrE experiment, modifications to the powder coating basket and ALD coating system mitigated the reduced coating thickness issue and resulted in more uniform and thick coatings. The difference in coating thickness cannot explain the opposite behavior observed in Fig. 7b; though, as was noted above, the differences in swelling observed in this comparison may be obscured by the as-fabricated porosity terms.

## 4.2.5. Heat treatment

In the case of PVD and ALD plates that contain no fines, there was a slight reduction in swelling behavior when the U-7Mo powder was annealed prior to coating, see Fig. 8a and b. While the difference in data-sets observed in Fig. 8b is accentuated by the increased pre-irradiation porosity terms for Type 7A plates noted previously (Table 3), the statistically significant difference remains even if pre-irradiation porosity is neglected. This slight reduction in swelling was anticipated, as a primary experimental parameter, and was likely due to increased initial grain size delaying grain refinement and the subsequent transition to fission gas dominated swelling regimes, within the fuel particles themselves [17,21,23,28]. However, examining Fig. 8c and d, the effect of heat treatment appears to reverse when fines are included in the Type 1 and 8B fuel cores, again recognizing that plate-type limitations make this comparison limited for PVD plates.

A potential source for the higher swelling in these plate-types was observed in the fresh fuel powder characterization from both the EMPIrE and SEMPER FIDELIS experiments. In both experiments it was revealed that heat treated powders have a higher propensity than as-atomized powders for agglomerating, forming clusters and so-called “peanut” particle pairs [20,28,39]. It was also observed that particles in contact were more likely to “peel” their coatings during fabrication rolling operations [23]. This exposes the U-Mo to the Al-matrix and increases the Al-U-Mo interaction layer formation during fabrication and under irradiation [40], thereby increasing the interfaces available as fission gas sinks [8,17]. The increased number of particles and surface area afforded by the fines in the Type 1 and 8B plates would make these plate-types more susceptible to such a process. Given that the reduction in swelling with heat treatment observed in PVD and ALD plates with 0wt% fines was minor in Fig. 8a and b, it seems likely that any benefit from increasing the initial grain size would be overridden by such annealed fines agglomeration and subsequent loss in diffusion barrier integrity. It should be acknowledged that the agglomeration of U-Mo particles during heat treatment may be avoided through improving the powder annealing process with additional powder agitation. Therefore, any potential competition between fabrication variables observed in this experiment may be nullified by eliminating what currently appears to be a limitation of the annealing process.

## 5. Conclusions

The EMPIrE test was intended to explore several design, fabrication, and irradiation condition variables, and this work nondestructively evaluated their influence on the fuel particle swelling performance. Mini-plate profilometry was used to perform a local swelling analysis and significantly increase available data, such that the statistical method of binning and fitting data across individual mini-plates revealed trends in performance. The swelling behavior of the EMPIrE test was demonstrated to be consistent with previous U-Mo fuel plate irradiation experiments. The mini-plate geometry had no observed effect on the results and increased Mo alloying content in the coated particles did not have a detectable beneficial effect on the fuel swelling. These results suggest a continuity of behavior from mini-plate to large-scale plates in the coated dispersion U-7Mo fuel form that was observed previously in monolithic U-10Mo fuel plates. The swelling in the EMPIrE test did not appear to show signs of entering a breakaway swelling regime or plate pillowing.

Fission rate and BOL fuel meat temperature were varied in several plate-types, but no effect on fuel particle swelling was detected non-destructively in this experiment. A new, laboratoryscale powder fabrication process was also evaluated and appears to result in increased fuel swelling. In plates containing annealed U-Mo powder, the thicker ZrN coating of the fuel, achieved via PVD, appears to reduce the overall fuel swelling as compared to the thinner ALD coatings achieved during this experiment; though, thicker, more uniform ALD coatings are now possible. At this time, the trend is unclear when as-atomized powder is utilized. Both behaviors will need to be further evaluated during DE-PIE to determine whether this is caused by lower ALD coating thickness, retained pre-irradiation porosity, or other differences in plate fabrication. Annealing the powder prior to coating application appeared to slightly reduce the overall swelling as expected, likely through delaying U-Mo grain refinement and thus reducing the density of nucleation sites for micron-size fission gas bubbles. However, the challenge of agglomerating fuel powder during the heat treatment also appears to compound with the increased surface area afforded by particle fines, where the beneficial effects of annealing are potentially negated by the swelling increases from increased formation of interaction layer interfaces that act as fission gas sinks. Meanwhile, adjustments to the annealing process to prevent fuel particle agglomeration during the heat treatment may eliminate the effects on the swelling altogether.

It should be emphasized that while several variables were observed to affect the swelling behavior in the EMPIrE test, the magnitude of some effects may be within tolerances for fabrication design and safety analyses. That said, pending DE-PIE, it seems clear that consistency in fuel powder fabrication and final particle coating thickness can be important parameters to the performance of this fuel form. Microstructural characterization is in progress to continue the analysis efforts, define mechanisms for the observed effects, and confirm the overall suitability of the fuel form for use in high-performance research and test reactors.

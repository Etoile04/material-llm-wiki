## a b s t r a c t

Fuel swelling of U–Mo alloy was modeled using the measured data from samples irradiated up to a fission density of -7  1027 fissions/m3 at temperatures below -250 -C. The overall fuel swelling was measured from U–Mo foils with as-fabricated thickness of 250 lm. Volume fractions occupied by fission gas bubbles were measured and fuel swelling caused by the fission gas bubbles was quantified. The portion of fuel swelling by solid fission products including solid and liquid fission products as well as fission gas atoms not enclosed in the fission gas bubbles is estimated by subtracting the portion of fuel swelling by gas bubbles from the overall fuel swelling. Empirical correlations for overall fuel swelling, swelling by gas bubbles, and swelling by solid fission products were obtained in terms of fission density.

## 1. Introduction

U–Mo alloy, as a potential fuel for research and test reactors, generally has stable irradiation performance as long as fission product-induced swelling is concerned. Up until very high burnup, fission gas bubbles are small and predictable at temperatures of interest, which are below -250 -C. In a fuel plate, which is a typical geometry for research and test reactors, fuel swelling causes the plate thickness to increase. Hence, excessive fuel swelling is a potential hazard that can disturb the coolant flow path.

The predominant form of research and test reactor fuels currently in use in the world is that of fuel particles dispersed in an inert, typically aluminum, matrix. The fueled zone in a dispersion fuel plate, i.e., the fuel particles-matrix mixture zone, is frequently called the ‘fuel meat’ or ‘fuel core’ and is metallurgically bonded to aluminum-alloy cladding. In the US Reduced Enrichment for Research and Test Reactors (RERTR) program, U–Mo alloy fuel has been tested primarily in a dispersion form. In this program, a monolithic form has been also tested, in which a U–Mo alloy foil is directly bonded to aluminum-alloy cladding. Fig. 1 schematically shows the cross sections of these two fuel forms in plate-type fuel geometry.




The data that deal with U–Mo swelling at typical temperatures for research and test reactors are scarce in the literature. Rest et al. [1] recently published a paper including a mechanistic modeling of evolution of fission gas bubbles on grain boundaries in U–Mo up to a fission density of -3  1027 fissions/m3 . For fuel performance analysis, a fuel swelling model is required, in particular for high fission densities beyond -3  1027 fissions/m3 where fuel swelling becomes more important.

For this present work, the overall fuel swelling was quantified by measuring fuel volume changes, specifically U–Mo alloy foil thickness increases after irradiation in test plates. For convenience of analysis, the overall fuel swelling is defined and quantified as consisting of two main contributions; one part caused by solid and liquid fission products including small solid fission gas precipitates, and the other by fission gas bubbles. To obtain the part due to fission gas bubbles, volume fractions occupied by fission gas bubbles were measured using SEM (scanning electron micrograph) images of fracture surfaces of irradiated dispersion fuel samples. The portion by non-gaseous, i.e., solid and liquid, fission products was then calculated by subtracting the volume of gas bubbles from the overall fuel swelling.





## 2. Pre-irradiation microstructures of U–Mo




Alloying Mo in uranium is to stabilize uranium in the c-phase. Having a bcc structure, the c-phase has more open spaces than the close-packed orthorhombic a-phase, which accommodates more fission products as interstitials without much lattice expansion. Hence, the c-phase uranium alloy has lower swelling than the a-phase [2]. The bcc c-phase also has isotropic swelling behavior while the a-phase has anisotropic behavior. It is known that, for U–Mo alloy, the maximum c stabilizing power can be achieved when the Mo content is 10 wt% [3]. A U–Mo alloy with a low Mo content is transformed from the c-phase, metastable at irradiation temperatures, to the two-phase equilibrium a + c0 . An example of the low Mo content alloy (U–4Mo) in which this transformation occurred during irradiation is shown in Fig. 2. Apparently, at such a low Mo content the known c stabilizing effect of fission spikes [4] is no longer sufficient to overcome the thermodynamic energy that drives the alloy to its two-phase equilibrium.

The atomization technology widely used in powder metallurgy is applied to fabricate spherical U–Mo alloy powders used in some test plates of the present study. This method typically uses a rotating disk in a vacuum chamber. When a liquid fuel melt is poured on the disk, the centrifugal force of the disk produces liquid fuel droplets that are cooled in the process of flying away. The size of fuel particles is determined by controlling the disk rotation speed. The typical fuel particle size is in average -70 lm (see Ref. [6] for more details). Atomized powders, as the particles are spherical, used in dispersion plates have an advantage over machined powders because they have lower surface-to-volume ratios, which lowers volume expansion by interaction products between the fuel particles and Al matrix.



Mo-content network may appear as thick boundaries, which are sometimes called ‘grain boundaries.’ As reported by Kim et al. [7], the average ‘grain size’ is -2 lm for an atomized U–10Mo powder. Our measurement using an SEM picture of an as-fabricated RERTR-3 plate yielded a grain size of 2.5 lm. The grain is surrounded by a layer that is -0.5-lm thick and has a nominally lower Mo content. Therefore, the term ‘grain boundary’ used here is rather a layer or a zone, which is different from the typical ones that are much thinner and have the same composition as the grain interior.

A lower Mo-content U–Mo alloy has a higher swelling rate than a higher Mo-content alloy, because of higher bubble growth rate. By analogy, it is arguable that the lower Mo-content grain boundaries also have a more favorable environment for bubble nucleation.

A similar process for a lower Mo-content alloy, such as U–7Mo, is shown by the left arrow (B in Fig. 3). The gap between the solidus and liquidus lines on arrow B is slightly smaller than on arrow A. Thus, U–7Mo will have slightly smaller solid islands, i.e., smaller grains than U–10Mo. In addition, the grain boundary Mo content of U–7Mo is lower than that of U–10Mo.

In order to assess to what extent the as-solidified Mo concentration in the grain boundaries will be eliminated as a result of fission enhanced diffusion, we use Bleiberg’s analysis of a + c0 to c phase reversal during fissioning. Bleiberg [9,10] reported that the aphase lamellae initially spaced by the thin c0 -phase layers in U– Mo alloy fuel disappeared during irradiation to a burnup of -0.1 at% total U burnup (LEU equiv. 0.5%U-235 burnup, which is defined a burnup based on 20% U-235 enrichment). The average distance between the a lamellae centers was 0.5 lm and their thickness was 0.2 lm. Bleiberg concluded that phase homogenization was sensitive to the fission rate and the distance between lamella centers.

The as-fabricated Mo concentration at the grain interior is estimated to be 22 at% and that at the grain boundary 17 at% [7,8].

Machined powders are made from well homogenized cast alloy, and therefore do not contain the cored structure typical of the rapidly solidified atomized powders. They are heavily coldworked, so they contain a high concentration of dislocations. During hot rolling fabrication of the fuel plates and subsequent irradiation, this dislocation structure will undergo polygonization. This final subgrain structure of machined particles is similar to that of the atomized, providing nucleation sites for gas bubbles.

U–Mo foils are made by hot rolling of a U–Mo alloy ingot to reduce thickness down to typically 0.25 mm or 0.5 mm. Several heating and rolling steps are repeated during the thickness reduction process. The final rolling is performed at room temperature. Therefore, like the machined powder particles, the U–Mo foils have a heavily cold-worked structure and contain a high concentration of dislocations.

The relative extent of cold working among atomized powder, machined powder and foil can be estimated by using their hardness data. In Table 1, microhardness data available in the literature are compared. The data for atomized powders and machined powders are in as-fabricated plates and for foils in asfabricated foils. As expected, machined powders have the highest hardness and the atomized powders the lowest. Foils have slightly higher hardness than atomized particles. However, during plate fabrication, the foils will undergo heat processes to establish bonding between the foil and Al-alloy cladding, which will reduce hardness. After plate fabrication, all U–Mo alloy test samples, powdered or foil, are subjected to a subsequent blister annealing test (1 h at 485 -C). Therefore, the cold-worked microstructure will be somewhat annealed. Monolithic fuel and atomized fuel have after all similar structures when ready to be loaded for irradiation, which also renders virtually equivalent fuel swelling kinetics for both cases. In the present study, because SEM data for the monolithic fuel plates are unavailable, SEM data only for atomized dispersion fuel plates are used to represent for both cases.

It may be safely assumed that as long as there are no fission gas bubbles, fission product swelling of U–Mo alloy fuel is a linear function of burnup. At a fission density between 2.5  1027 and





3.5  1027 fissions/m3 , fuel swelling shifts to a higher rate due to enhancement of fission gas bubble formation and growth. In this transition, the c U–Mo fuel undergoes grain refinement or ‘recrystallization’, by which more grain boundaries become available for bubble nucleation and growth [14,15]. This phenomenon is a result of irradiation damage and fission product accumulation in the fuel lattice. As grain refinement progresses to grain interior regions at higher burnup, more grain surfaces become available for new bubble formation and growth. Hence, higher fuel swelling at higher burnup. Grain refinement starts over a range of fission density between 2.5  1027 and 3.5  1027 fissions/m3 . In the present modeling, the fission density for this transition is set at 3  1027 fissions/ m3 , a representative value adopted for modeling purpose. Grain refinement will continue until a certain burnup is reached. The time of completion of grain refinement also appears to have a range. The application limit of the present model is set by the completion of grain refinement.

SEM micrographs of post-irradiation fracture surfaces of U–Mo are shown in Fig. 10. Few fission gas bubbles are observable heterogeneously over the fuel cross section in the low burnup sample shown in Fig. 10a. The bubble formation sites are on the grain boundaries, as Kim et al. [14] showed. Bubbles are rarely visible in the grain interior regions; the ones shown in the grain interior are probably close to grain boundaries in three dimensions. Considering that the SEM resolution limit is -0.1 lm, one can argue that fission gas bubbles greater than 0.1 lm in size first form in the grain boundaries. Until recently before Van den Berghe et al. [17] reported their TEM observation, the question had remained open as to whether smaller gas bubbles than 0.1 lm form in this grain interior. They showed by TEM work that intra-granular gas bubbles of about 2 nm-size were formed in a super lattice with a spacing of 6–7 nm in the U–7Mo fuel matrix irradiated to 2.4  1027 fissions/m3 . As the nano-bubbles are small, even though their number density is large, these bubbles increase the fuel volume by only -1.3% and may be solid precipitates. Gan et al. [18] also reported TEM results of U–7Mo at a higher fission density of 4.5  1027 fissions/m3 . Grain refinement has progressed, but intra-granular regions still exist at this burnup. The bubble size is 3.5 nm with the superlattice constant of 11.5 nm. Comparing with Van den Berghe’s nano-bubble size, it appears that the size of nano-bubbles increases with burnup. The intra-granular bubbles take a volume fraction of -5.9%. These bubbles are still invisible through SEM although their volume fraction is considerable. Hence, this volume fraction is considered a part of the solid fission product swelling.





The population of ‘SEM-visible’ grain boundary bubbles, which are larger than the resolution limit of our SEM microsope, -0.1 lm, increases and additional bubbles progressively appear in newly formed grain boundaries with burnup as grain refinement continues (shown in Fig. 10c). At this stage, the average size of SEM-visible bubbles also increases with fission density as the number density increases, both of which promote fuel swelling. Eventually, at higher burnup, SEM-visible bubbles uniformly occupy the entire fuel cross section (shown in Fig. 10d) as the grain refinement is nearly completed.

The measurement data obtained here are based on the measurements of bubble cross sections shown on the fracture surfaces. Readings on SEM pictures with resolution limit of 0.1 lm lead to considerable uncertainties in the measurements. The error bars provided in Fig. 11 are a reflection of these considerations.

Grain refinement or recrystallization affects fission gas bubble formation and growth, and as such fuel swelling. The machined particles are heavily cold worked, which produces higher dislocation densities than atomized particles. This renders the machined particles susceptible to grain refinement at a lower burnup, which causes higher swelling by gas bubbles [14]. In Fig. 12, the data obtained in samples with machined powders are consistently higher than those for atomized powder samples.




Fig. 13 shows comparisons for two pairs of samples tested sideby-side so that the test conditions are the same. The grain sizes and fission gas bubble population of the machined powder samples are contrastingly different from the atomized powder samples.





Formation and growth of fission gas bubbles are influenced by the availability of grain boundaries, so the effect of grain size is considerable. For example, Z03 and Y01 given in Table 4 contain c-phase annealed powders, in which grain sizes are larger and therefore less grain boundaries exist than other plates containing as-fabricated powders. Fission gas bubble swelling for these plates is remarkably smaller. In this case, more fission gases contribute to solid fission product swelling by remaining in the nano-bubbles or in the intra-granular regions, not included in SEM-visible bubbles in the grain boundaries.

## 6. Fuel swelling by solid fission products

As discussed earlier, fuel swelling by the smaller bubbles than the SEM resolution limit is small at low fission densities less than 3  1027 fissions/m3 . During recrystallization, some of the nanobubbles re-solve and those gas atoms contribute to form larger bubbles in the inter-granular regions. At this stage, the nano-bubble supperlattice near the grain boundaries will be destroyed by recrystallization and nano-bubbles there coalesce to form SEM-visible bubbles in the inter-granular regions. Both of these two mechanisms enhance gas bubble swelling.





Fuel swelling by solid fission products is estimated using Eq. (11) and data shown in Figs. 9 and 12. The result is shown in Fig. 14. The data for overall fuel swelling, (DV/V0)f, were measured from U–10Mo foil samples, and the data for gas bubble swelling, (DV/V0)g, were obtained mostly from U–10Mo particles dispersed in aluminum. Two samples with U–6Mo and U–7Mo with low burnups were also examined to obtain fission gas bubble swelling. The solid fission product swelling is obtained by subtracting the gas bubble swelling from the overall fuel swelling.





Fuel swelling by solid fission products is linearly dependent on burnup, which is caused by volume difference between fissioned uranium atoms and produced solid fission products (including liquid fission products) that reside in the UMo lattice. Therefore, this type of fuel swelling is largely independent on fuel type and probably temperature also. As seen in Fig. 12, fuel swelling by fission gas bubbles enhances at higher burnup. It is this gas bubble contribution that enhances overall fuel swelling at high burnup.

## 7. Discussion




The temperatures of the samples analyzed for gas bubbles are below -250 -C, and the difference between the lowest and highest temperatures is -70 -C. A systematic trend to plot the effect of temperature was not apparent, probably masked by the large uncertainties in the measured data. With the temperature regime below -250 -C, for which the decomposition to the a + d phases is very slow, the assumption of only athermal swelling appears to be reasonable. However, there have been reports showing a dependence, although weak, on temperature [21,22]. As was discussed elsewhere [1,23,24], fuel swelling by fission gas bubbles is weakly dependent on the temperature at low temperatures because gas atom diffusion relies predominantly on fission enhanced diffusion (FED). The thermally activated diffusion is not important. It is also worth noting that fission rate dependence is not included in the present modeling, instead only fission density is considered. It is our view that the reason for this negligible fission rate effect is due to an effective mutual cancelation between FED that promotes gas bubble growth and gas bubble re-solution by fission fragments.

The SEM images used for gas bubble measurements were taken at the plate center region in width on the fuel cross section cut at the plate mid-region in the length direction (see Fig. 7). This location is away from the plate end region in the width direction where compressive stress is the largest. Due to thin, ductile Al-alloy cladding, external stress at the plate mid region is negligible. In addition, the bubbles were small and less susceptible to the external stress. Therefore, the effect of external stresses on bubble growth, specifically where the data were measured for the present study, is most likely less than measureable. Consequently, the results of the present work are considered to be for stress-free conditions.





All the data for overall fuel swelling were measured from U– 10Mo foil samples, and the data for gas bubble swelling were obtained mostly from U–10Mo particles dispersed in aluminum with several exceptions for U–6Mo and U–7Mo Therefore, the present model is basically applicable U–10Mo. However, until more data are measured that necessitate a more accurate model for other than U–10Mo alloy, the present model can be extended to U–Mo alloys with the Mo-content range of 7–10 wt%.





## 8. Conclusions




Fuel swelling of U–Mo alloy fuel induced by fission products is analyzed. Overall fuel swelling measured by post-irradiation fuel dimensions showed that it is primarily a function of burnup (or fission density) at temperatures below 250 -C. The fuel swelling portion caused by fission gas bubbles contributes to enhance fuel swelling at higher fission density (beyond fission density of -3  1027 fissions/m3 ). The fuel swelling portion by solid fission products is expressed as a linear function of fission density. No discernable effect of temperature on fuel swelling was found for the relatively low temperature data (<250 -C) considered in this work.

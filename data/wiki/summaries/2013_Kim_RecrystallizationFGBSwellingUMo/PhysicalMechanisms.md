# Kim 2013 — Physical Mechanisms: Recrystallization & FGB Swelling in U-Mo

**Source:** [[wiki/summaries/2013_Kim_RecrystallizationFGBSwellingUMo|Kim 2013 Index]]

---

## 1. Two Components of Swelling

U-Mo fuel swelling has two distinct contributions:

- **Solid FP swelling**: proportional to burnup (fission density) only; caused by volume difference between solid/liquid fission products and original U atoms
- **Fission-gas-bubble (FGB) swelling**: depends on fuel composition, fuel type, manufacturing process, and fission rate

## 2. Three-Stage Gas Bubble Evolution

### Stage 1: Pre-recrystallization ($F < \sim3\times10^{21}$ f/cm³)

- Only inter-granular FGBs visible by SEM form at pre-existing grain boundaries
- Only 5–10% of generated fission gas resides in these bubbles
- TEM reveals intra-granular bubbles forming a **[[wiki/entities/GasBubbleSuperlattice|bubble superlattice]]**:
  - At $2.4\times10^{27}$ f/m³: bubble size ~2 nm, lattice constant 6–7 nm, volume increase only 1.3%
  - At $4.5\times10^{27}$ f/m³: bubble size ~3.5 nm, lattice constant 11.5 nm, volume increase 5.9%

### Stage 2: [[wiki/entities/Recrystallization|Recrystallization]] (~$3\times10^{21}$ f/cm³ onset)

- Original micron-sized grains are subdivided into sub-micron new grains
- [[wiki/entities/Recrystallization|Recrystallization]] initiates at pre-existing grain boundaries and advances inward
- Grain boundary area increases dramatically → many new FGB nucleation sites
- Intra-granular bubbles collapse and merge into inter-granular bubbles
- New grain size < 1 μm

### Stage 3: Post-recrystallization

- All grains fully refined
- FGB swelling rate reaches its maximum value

## 3. Physical Mechanism of Recrystallization

[[wiki/entities/Recrystallization|Recrystallization]] is a radiation-induced, **defect-driven process**:
- Fission events produce point and line defects + simultaneous annihilation/dynamic recovery
- Grain refinement via polygonization, cell formation, and subgrain formation
- New grains nucleate at pre-existing grain boundaries in U-Mo
- Analogous to the **rim effect** in UO₂

## A B S T R A C T

A simple, repeatable method for characterization of fission gas bubbles in irradiated U-Mo fuels has been developed. This method involves mechanical potting and polishing of samples along with examination with a scanning electron microscope located outside of a hot cell. The commercially available software packages CellProfiler, MATLAB, and Mathematica are used to segment and analyze the captured images. The results are compared and contrasted. Baseline methods for fission gas bubble characterization are suggested for consideration and further development.

## 1. Introduction

Development and deployment of low enriched uranium (LEU) fuels for research reactors has been pursued for more than thirty years around the world, starting with the Reduced Enrichment for Research and Test Reactor (RERTR) program in the US [1]. The more recently established Materials Management and Minimization Reactor Conversion Program continues these efforts by significantly accelerating the program's national and international nonproliferation objectives. Converting the fuel used in civilian research and test reactors to LEU permanently secures the site by removing the threat posed by continued high-enriched uranium (HEU) operations. Reduction of the enrichment requires an increase in the uranium density of the fuel to provide acceptable performance in reactor, leading to the development of new, higher density dispersion and monolithic fuel plate designs. Uranium alloyed with nominally 10 wt% molybdenum (U-10Mo) is currently being developed as one potential high density LEU fuel to convert highperformance research and test reactors from the use of HEU fuels. In U-10Mo, the molybdenum in uranium stabilizes the cubic gamma phase allowing for acceptable swelling and integrity under irradiation [2–4].

Characterizing the performance of new LEU fuels as a function of burnup and temperature is essential in moving toward final design, fabrication, and qualification for use in existing or new reactors. Fission gas bubbles within the irradiated fuel matrix serve as key indicators of fuel performance. These bubbles contribute to swelling and thermal conductivity degradation and may ultimately be responsible for fuel failure. The size, number, distribution, eccentricity (a measure of bubble tendency to be spherical or lamellar), and orientation (a measure of non-spherical bubbles toward a preferential alignment) of bubbles can be determined from a cross-sectional image of irradiated fuel. These bubble properties provide potential insight into the fission gas evolution within the fuel matrix during irradiation and how this evolution contributes to fuel performance degradation and eventual failure. The Advanced Test Reactor (ATR) Full-size Plate In Center Flux Trap Position (AFIP) experiments were designed to evaluate the performance of monolithic fuels at a scale prototypic of research reactor fuel plates. The AFIP-3 experimental fuel plates consisted of AA6061 clad monolithic fuel plates using a metallic foil of U-10Mo enriched to nominally 19.75% 235U. The metallic fuel foil of interest in this study was prepared by hot co-rolling a U-10Mo alloy ingot with Zr on either side [5]. The nominal thickness of the Zr was 25 μm on either side of the foil and was introduced to minimize interaction between the U-10Mo fuel and the AA6061 cladding, thereby acting as a “diffusion” barrier. The foil was hermetically sealed and bound within the AA6061 cladding using a hot isostatic pressing (HIP) process [6]. Additional details on the various aspects of the AFIP-3 experiment can be found in Perez et al. [7].

Post-irradiation, rectangular segments (roughly 12.5 mm × 27 mm) were taken from the AFIP-3BZ (referred to as Segment E) fuel plates at Idaho National Laboratory (INL) and transferred to Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL) for examination of thermal properties [8]. The analyses performed at PNNL provided information regarding thermal transport and fission gas release on the physical scale of several millimeters [9,10]. Additionally, these analyses provided data and insight for efforts to model microstructural evolution during irradiation and the subsequent impact on thermal properties [11]. However, more detailed information regarding microstructure and fission gas bubble distribution is necessary than was possible to acquire with an optical microscope. In order to obtain this detailed information, potted metallography samples were resurfaced and examined with scanning

electron microscopy (SEM).

Recent additions to the literature have documented efforts to identify and characterize fission gas bubbles in an irradiated U-Mo fuel sample using the open source software CellProfiler and the MATLAB image processing toolbox [12,13]. In these articles, a fair amount of effort was devoted to the removal of sample preparation artifacts and processing of the image taken from the SEM in order to generate an image in which fission gas bubbles could be clearly and reliably identified and characterized. The work presented in this paper builds upon these efforts in an attempt to further develop an acceptable procedure for characterizing fission gas bubble properties and distribution throughout an irradiated fuel matrix.

## 2. Material and Methods

Fuel segment E (TE), taken from the fuel plate irradiated in experiment AFIP-3BZ, was received at PNNL in February 2014. The AFIP-3BZ fuel plate had in initial 235U enrichment of 19.937%, an initial Mo concentration of 10.3 wt%, and was irradiated in the ATR for 101.0 Effective Full Power Days (EFPD) during which it reached an average burnup of 63.5 at.% and a calculated fission density of 4.32 × 1021 fissions per cm3 [8,9]. Segment E was sectioned into smaller pieces at PNNL and each piece was designated for a particular measurement within the suite of examinations necessary for robust thermal analysis (optical metallography, laser flash analysis, gas pycnometry, and differential scanning calorimetry). Of these smaller pieces, two were dedicated to optical metallography; one (TE-OM1) was prepared so that the examination was in the direction longitudinal to rolling during fabrication and one (TE-OM2) was prepared so that the examination was in the direction transverse to rolling during fabrication. Due to the cutting pattern for sectioning fuel segment E, TE-OM2 actually consisted of two small pieces potted together in one metallography sample. It was decided for this work to focus on SEM image analysis of TE-OM1 as it remained a single intact sample. As the analytical methods presented in the paper have not been fully applied to TE-OM2, no conclusive results regarding the influence of rolling direction are currently available. However, preliminary results are available [14]. Fig. 1 shows a low magnification optical microscopy image of TE-OM1 while Fig. 2 shows a higher magnification image of the same sample [8]. In Fig. 1, the entire fuel structure, including the fuel, zirconium “diffusion barrier”, and cladding is shown. In Fig. 2, only a portion of the fuel is shown, but several features of the microstructure are visible. Bands of fission gas bubbles are visible within a recrystallized matrix surrounding intact grains, but it is challenging to reliably differentiate between individual bubbles, necessitating the use of an SEM.

Approximately two years elapsed after the completion of optical analyses for the initial thermal property work during which Fig. 1 and Fig. 2 were generated and the initiation of the SEM examinations discussed in this paper. During that time, the high beta radiation field generated by the fuel significantly degraded the epoxy in which the samples were set. Fig. 3 shows the condition of these samples at the time that the current work was initiated. The epoxy foam that expanded over the samples was hard and had to be removed with 320 grit abrasive. Once the surface was exposed, mechanical polishing for SEM analysis was completed according to the same process that has been described previously [15]. Having re-prepared the surface for imaging, the sample was transferred out of the hot cell and onto an SEM for imaging.

However, the gap between the fuel and the epoxy and this sample allowed for direct examination without the application of such a coating. Proceeding without sample coating offered several advantages, including; simplification of the procedure for sample preparation, lowering exposure time to the sample preparer, elimination of possible artifacts introduced by imperfect coating, ability to transfer the sample directly between optical and electron microscopes, and not introducing a potentially dispersible layer to a sample that had been deemed nondispersible. The sample was grounded to the mounting post with silver paint. For the purposes of this paper, five equally-spaced positions along the midline of the sample were examined at magnifications of 2000×, 5000×, 10,000×, 15,000×, 20,000×, and 25,000×. The distance between these two positions was 7.3 mm, which was less than the 9.1 mm length of sample TE-OM1 reported previously [8]. This difference is due to the 1.8 mm of the sample being covered by silver paint (seen on the right side of the bottom two images in Fig. 4) that was used to ground the sample. Position 3 was located 0.5 mm to the right of Position 1 and Position 7 was located 0.5 mm to the left of Position 2. Positions 4 through 6 were then equally spaced (1.575 mm apart) between Positions 3 and 7. The suite of images taken at all 6 magnifications for Position 3 is shown in Fig. 5.

## 3. Theory/Calculation

As has been discussed previously in the literature [12,13], proper identification and characterization of fission gas bubbles in images such as those presented in Fig. 5 can be difficult with the results potentially being influenced by both the analyst and the software. As such, the purpose of the current work was to attempt to generate methods that are simple and reproducible in order to generate baseline analyses to which future analyses can be compared. It was a main objective to remove to the greatest extent possible any subjectivity and discuss the possible impacts of doing so. While many image analysis software packages are available and new algorithms are constantly in development, the current work focuses on using the CellProfiler and MATLAB image processing toolbox packages investigated in recent publications [12,13] in addition to the Mathematica image processing and analysis suite of tools.

Depending on the quality of the initial image captured from the

SEM, several pre-processing steps may be necessary in order to remove artifacts, remove background data, account for uneven illumination, and optimize contrast. Recently published work indicated that a significant effort was necessary in order to remove curtaining artifacts generated during sample preparation with a focused ion beam (FIB) and account for improper illumination [12,13]. In the current study, these issues could be neglected as the sample preparation with mechanical polishers did not generate curtaining artifacts and performing SEM analysis outside of a hot cell allowed for greater flexibility in instrument control so images with minimal illumination issues could be generated, as demonstrated in Fig. 5. As many of the difficulties in image preprocessing could be neglected, analysis in the current work proceeded by attempting to generate simple and as identical as possible analysis techniques in CellProfiler, MATLAB, and Mathematica. The lists of commands or modules used for comparative analysis in this paper are presented in Table 1.

While the lists of commands in Table 1 for CellProfiler and MATLAB are longer than that for Mathematica, the operations on the original SEM image being performed by each software package are essentially the same. The first four modules listed for CellProfiler are shaded as they are input modules and necessary for any pipeline, while the final six modules are analysis modules. Additionally, the list of parameter settings that must be specified by the analyst within each module in the pipeline is extensive [14].

The first four (input) modules listed for CellProfiler accomplish the same task as the first command for MATLAB and Mathematica, which is simply to import the raw SEM image into the software package. After this, all three software packages crop the image in order to remove the data associated with the image that is generally listed at the bottom by the SEM software. If this information is not removed from the image, it will disrupt the process of determining the appropriate threshold value for image segmentation due to the presence of pure black in the background and pure white in the letters. Subsequently, the image is converted to grayscale and the intensity threshold for segmentation is determined. The image is segmented and inverted (black pixels are turned white and white pixels are turned black). Fission gas bubbles are then identified as groupings of white pixels in contact with one another and completely separated from other white pixels by surrounding black pixels. Finally, the population of identified fission gas bubbles is characterized for comparison with other images.

Each software package has built-in algorithms for characterizing the population of identified objects (fission gas bubbles in this case). In the present work, the characteristics that are investigated are 1) number of bubbles, 2) bubble size, 3) bubble orientation, and 4) bubble eccentricity. The first two characteristics are straight forward as the number of objects identified and the number of pixels comprising each object are direct products of the image identification process. The bubble orientation and bubble eccentricity are both more complex as they are based on the concept of a best-fit ellipse.

In order to understand the results generated from each software package, it is important to identify the methods for generating and interpreting characteristics of best-fit ellipses. Determining the exact numerical process that each software package uses for the determination of best-fit is not straight-forward. The user manuals for each software package lack an explicit definition of a best-fit ellipse. Insight into this issue can only be gained by the given definitions for the characteristics of interest as are listed in Table 2.

The definitions for Eccentricity and Orientation within CellProfiler and MATLAB appear to be the same with the best-fit ellipse being defined as the ellipse with the same second-moments as the defined region. On the other hand, Mathematica only defines the parameters in terms of an undefined best-fit ellipse. Likely, in all cases, a numerical minimization method is used to generate a best-fit ellipse that has the same second central-moment. In addition to the modifications suggested in Table 4, additional changes are necessary due to the method of reporting values for each software package. Specifically, the second and third objects must be switched in order for the results generated by MATLAB to match those of CellProfiler and Mathematica. Object order is not important for analysis of average values, but is in the direct comparison of individual objects as is done in Table 3 and Table 5. Additionally, the values for the semiaxes must be divided by 2 for the results generated by Cell-Profiler and MATLAB in order to match the half-values presented by Mathematica. With these adjustments, as can be seen in Table 5, good agreement exists for all values generated for these parameters by the three software packages, with the exception of the values of the semiaxes and eccentricity. This exception is most likely due to different numerical algorithms being employed by each software system in order to determine the best-fit ellipse for each object. These results are shown graphically in Fig. 7. Thus, after the modifications discussed are implemented, the results for number of objects, object size, and object orientation can be interpreted quantitatively, while the results for eccentricity should be interpreted qualitatively until the algorithm for determining the best-fit ellipse by the software package being used is fully understood.

The average bubble size has been converted to equivalent spherical diameter as this is a more accepted unit in this field of study. Mathematica automatically determines the “EquivalentDiskRadius” within its “ComponentMeasurements” function. This equivalent disk radius is calculated by determining the radius of a circle that has the same area as the bubble. The equivalent disk diameter was determined by multiplying the equivalent disk radius by 2. As the images are 2-dimensional, it is not directly possible to directly determine the equivalent spherical diameter. However, assuming that the equivalent disk were to be rotated through Pi radians to generate a sphere, that sphere would have the same diameter as the equivalent disk. As such, the equivalent disk diameter is presented as the equivalent spherical diameter in Fig. 9. For consistency, the width of each size bin used in the development of each distribution shown in Fig. 10 is 0.1 μm.

## 4. Discussion

The methods and results discussed in the previous sections demonstrate the effectiveness of a simple method for analyzing fission gas bubbles in irradiated U-Mo fuel for the purpose of baseline analysis across various researchers in the field. However, as alluded to, further discussion of these methods and results are warranted. The first topic of discussion is the outlying datapoint in Fig. 9 for average eccentricity at a magnification of 25,000× at position 7. Upon further inspection of this image, it was discovered that there was an artifact in the image acquisition and that removal of this artifact resulted in an average eccentricity value for this image of 0.705 instead of 0.759. This image with and without the artifact is shown in Fig. 11. This dropped the outlying data point back into a value more consistent with the trend of the other data points as shown in Fig. 12. Fig. 12 also provides a comparison of the average eccentricity and average orientation values for each position and location for the two cases 1) all bubbles are considered and 2) only bubbles with areas greater than 1 pixel are considered. The results show that even if the artifact is removed from the 25,000× magnification image at position 7, the eccentricity continues to trend upward if bubbles comprised of a single pixel are not considered. Also, the average eccentricity values become more consistent across magnifications with the removal of single pixel bubbles. Additionally, removal of single pixel bubbles has little effect on the average orientation values other than to drive the values for low magnifications further from zero. These trends in average eccentricity and orientation are due to the fact that all single pixel bubbles by definition have orientations and eccentricities of zero.

There are two interesting observations to be made by comparing these images with the results shown in Fig. 12. First, the average orientation of the bubbles appears to directly correlate to the orientation channels of recrystallized matrix in which the bubbles reside. This is uniquely observable due to one particular channel comprising the majority of the areas of the images presented in Fig. 13. In lower magnification images, many more channels are visible, acting to cancel out the effect of bubble-channel alignment. Additionally, in other high magnification images, single directional channels are not present. The second observation is that the eccentricity of the bubbles in the image from position 5 is affected by long, stretched bubbles occurring right at the edge of the intact matrix/grains within the recrystallized matrix. While these bubbles appear in the image from position 6 as well, they are more prevalent in the image from position 5, acting to drive the average bubble eccentricity value up.

For the most part, the trends in the results presented in Fig. 9 are expected. The number of objects in each image for a given position decreased steadily as the magnification increased. This is the case when one-pixel objects are included and when they were neglected in the analyses. A similar trend, although less linear, is seen for the average object equivalent spherical diameter. The trend in the average object eccentricity is the inverse of that of the average equivalent spherical diameter. The trend in the average object orientation is not the same for the different positions examined as are the trends for the other parameters. Instead, the trend is that as the magnification increases, the spread between the average orientation values for each position increases.

One observation that can be seen in the average equivalent spherical diameter is that there is a large jump from 2000× magnification to 5000× magnification. Comparatively, this value flattens out to a nearly constant value at all magnifications between 5000× and 25,000×, but with a consistent, small downward trend. This could be caused by a much larger area being analyzed, allowing for more of the rarer, exceptionally large objects to be captured, contributing to the larger size. Additionally, the presence of image artifacts can have greater effects on lower magnification images (fewer pixels per object). Another explanation is that if a portion of the object takes up a portion of a pixel and the pixel is assigned to the object, more pixel space that is not actually part of the object can be assigned to the object when pixels represent larger spaces (as is the case for lower magnification images). These hypotheses can be investigated with the image analysis below, which indicate that the third option is the most likely. One way to investigate this is to use “Area” instead of “Count” to determine object size in Mathematica, but this was not investigated here as the algorithm is not openly defined and utilizing it goes against the primary objective of this investigation. The “Count” option counts the number of pixels assigned to the object after segmentation, while the “Area” option determines the approximate area of the object, where each pixel area is weighted by its neighborhood configuration [18]. A similar option is available in MATLAB with “Area” counting the number of pixels associated with an object and “bwarea” weighting different patterns of pixels differently [17].

In order to investigate the plausibility of each of these options, the 2000× magnification image at position 3 is examined in more detail in Fig. 14. There are what appear to be two surface preparation artifacts that lead to large objects in the segmentation of the image. These artifacts are indicated with red circles. Additionally, there is a truly large bubble in the image that is captured appropriately in the segmentation process that is indicated by the blue circle. The two large objects in the binary image generated by the artifacts are identified within the list of objects created by the segmentation project by their size. In order to verify that the two largest objects are indeed those caused by the artifacts they are plotted by themselves along with the full image with these two objects removed in Fig. 15. The results of removing these two large objects from the object analysis process (the pixels assigned to these objects were simply converted from white to black, effectively making them part of the background) are shown in Table 7. As can be seen, removal of these artifacts has little impact on the results. Larger artifacts must be present in order to present a true issue.

As the artifacts are shown to have minimal impact, there are two remaining causes in the larger than expected object sizes in the 2000× magnification images. There are some bubbles in the images in Fig. 14 that are well above the mean size; one is directly indicated by the blue circle. However, as these are smaller than the objects created by the artifacts and not overly abundant, it is unlikely that their presence is the cause of the large average object values for the low magnification images. It is most likely that the third and final option is the culprit. The fraction of pixels that are assigned to objects that are not actually part of the objects (due to lack of resolution) causes this phenomenon. The fact that the jump in size from 5000× to 2000× magnification is observed for all 5 positions supports this conclusion.

The average eccentricity of the bubbles in each image, regardless of position and magnification is fairly constant, especially when 1-pixel objects are neglected. It makes sense that the eccentricity increases the most for 2000× magnification images when one-pixel objects are neglected, as a one-pixel object has an eccentricity of zero, and the most one-pixel objects occur in the 2000× magnification images. The values of the orientation seem to vary greatly as a function of position and magnification. This is likely an indication of a fairly random distribution in bubble orientation. However, the average orientation does tend to be fairly low in all cases, indicating either that not many elongated bubbles are situated in a vertical configuration, or that just as many bubbles that are configured with a high orientation are configured with a low orientation. The removal of the one-pixel objects from analysis causes the absolute value of average orientation to increase, as the orientation of a one-pixel object is zero.

While the average values for parameters of interest are significant as presented above, additional information can be gleaned by observing data gained from the processing of segmented images in other formats such as the log normal equivalent spherical diameter distributions presented in Fig. 10. As mentioned previously, these analyses require further analyst action which the effort described in this paper strove to minimize. In order to fit data to a distribution, it is first necessary to place the values acquired from the image into size bins as discussed in the previous section. The selection of the size of the bin can affect the fitting parameters associated with the final associated distribution. For the distributions presented in Fig. 10, bins with widths of 0.1 μm were used. This bin sizing for distribution determination was not automated, but was kept consistent for the present analysis. Further investigation into an automated determination of optimal bin sizing is suggested for further work. Additionally, it should be noted that in the current work, no consideration was given to correlating the results gathered from the two-dimensional analysis to the three-dimensional properties of the samples that were investigated. These two endeavors: optimal binning and mapping from two-dimensional characterization to three-dimensional characterization are of great interest and impact and insights can be gathered from existing literature [20–22]. The objective of the current work was to provide a simple baseline approach to fission gas bubble analysis that could serve as a launching pad for such further development.

## 5. Conclusions

This paper presents a proposed baseline method with minimal analyst intervention for providing analysis of fission gas bubbles in irradiated U-Mo fuels. The full method involves sample preparation and analysis, and it is understood that it may be divided along these lines for implementation as preparation of smaller samples with focused ion beams allows for limitations of dose to analysts. However, the authors do point out that the samples prepared with mechanical methods presented here do not exhibit the curtaining effects that cause a great effort in pre-analysis filtering discussed previously in the literature. The preparation of non-dispersable, polished samples encased in epoxy allows for removal of samples from hot cells where they can be handled with shielding and extension tools. These samples do not exhibit curtaining effects and can be immediately subjected to the analysis methods presented here.

![](images/314a3799e32048ff2c89556bb1ba4b3ca18638b4868f6a2543c37abcc152c764.jpg)  
Fig. 15. Visual demonstration of removal of two artifacts.

Three software packages were examined for sample analysis in the paper. It was determined that the methods of MATLAB and Mathematica were well aligned, while those of CellProfiler varied somewhat. It is likely that this deviation can be corrected if sufficiently investigated. However, as the purpose of this work was to determine a baseline pathway of sample analysis that minimizes analyst intervention, it is concluded that MATLAB or Mathematica provide a more direct pathway to that end. The standard image analysis tool sets available in each of these software packages allows for rapid analysis with no modification of the automatic values set within them. The conversion of results from units associated with pixels to physical parameters such as equivalent spherical diameter introduces steps that could potentially contain errors, but analyst involvement is minimal. The fitting of these parameters to distributions, such as the log normal distributions presented here for bubble size distribution introduces a somewhat subjective decision on the part of the analyst to determine the size of the bins used for distribution fitting. It is recommended that such bin sizes be reported with any analysis so that work can be reproduced by other researchers.

It is important, as discussed in this paper, that analysts carefully consider and investigate the methods used by the software package of choice to ensure that the resulting values from analysis carry the prescribed meaning. For instance, while all software packages discussed in this paper defined eccentricity and orientation in the same way, the values they generated differed somewhat. The simple tests presented here allowed for determining the meaning of the results. However, even though the values are well understood, it is not clear that they are the most appropriate results. For example, the final method of determining orientation presented here uses a value of −90° for the orientation of all bubbles whose equivalent ellipse is vertically oriented. Perhaps assigning these bubbles an orientation of 0° would be more appropriate as it would average out those with an orientation of 90° and those with an orientation of −90°. Still, the current analysis does present results that are useful for physical interpretation as evidenced through Fig. 13.

While the authors feel that the work presented in this paper provides a useful and valid proposal for baseline analysis of fission gas bubbles in irradiated U-Mo fuels, it is acknowledged that no such method will ever be truly devoid of analyst intervention and interpretation. The method does make it clearer to locate image artifacts and process images. The results from this image processing will ultimately have to be reviewed carefully for final interpretation. However, adhering to a well-defined baseline method that minimizes subjective analyst interpretation prior to additional presentation of analyst interpretation would provide a useful calibration for these results among the various groups currently working in this field.

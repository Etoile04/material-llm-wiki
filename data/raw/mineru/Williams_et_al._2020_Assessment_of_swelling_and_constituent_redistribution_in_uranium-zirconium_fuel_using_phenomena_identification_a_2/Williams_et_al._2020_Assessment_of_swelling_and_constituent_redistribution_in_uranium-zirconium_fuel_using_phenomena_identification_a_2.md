# Assessment of Swelling and Constituent Redistribution in Uranium-Zirconium Fuel Using Phenomena Identification and Ranking Tables (PIRT)

## Abstract

Metallic alloy U-Zr nuclear fuels remain a candidate for future US fast reactors. However, with more than 30 years of investigation into the system, U-Zr fuel remains unqualified and lacks predictive modeling capabilities. Phenomenon identification and ranking tables (PIRT) were developed for U-Zr fuels to prioritize the study of microstructural phenomena related to fuel performance and to aid predictive models. Two areas of emphasis, fuel swelling and constituent redistribution, were identified with PIRT and were evaluated in detail. The analysis identified influencing parameters that should be considered during future studies including: external mechanical constraint, temperature, composition, crystallographic texture, temperature gradient, fabrication constraints, fission density, fission rate, initial porosity, and pre-irradiation grain size. The PIRT results support the need for a comprehensive study that focus on the decoupling of fission rate, irradiation temperature, and alloy composition to independently investigate the microstructural evolution in the multiple phases typical of advanced reactor conditions.

Keywords: U-Zr, Constituent Redistribution, Fuel Swelling, PIRT

## 1. Introduction

Nuclear fuel is the most critically engineered component in a nuclear reactor. This component is subjected to the most severe operating environment of any reactor component and thus has the shortest operating lifespan. This operating environment includes a confluence of high and cycling temperatures, mechanical forces, irradiation damage, and the accumulation of fission products that significantly alter the system’s chemistry; all of which influence the fuel’s performance limitations. These performance limits, along with a safety margin, are typically reflected in the overall design and operating constraints for the entire reactor system. Systematic studies of the fundamental physical behaviors occurring within the fuel may lead to an improved mechanistic understanding of the dominant fuel performance criteria and, thus, optimization of a given design and allow for a fuel design to reach appreciably higher burnups.

Nuclear fuel designs based on metallic fuel alloys have long been a focus of development for sodium cooled fast reactor applications. Intrinsic beneficial characteristics of metal alloy-based fuels include ease of fabrication, thermal-physical properties, and high fissile density that offers unique fuel performance, reactor safety, and fuel cycle attributes (Kaity et al., 2012). A wide variety of designs have been proposed and developed to optimize this fuel system for specific applications (Hofman et al., 1997). The most common fuel type, and the focus of this paper, is the uranium-zirconium (U-Zr) alloy; which was used extensively in the operation of the Experimental Breeder Reactor-II (EBR-II) and Fast Flux Test Facility (FFTF) in Idaho Falls, Idaho, U.S. (Burkes et al., 2009; Pitner and Baker, 1993; Walters, 1999).

U-Zr (or U-Pu-Zr) based alloys are the primary candidate fuel for future U.S. fast reactors as well as other emerging advanced reactor technologies. Therefore, it is important to establish a more comprehensive understanding of U-Zr fuel performance such that models and experiments can be coupled together in a manner that enables the fuel qualification process to be shortened (Chang, 1989; Wade and Chang, 1988). This is highlighted by the fact that thousands of U-Zr fuel pins were irradiated in EBR-II over the course of 30 years where the qualified envelope for use reached 10 atom percent (at%) burnup and demonstrated to 20 at% burnup (Crawford et al., 2007; Pahl et al., 1990; Walters, 1999). These historic irradiations focused predominantly on fuel performance phenomena, such as fuel swelling or fission gas release as a function of fission density in the EBR-II specific configuration and operating environment. Due to the fact that most of the test pins were irradiated under similar environmental conditions, many of the forces controlling microstructural evolution were not separable such that the final alloy microstructure, thermal, or mechanical properties could not be directly related to the initial alloy microstructure, fission rate, fluence, or irradiation temperature. This convolution also prevents a fundamental understanding of these relationships and phenomena (Hofman et al., 1997; Janney and Hayes, 2018), which in turn prevents the ability to reliably predict performance at higher burnups (\~30- 40 at%) or new configurations that may be required for different reactor designs (including pin diameter, length, linear heat generation rate, smear densities etc.) without extensive parametric testing.

The microstructure of U-Zr alloys evolves during irradiation through constituent redistribution, porosity growth, and phase changes (Harp et al., 2017; Pahl et al., 1990; Thomas et al., 2019). These changes can have an impact on the engineering performance of a fuel system by altering the physical properties, such as thermal conductivity, hardness, solidus temperature, etc. over time. The past ability of fuel engineers to provide mechanistic explanations and validating

computational models have been hindered by the complexity of the reactor operating environment, the difficulty of controlling and isolating operational parameters in prototypic irradiations, and multiple microstructural phenomena that concurrently exist and evolve within the fuel during irradiation (e.g. constituent redistribution, swelling, fission product generation, phase transformations, and irradiation-induced defect formation). These explanations and models are desired to guide fuel design decisions and qualification. Nuclear fuel licensing requires the demonstration of the fuel to meet basic regulatory requirements for radionuclide retention and mechanical performance under the full range of postulated environmental conditions (ranging from normal operation to accident scenarios). In general, this requires that the fuel exhibits stable and predictable fuel performance without the loss of mechanical integrity. Currently, these fuel tests are performed experimentally, requiring tens of years of fuel fabrication, irradiation, and post-irradiation examination, before demonstrating an acceptable operational parameter space for the fuel system.

This research employs phenomena identification and ranking tables (PIRT) to identify knowledge gaps in the U-Zr fuel system during in-pile irradiation that may be investigated to guide future experiments to assist in de-convoluting irradiation parameters, improving predictive modeling capabilities, and therefore potentially reducing the decades of research required to qualify a new fuel.

## 2. Phenomena Identification and Ranking Tables (PIRT) Process

The PIRT process has evolved since its inception and has now become a more generalized method to aid in a quantitative decision-making process using qualitative inputs. PIRT analysis is a method to identify phenomena and their possible causes, or source variables, in order to address the phenomena through future research and/or design. This analysis ranks the identified sources in terms of correlation of a variable with a phenomenon and current understanding of the correlation in order to explore the phenomena in a guided and strategic manner (Diamond, 2006; Wilson and Boyack, 1998). While the PIRT process has been routinely used in the investigation of accident scenarios, it has also been applied to material behaviors or phenomena of interest (Boyack et al., 2001; Larson et al., 2007; Moris, 2004; Singh et al., 2019; Wilson and Boyack, 1998). This current study utilized the PIRT process to investigate in-pile U-Zr fuel phenomena for knowledge gaps, or areas in which future research would have a high impact. This scheme used for this PIRT process is outline below:

1. Identify phenomenon that influence fuel behavior and limit fuel performance.

2. Define the parameters (e.g microstructural characteristics and reactor conditions) that influence these phenomena.

3. Determine or estimate the strength of the influencing parameters on the phenomenon

4. Estimate how well their influence is understood.

5. Rank these estimations to guide further experimental, analytical, and computational studies.

In order to investigate phenomena in U-Zr fuels subjected to neutron irradiation using the PIRT process, fourteen subject matter experts were chosen to participate. This panel was comprised of members from various national laboratories and universities from multiple countries with specific expertise in the metallic alloy nuclear fuels community including design, fabrication, irradiation, fuel performance, characterization, and modeling. (Additional details are provided in the Acknowledgements). The panel identified the performance limiting phenomena observed in the U-Zr alloy system and the parameters affecting the phenomena. Parameters were defined as either in-pile conditions or material properties. Following the PIRT analysis, highly ranked parameters were prioritized for further investigation. This PIRT process and its results are summarized herein, including a discussion of the identified parameters.

## 2.1. Identification and Selection of Phenomena

The baseline U-Zr fuel system evaluated during this PIRT process is the fuel pin design, fabricated through injection casting or extrusion, that was used extensively in the operation of EBR-II (Carmack et al., 2009; Pahl et al., 1992). A typical geometry for EBR-II metallic fuel pins consisted of a sodium bonded 0.439 cm diameter fuel slug in 0.76 cm thick HT9 cladding. The fuel was fabricated with a pre-existing void space to accommodate fuel swelling; the cross sectional area filled by the fuel is defined as the smear density and was typically near 75% (Lahm et al., 1993).

The U-Zr fuel systems used in fast reactor applications have four main phenomena that limit fuel performance: swelling, fuel constituent redistribution, fuel-cladding chemical interaction (FCCI), and fuel-cladding mechanical interaction (FCMI) (Hofman et al., 1997; Karahan and Andrews, 2013; Kittel et al., 1993). Each phenomenon may influence structural integrity, thermal properties, and/or radionuclide retention in a fuel life-limiting manner. Exploration of the fundamental phenomena that dictate bulk fuel behavior are critical to the fuel development process. Each of these phenomena are summarized below in Sections 2.2 to 2.5. Figure 1 illustrates a flow chart of the main phenomenon identified, as well as how they manifest and their interconnectivity in the U-Zr system. Note that the right side shown in blue indicates the fuel behavior and the left side shown in yellow indicates the cladding behavior.

## 2.2. Fuel Swelling

Fuel swelling is the result of the gaseous and solid fission products that accumulate within the fuels during the residence time of the fuel in the reactor. Fuel swelling may stress the cladding and threaten its mechanical integrity, limit radionuclide retention, reduce coolability due to a reduction in thermal conductivity, and cause a loss in reactivity due density reduction of the fuel. There are three main driving forces for fuel swelling: solid and gaseous fission product accumulation, mechanical cavitation from anisotropic crystal growth, and defect-induced growth from interstitial and vacancy loops (Pahl et al., 1990). Under thermal conditions relevant to fast reactor applications, 550-800 °C (Galloway et al., 2015; Kramer et al., 1993), U-Zr fuels undergo rapid swelling until approximately 2 at% burnup that is driven by fission gas generation (Hofman et al., 1990; Ogata et al., 2012; Rest, 2012, 1993). The majority of radial growth takes place during a lower burnup interval (\~1-2 at%) before the fuel slug contacts the cladding and fills the void between the two (Hofman et al., 1990). The size of this initial void area (defined by the smear density) is carefully selected to mitigate cladding stress. The axial fuel elongation can range widely, as it is limited by the fuel-cladding contact induced sheer stress which varies with smear density (Hofman et al., 1990). The U-Zr fuel swells anisotropically with the preferential

swelling direction occurring radially, as opposed to axially. A smear density of 75% or lower allows for \~30% swelling before fuel-cladding contact, which is the threshold assumed for fission gas porosity interlinkage after which fission gas is released to the plenum, thus slowing the swelling rate (Pahl et al., 1990). Solid fission products have been found to induce volumetric swelling of approximately a 1.5% swelling increase per 1 at% burnup (Ogata et al., 2012).

Fuel swelling is a concern because it is unavoidable and can result in a loss of thermal conductivity, reduction in local reactivity, and causes FCMI if not properly accommodated for. Porosity and void formation reduce the thermal conductivity by acting as micro-gas gaps that subsequently lower the local reactivity by decreasing the local uranium density. This reduction of thermal conductivity increases the fuel’s centerline temperature and hence, increasing the temperature gradient from the cladding to the fuel center (Chen and Bai, 2018; Ondracek and Schulz, 1973). Decreased thermal conductivity and larger temperature gradients can also result in more FCCI due to the increase in temperature at the fuel’s periphery.

Historic testing of U-Zr fuels was done to support the engineering scale operation of the EBR-II fast neutron source, rather than for fundamental research. Due to this, there remains an opportunity to further the understanding of porosity generation and growth on a mechanistic level (Pahl et al., 1990). As such, fuel swelling was chosen for the PIRT analysis due to the numerous impacts on fuel behavior and the overall lack of fundamental swelling knowledge that still exists.

## 2.3. Constituent Redistribution

In U-Zr based fuel systems utilized for SFRs there is an elemental redistribution/diffusion of U and Zr, known as constituent redistribution, that occurs due to both the thermal and compositional gradients within the fuel (Hofman et al., 1996; Mohanty et al., 2011; Ogawa et al., 1991). A thermal gradient exists from the high-temperature fuel center, resulting from fission, to the lower temperature cladding which is in contact with the coolant. The decreasing thermal conductivity throughout irradiation increases the thermal gradient further (Chen and Bai, 2018; Ondracek and Schulz, 1973). In the presence of the temperature and concentration gradients, Zr diffuses up the temperature gradient towards the hotter center (Carmack et al., 2009; Galloway et al., 2015; Harp et al., 2017; Ishida et al., 1993; Kim et al., 2006). This thermomigration results in a Zr-rich center, a Zr depleted middle ring, and a partial Zr enrichment of the outer ring region adjacent to the cladding. This redistribution is thought to be accelerated by irradiation. Since the fuel temperature and reactor power are coupled, the presence, size, and number of these redistributed zones varies over different irradiation conditions and fuel compositions. Constituent redistribution can cause a U-10wt.%Zr fuel to be redistributed from its initial alloy concentration to regions consisting of nearly pure U to regions with nearly 40 wt.% Zr; (Hofman et al., 1996; Kim et al., 2006; Sohn et al., 2000). Compositional inhomogeneity in the fuel system due to Zr redistribution will result in different phase concentrations, crystal structures (e.g. phases), thermal-physical properties, grain size, and intergranular structure. These changes can in turn affect other properties, behaviors, and irradiation parameters, including temperature, thermal conductivity, and local power density. As a result of these attributes affecting fuel performance, constituent redistribution was identified as the second candidate for the PIRT analysis.

## 2.4. Fuel-Clad Chemical Interaction

Interdiffusion at the interface between the fuel and cladding can reduce the solidus temperature of the fuel, as well as degrade the mechanical performance of the cladding. This fuel-cladding chemical interaction (FCCI) is caused by the diffusion of fuel and cladding constituents into one another in either direction. The first diffusion pathway originates within the fuel system and migrates into the cladding, where both U and the lanthanide series fission products diffuse into the cladding (Kim et al., 2017). This results in changes to the mechanical properties of the cladding by increasing the hardness and causing embrittlement of the cladding. The second diffusion pathway is the migration of cladding constituents into the fuel (Hofman et al., 1997; Matthews et al., 2017; Pahl et al., 1990). In steel cladding, the primary elements of concern are Ni and Fe, which diffuse into the outer ring of the fuel that is in contact with the cladding. These elements lower the solidus temperature of the fuel (Hofman et al., 1997; Ogata et al., 2013; Pahl et al., 1993). As such, both of these diffusion pathways can occur during normal, off-normal, and transient conditions, where lower solidus temperatures and cladding embrittlement are of concern. The cladding degradation is also a concern for steady-state conditions through the loss of mechanical integrity (Kaity et al., 2012).

Fuel-cladding chemical interaction in U-Zr systems, particularly those with traditionally used HT-9 steel cladding, is a life-limiting phenomenon. While various claddings and cladding diffusion barriers can help to mitigate FCCI, it remains a fuel performance concern (Aitkaliyeva et al., 2015; Kaity et al., 2012; Yang et al., 2010). FCCI has been investigated for over two decades and a critical review of the topic can be found in (Matthews et al., 2017). Recent efforts have focused on FCCI mitigation techniques by the introduction of various elements into the fuel that reduce lanthanide mobility (Benson et al., 2018, 2017; Xie et al., 2018). Other efforts included the introduction of diffusion barriers at the interface of the fuel and cladding or dopants to limit lanthanide mobility (Keiser, 2012; Mariani et al., 2011; Ryu et al., 2009; Xie et al., 2018).

## 2.5. Fuel-Clad Mechanical Interaction

Fuel-cladding mechanical interaction (FCMI), is the deformation at the fuel-cladding interface due to irradiation induced swelling and/or plenum gas pressure (Pahl et al., 1990). Since fuel swelling is largely unavoidable as it is intrinsic to fission events, FCMI was one of the first historic problems to be addressed in this system. FCMI was mitigated by the introduction of a larger plenum and the reduction of fuel smear densities to below 80% (Ogata et al., 2012). These changes allowed the fuel to swell to the point of fission gas bubble interconnection, at which point fuel swelling dramatically slows due to direct fission gas release to the pin plenum, hence reducing excessive strain on the cladding until plenum pressure limits are reached (Carmack et al., 2009). With the implementation of larger smear densities, FCMI is not currently life-limiting during steady state operation for burnups up to 10 at% (Hofman, 1980); however, it may become important during off-normal overpower conditions or with the achievement of higher burnups.

Research in all four of the phenomena identified (fuel swelling, fuel constituent redistribution, FCCI, and FCMI) is important to support modern fuel development, design, and licensing. However, the current PIRT analysis specifically evaluated fuel swelling and constituent redistribution. FCCI and FCMI may be evaluated in future work; a PIRT analysis was not performed on these areas beyond the aforementioned discussion.

## 4. Discussion of PIRT Identified Source Variables

Source variables that were identified by the PIRT process are subsequently implied to have an influence on the constituent redistribution or fuel swelling phenomena. A discussion of the relationships of the highly ranked source variables with fuel swelling and constituent redistribution is provided below. This discussion is categorized by the family of source variable identified in Table 1 (external, thermodynamic, compositional, microstructural, and neutronic sources) and further subdivided by the phenomena of interest.

## 4.1.1. External Sources’ Impact on Fuel Swelling

External mechanical constraint, or stress imparted on the U-Zr fuel from cladding or internal plenum pressure, was identified as the highest weighted source variable for both fuel-swelling and constituent redistribution. The impact of external mechanical constraint on fuel performance has been assessed through the investigation of various smear densities. These investigations were predominantly performed to reduce the severity of FCMI early in life and extrapolated to higher burnups (Chichester et al., 2012). However, the data has been used to evaluate other specific fuel performance attributes. One such evaluation was how shear stress between the cladding and fuel affected fuel swelling (Hofman et al., 1990). As the fuel comes into contact with the cladding, there is a compressive stress applied radially as well as a shear stress applied axially. It was found that sheer stress of the cladding-fuel contact surface limits axial fuel swelling, or fuel column elongation, (Hofman et al., 1990). This demonstrated that the mechanical constraints can have an influence on swelling and swelling anisotropy. Novel fuel designs, such as slotted fuel, which is in contact with the cladding prior to irradiation (Golovchenko, 2013, 2011), should take into account the shear stress and time to fuel-cladding contact when investigating fuel swelling, specifically in relation to calculating nominal smear densities.

Pahl and Walters studied fuel system’s performance with respect to porosity growth and swelling by reviewing experiments with known stresses (e.g. known dimensional changes and diametral strain) on the cladding (Pahl et al., 1992; Walters et al., 1984). The experiments that were drawn upon isolated plenum-fuel size ratio, cladding, cladding strength, and smear densities from one another. These comparisons were used to select new claddings (HT9) and decide upon plenumto-fuel ratios (>1) and smear densities (\~75). However, opportunities to further understand relationships between mechanical constraints and fuel swelling still remain, such as the development of fundamental equations coupling fission gas retention to cladding stress or plenum pressure. Recent studies have focused on evaluating lower smear density fuel performance, using the Advanced Test Reactor at Idaho National Laboratory, where direct correlations between external mechanical constraints and fuel swelling may be observed or measured (Carmack et al., 2017; Chichester et al., 2016; Harp et al., 2018a; Hayes et al., 2016; Hofman et al., 2012; Porter et al., 2015). The effects of mechanical constraints on swelling appear to be significant in overall fuel performance, but there are a multitude of historic and current experiments that may be drawn upon to help guide future research, develop novel fuel designs, and improve fuel performance models.

## 4.1.2. External Sources’ Impact on Constituent Redistribution

The highest weighted source variable for constituent redistribution was also external mechanical constraint. Modest microstructural comparisons of both historic and modern tests were previously performed using scanning electron and optical microscopy. Such data has been used in the past for the development of irradiation behavior analysis codes for metallic fuels (Ogata et al., 2012; Ogata and Yokoo, 1999), where mechanical constraint is shown to have an influence on creep, fission gas induced porosity growth, and swelling. Extensive comparisons between mechanical constraint and constituent redistribution were not found in literature, however, the data from existing reports and journal articles may be sufficient for a preliminary investigation into mechanical constraint’s possible influences on constituent redistribution. It is also of note that while FCMI has been mitigated to the point that it is no longer a limiting factor in metallic fuel lifetime for current design applications below 20 at% burnup, it may become more important or a limiting factor as solid fission products continue to accumulate and migrate within the fuel (Carmack et al., 2009; Ogata et al., 2012). This mechanical interaction will result in an additional mechanical constraint on the fuel system and its effects on the fuel system’s microstructural evolution may need to be re-approached as fuel lifetimes increase to significantly higher values.

## 4.2.A. Thermodynamic Sources’ Impact on Fuel Swelling

The fuel temperature and temperature gradient were identified as highly ranking weighted source variables for fuel swelling. As discussed in section 2.2, swelling is attributed to fission product accumulation, mechanical cavitation, and defect-induced growth. Fission gas induced porosity and cavitation growth have been shown to be a function of temperature and composition (Karahan, 2017; Ondracek and Schulz, 1973; Rest, 1993; Thomas et al., 2019, 2018), which would imply a direct correlation with fuel swelling. Recent research quantified the swelling that was specific to unique phase regions in neutron irradiated U-10Zr (Thomas et al., 2019, 2018). Recall that the differing concentric phase regions are attributed to both thermal and concentration gradients within the fuel, hence having a direct impact on swelling.

It has also been shown that the cavitation voids forming on grain boundaries due to irradiationinduced defects increases the swelling rate beyond that achievable with fission-induced swelling alone. The ability for these defects to nucleate is directly correlated to fuel temperature (Rest, 1993); as such, temperature may be more critical to fuel performance at low burnup, where cavitation plays a larger role in fuel performance. As the fission gas induced porosity becomes interconnected the impact of temperature on the microstructure, and thus, impact on the fuel performance, may lessen. For higher burnup fuels, it is hypothesized that there will be a continuous decay and formation of interconnected porosity due to solid fission product induced swelling (Pahl et al., 1986), thus temperature would retain a moderate impact on fuel performance through a constant influence on the microstructural and porosity evolution.

Evidence of isolated temperature effects were demonstrated using out-of-pile calorimetric studies on unirradiated material, where cooling rates directly determine the microstructure and allow the understanding of phase transformation kinetics (Eckelmeyer et al., 1984; Irukuvarghula et al., 2016; Rai et al., 2015). This difficulty in the determination of temperature driven microstructure is also present with reactor cooling rates following irradiation, further complicating the understanding of in pile-behavior. While the fuel swelling dependencies on temperature are apparent, there are remaining opportunities to fully understand the exact impact by deconvoluting other variables. For example, there are codependent variables such as fission rate and composition where fission rate controls the amount of heat present in the system and composition can control the thermal conductivity.

## 4.2.B. Thermodynamic Sources’ Impact on Constituent Redistribution

The fuel temperature and temperature gradient were also identified as highly ranking source variables for constituent redistribution. Extensive calorimetric and temperature related studies on the U-Zr system have been performed on unirradiated and irradiated material, contrasting the low KR values in this study (Ahn et al., 2014; C. B. Basak et al., 2009; Hofman et al., 1996; Irukuvarghula et al., 2016; Pahl et al., 1993; Rai et al., 2015; Sohn et al., 2000; Takahashi et al., 1989). A typical U-Zr fuel pin irradiation temperature can range between 550-850°C (Carmack et al., 2009; Kramer et al., 1993; Rest, 1993). This range of temperatures can result in internal temperature gradients (from centerline to cladding interface) ranging from 50 to 150°C. Depending on the linear heat generation rates during irradiation, the fuel may include the α and δ phases in the cooler peripheral regions, the β and γ’’ phases in the intermediate temperature region, and the γ’ to γ phases in the hot center (Sheldon and Peterson, 1989). The wide range of operating temperatures and subsequent phase variation occurring in the fuel system can make it difficult to isolate thermal effects from intrinsically coupled values such as reactor power, fuel composition, fission product accumulation, and irradiation damage, resulting in the high impact and lower knowledge ratios.

Under normal operating conditions the aforementioned temperatures and temperature gradients are known to result in a multiphase system. This multiphase system, as well as thermal and compositional gradients cause constituent redistribution, or de-mixing, of primary constituents (Galloway et al., 2015; Hofman et al., 1996; Ishida et al., 1993; Kim et al., 2004; Mohanty et al., 2011; Ogawa et al., 1991). This redistribution and compositional change, where Zr atoms migrate up the temperature gradient, thereby increasing the Zr content in the high temperature central fuel zone, will also affect fission gas bubble morphologies and resulting swelling observed in irradiated fuels (Thomas et al., 2019).

This implies that any study of the temperature gradients in the irradiated U-Zr microstructure also incorporates changes to composition and thermal conductivity. This amalgamation of source variables and various phenomenon makes isolating temperature gradient effects difficult in irradiated material. However, out-of-pile tests and simulations were able to show the redistribution of elements using isolated thermal gradients in U-10Zr and U-19Pu-10Zr alloys (Mohanty et al., 2011; Okuniewski et al., 2012; Okuniewski and Sohn, 2011; Sohn et al., 2000).

In order to capture the effects of temperature and temperature gradients on constituent redistribution, it is recommended that low burnup experiments with carefully selected reactor conditions are investigated in order to capture the onset of constituent redistribution, prior to the complete formation of the expected concentric fuel zones. This would allow for the separation of temperature and temperature gradient effects before the effects of other source variables become dominant, which would vary the previously isolated temperature ranges. Examples of other influential source variables that could convolute these results include: (1) porosity influencing thermal conductivity and subsequent temperature regimes, (2) compositional changes resulting in local variations in power and temperature, and (3) general loss of reactivity as burnup increases. Alternatively, it would be beneficial maintain an entire sample (such that zirconium produced through fission is captured), representative of the various the stages of constituent redistribution, at constant temperature in order to remove the temperature gradient and study how the individual regions evolve under irradiation.

## 4.3.A. Compositional Sources’ Impact on Swelling

Composition and impurities were ranked as low weighted source variables for fuel swelling. While U-Zr alloy-based fuels are being considered for multiple fuel applications, U-10wt.%Zr has been the most widely accepted and tested composition in the community. The 10 wt.% addition of zirconium is common in binary fuel systems, ternary fuel systems, and as blanket fuel (Crawford et al., 2018, 2007; Walters et al., 1984). However, during operation the composition can change, due to temperature driven constituent redistribution, from the starting composition of U-10wt.%Zr to redistributed regions of nearly depleted Zr to approximately 40wt.% Zr in different radial locations (Harp et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2004). Studies show that the weight percent of Zr influences the microstructure of the U-Zr system, as well as physical properties (C. Basak et al., 2009b; Irukuvarghula et al., 2016; Moore et al., 2013; Rai et al., 2015), making predictive modeling more difficult (Chen and Bai, 2018). As little as 2wt.% variation in Zr is shown to result in a noticeably different microstructure. An example of this is the measurable reduction of inter-lamellar spacing with increasing Zr concentration (C. Basak et al., 2009c; Irukuvarghula et al., 2016).

Fuel designers routinely desire reduced Zr composition in order to increase fissile density, and hence increase both burnup and/or power density. As such, any redistribution in zirconium

would result in a comparable change in power density and burnup. This inhomogeneity in power and burnup will also result in inhomogeneous production of fission products and defects, two of the primary causes of fuel swelling. It can then be inferred, and has been shown, that there will be inhomogeneous swelling present in the fuel system as a function of composition (Thomas et al., 2019, 2018). Albeit there is a plethora of varying compositions that have been historically examined for fuel swelling, opportunities still exist to further understand the phenomenon and compositional impact by isolating composition from other critical variables such as fission rate, temperature, and burnup.

## 4.3.B. Compositional Sources’ Impact on Constituent Redistribution

Composition was identified as the third most highly ranked weighted source variable with regards to constituent redistribution. Hofman et al. described the thermomigration that leads to constituent redistribution as a phase transformation driven phenomenon that takes place in the presence of the β and γ’’ phases (Hofman et al., 1996). Today, this explanation, coupled with the Soret effect (or atom specific thermodiffusion), is used to model and explain the constituent redistribution in U-Zr and U-Pu-Zr systems (Galloway et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2006, 2004; Ogata et al., 2012; Ogawa and Iwai, 1991). As the current explanation requires only the presence of a thermal gradient encompassing the β and γ’’ phases, it can be inferred from a phase diagram (Sheldon and Peterson, 1989) that the redistribution would occur in any U-Xwt.%Zr alloys, where X ranges from approximately 4 to 36.5 weight percent (10 to 60 atomic percent), as the required phases are still present.

Since the 1960’s there has been a multitude if irradiations on the metallic Zr-based fuel systems that have varied compositions (Harp et al., 2018a; Hofman et al., 1997; Murphy et al., 1969; Porter and Tsai, 2012). Fuels that remain in the 4 to 36.5 weight percent bounds all undergo constituent redistribution. However, no detailed studies were found where the kinetics of the redistribution were investigated with regard to composition. The authors hypothesize that the redistribution rate may depend on the composition because the β and γ’’ phase fractions are compositionally dependent.

Additionally, “composition” may encompass non-primary constituents, including impurities and dopants. These may affect other material properties such as thermal conductivity, which could influence the redistribution by increasing the centerline temperature. However, there have been studies on doped materials that found no notable changes in fuel constituent redistribution with the addition of 2 wt.% palladium (Harp et al., 2018b).

## 4.4.A. Microstructural Sources’ Impact on Fuel Swelling

Crystallographic texture, pre-irradiation grain size, and phase aspect ratio were the highest identified weighted source variables within the microstructural family of source variables with regards to swelling. The microstructure (grain size, interlamellar spacing, aspect ratio, preferred crystal orientation, porosity, etc.) of U-Zr alloys varies with the method of fabrication. Historically, U-Zr alloys were fabricated using injection casting, drop casting, extrusion, and drawing (Carmack et al., 2009). However, the injection casting methods used to produce thousands of EBR-II fuel pins are still the current standard for fabrication. The fuel fabrication methodology and thermal treatments determine the material’s pre-irradiated microstructure and potentially, its in-pile performance. As such, experiments on fuels have led to irradiation of samples with lamellar microstructures, as well as fuels with near, or apparent, single solution microstructures (McKeown et al., 2013; Ogata et al., 1996; Okuniewski et al., 2014). U-Zr fuel consistently shows anisotropic swelling, thought to be intrinsic to the asymmetric crystal structures that exist at temperatures below the cubic γ phase, specifically the α-U phase (orthorhombic) and the δ U-Zr2 (modified C32 or P6/mmm) phases (Mariani et al., 2012).

Out-of-pile annealing tests on unirradiated U-10wt.%Zr show a combination of loss of texture in annealed α-U and texture memory in annealed δ U-Zr2. This texture retention may result in further anisotropic swelling behavior under irradiation (Crawford et al., 2007; Irukuvarghula et al., 2017). It is also unclear if the kinetics of texture recovery will be accelerated or shifted under irradiation. While out-of-pile data suggests a texture retention in δ U-Zr2, it is unclear if there is a texture dependency on swelling in-pile. Historic experiments conducted with uranium-fissium (a U-Pu system with the addition of Ru, Mo, Pd, Te, Rh, Nb and Zr (Kruger, 1966)) fuels show a complete loss of the initial grain structure following irradiation (Hofman, 1980). However, lacking modern nuclearized instrumentation and techniques, it is possible that the experiments did not control or adequately quantify the microstructure well enough to draw definitive conclusions on texture dependencies in reactor.

Aside from texture, swelling in U-Zr in the early stages of irradiation, is dominated by fission gas bubble nucleation and growth. This occurs until swelling reaches \~33%, when the fission gasses can transport freely to the plenum (Hofman et al., 1990; Pahl et al., 1992). One model for this is that the damage-induced vacancies and gas atoms migrate to sinks such as grain boundaries, coalesce, and nucleate into fission gas bubbles. This nucleation induces swelling until porosity becomes interconnected (Rest, 1993). As such, defect transport to sinks is suspected to affect fuel performance. Thus, microstructural features that can act as sinks, such as grain boundaries and interlamellar spacing, would influence swelling.

The microstructural evolution of grains and intergranular lamella are controlled by cooling rates and steady-state temperatures (C. Basak et al., 2009a; Rai et al., 2015), yielding a varying sink density in-reactor. This makes coupling the swelling behavior to lamellar spacing difficult since the microstructure can change immediately upon cooling. Studies in UO2 and fissium fuels suggested a direct correlation between fission gas release and grain size (Fenske et al., 1987; Kittel et al., 1993; Notley and Hastings, 1980; Turnbull, 1974). However, this dependency is thought to be less influential in U-Zr systems due to the smaller grain-boundary energy to surface energy ratio, lowering the driving force for defect annihilation on grain boundaries with respect to ceramic (Ogawa et al., 1998). Aside from cavitation, grain boundaries influence sink density for fission gasses as well, and may still prove to influence fuel swelling. Thus, for any predictive modeling capability it will be critical to isolate and understand both grain size and lamellar spacing in regard and fuel swelling and diffusion rates (as diffusion will influence the nucleation of cavities and pores).

## 4.4.B. Microstructural Sources’ Impact on Constituent Redistribution

Crystallographic texture, pre-irradiation grain size, and phase aspect ratio where also the highest weighted microstructural source variables for constituent redistribution. The application of irreversible thermodynamics that is used to explain and model constituent redistribution utilizes multiple variables including lattice site density and diffusivity (Carlson et al., 2013; Galloway et al., 2015; Hales et al., 2016). Lattice site density and diffusivity are directly affected by the local microstructure. As phase transformations occur in the material, the different lattice structures will have varying numbers of nearest neighbors. This, in turn, varies the local lattice site density and thus influences the redistribution rates. The influence of the microstructure is more apparent with respect to grain size and interlamellar spacing, as boundaries within any material greatly influence the diffusivity, one of the most prominent values in the calculations for redistribution. For example, it has also been shown that the diffusion coefficients in U-Mo are directly proportional to interlamellar spacing for both fresh and irradiated fuel (Bleiberg, 1959), however no such test has been carried out for U-Zr. No comparisons between fuel constituent redistribution and texture or phase aspect ratios were found.

## 4.5.A. Neutronic Sources’ Impact on Fuel Swelling

Fission rate was identified as swelling’s highest weighted source variable under neutronics, with fission density and neutron spectrum being low. Fission density and fission rate comparisons have been historically seen as causal variables for fuel performance of these dynamic systems rather than seen as variables to control or isolate. Fission rate is experimentally observed to have an effect on fuel swelling, however this has not been quantified (Pahl et al., 1990). Qualitatively, this correlation appears accurate since defects and fission gasses are known to influence fuel swelling. As such, their generation rates would directly influence their respective nucleation rates, subsequently influencing fuel swelling (Rest, 1993). Quantitatively coupling fission rate to fuel swelling can be difficult as there is the convolution of fission rate, fission density, and temperature; all of which effect defect generation and annihilation.

This implies that fission rate should be isolated from other source variables to observe how defect and fission gas production rates may influence the overall microstructure and consequent influences to swelling.

## 4.5.B. Neutronic Sources’ Impact on Constituent Redistribution

Fission rate was identified as constituent redistribution’s highest weighted source variable under the neutronics family of source variables. Fission rate was postulated to influence the kinetics of fuel constituent redistribution in U-Pu-Zr alloys due to radiation enhanced diffusion. However, these experiments did not decouple the irradiation temperature from the fission rate (Porter et al., 1990). While radiation enhanced diffusion is known to exist, it is thought to be negligible compared to thermally enhanced diffusion in the typical U-Zr fuel operating temperature regime (Hofman et al., 1996).

As discussed previously, while under irradiation in fast reactor applications, U-Zr is subjected to a large radial temperature gradient that leads to the three distinct rings with different compositions and subsequent irradiation performance characteristics (Harp et al., 2018b, 2017; Hofman et al., 1996). These rings, varying in uranium concentration, have different local power densities, fission gas generation rates, compositions, and different phases. As such, fission density and fission rate studies would require the ability to decouple the thermal and compositional conditions from the irradiation conditions; which is not feasible when testing in prototypic engineering configurations. Fuel specimens meant to isolate these conditions would need to be in a geometry that is not self-insulating, such as a thin foil.

## 5. Conclusions

The PIRT analysis identified four main performance issues that limit, or have the potential to limit, the U-Zr fuel lifetime: fuel swelling, fuel constituent redistribution, fuel-cladding chemical interaction, and fuel-cladding mechanical interactions. A detailed PIRT analysis was performed on the U-Zr fuel system to prioritize future research directions specifically for the swelling and constituent redistribution phenomena and to identify the most influential source variables

impacting these phenomena. This analysis identified the areas in which prioritized investigations or intentionally focused research and development could aid in the understanding of these phenomenon. Given in order of weighted source variable ranking, from highest to lowest, these phenomena are: external mechanical constraint, temperature, crystallographic texture, temperature gradient, pre-irradiation grain size, fission rate, aspect ratio (phases), initial porosity, composition, residual stress, phase fractions, hydrostatic pressure, impurities, dislocation structure (defects), neutron spectrum, cover gas, and fabrication constraints.

Based upon the PIRT outcomes, re-evaluating historic tests and existing data may be adequate to advance the fundamental understanding of variables such as mechanical constraint, hydrostatic pressure, and neutron spectrum and their effect on fuel performance. However, it is unlikely that these historic tests can be sufficiently de-convoluted to further the mechanistic understanding in other areas such as temperature, power, and composition and their fundamental effects on the fuel system. While it may prove difficult to draw precise conclusions from these historic tests, they do represent a valuable resource that can aid in a more comprehensive understanding of source variable influence on fuel swelling and constituent redistribution. Specifically, there are modern specimen preparation and characterization techniques that can be applied to the historic irradiated fuels and used to validate assumptions or models.

The historical studies cited here have clearly shown a complex relationship between the irradiation conditions and other source variables, as well as the subsequent performance of metallic alloys. The research discussed was predominantly reactionary to the phenomena limiting the U-Zr system in the 1960’s and 1970’s, starting with increasing smear densities to mitigate FCMI and now currently focusing on controlling lanthanide transport to limit FCCI. During these times, relationships were identified, such as how the unirradiated microstructure or texture leads to different swelling behaviors and how temperature influences constituent redistribution. Yet, many of the correlations drawn from these historic studies have been incapable of producing predictive models on their own due to the interdependence of the parameters (e.g. temperature, power, burnup, and composition). Despite the fact that thousands of U-Zr based fuel pins were previously irradiated during decades of EBR-II operation, the nuclear fuels community is still lacking mechanistically-driven, predictive modeling that can aid in a better understanding of the fuel operating and bounding conditions required for the expansion of the metal fuel alloy utilization.

In order to address the knowledge gaps and impact ratios identified for fuel swelling and constituent redistribution, separate effects tests will be required to decouple the source variables which have been the most prominent convoluting factors in historic tests (e.g. temperature, fission rate, and fission density). Due to the interdependence of related source variables such as temperature, fission rate, and fission density found in traditional fuel systems, there is an inherent challenge in isolating the impact of these variables on the fuel’s microstructure and subsequent fuel performance. Separate effects studies that seek to isolate these variables during irradiation to understand fundamental microstructural evolution may require unique capsule designs and geometries (Okuniewski et al., 2014). However, separate effects irradiations which focus on isolating select variables should strive to remain representative of either an existing or proposed fuel system. For example, if fission rate is isolated successfully, it holds significantly less engineering relevance if temperature or fission density are not representative of known operating conditions. Other source variables that are shown to have an effect on fuel behavior (e.g. swelling and constituent redistribution) should also be studied include: crystallographic texture, grain size, impurities, phase fraction, and their subsequent evolution throughout irradiation, even if these are not isolated parameters.
